# VAK_4.111

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

सर्वत्र सर्वं ददतः कारुण्याद्दानपूरणम् ।
अङ्गच्छेदेऽप्यकोपात्तु रागिणः क्षान्तिशीलयोः ॥ ४.१११ ॥

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

**sarvatra sarvaṃ dadataḥ kāruṇyād dānapūraṇam |**  
**aṅgacchede ’py akopāt tu rāgiṇaḥ kṣāntiśīlayoḥ || 4.111 ||**

The verse begins an account of the Bodhisattva’s completion of the six perfections. VAK_4.111 treats dāna, śīla, and kṣānti; VAK_4.112 continues with vīrya, dhyāna, and prajñā.

## 3. Padaccheda

`sarvatra | sarvam | dadataḥ | kāruṇyāt | dāna-pūraṇam | aṅga-cchede | api | akopāt | tu | rāgiṇaḥ | kṣānti-śīlayoḥ`

Prose order:

`kāruṇyāt sarvatra sarvaṃ dadataḥ dānapūraṇam [abhūt]; rāgiṇaḥ aṅgacchede ’pi akopāt tu kṣāntiśīlayoḥ [pūraṇam abhūt].`

## 4. Grammar

- **sarvatra** — everywhere, in every circumstance.
- **sarvam** — everything.
- **dadataḥ** — of one giving.
- **kāruṇyāt** — from compassion.
- **dānapūraṇam** — completion of giving.
- **aṅgacchede** — when the limbs are cut.
- **akopāt** — because of absence of anger.
- **rāgiṇaḥ** — of one still possessing passion.
- **kṣāntiśīlayoḥ** — of patience and morality, genitive dual.

The dual shows that one decisive conduct completes two perfections: absence of anger constitutes patience, while absence of retaliatory transgression preserves morality.

## 5. Literal Translation

> For one giving everything everywhere from compassion, there is the completion of giving. And for one still possessing passion, through absence of anger even when his limbs are cut off, there is the completion of patience and morality.

## 6. Philosophical Translation

> The Bodhisattva perfected generosity by withholding nothing from any being and giving solely from compassion. He perfected patience and moral discipline when, although afflictive passion had not yet been completely abandoned, he remained free from anger and retaliation even while his body was being dismembered.

## 7. Technical Vocabulary

- **sarvatra sarvam** — everywhere and everything; universality of recipient and completeness of gift.
- **kāruṇya** — compassion as the determining ground of giving.
- **dānapūraṇa** — completion or fullness of generosity.
- **aṅgaccheda** — severing of limbs; extreme bodily injury.
- **akopa** — absence of anger as governing activity.
- **rāgin** — one who still possesses passion.
- **kṣānti** — patience, endurance, non-retaliatory reception of injury.
- **śīla** — moral discipline, stable non-transgression.

## 8. Doctrinal Determination

The verse establishes:

1. generosity is completed through universal, unreserved giving;
2. compassion is the motive converting giving into Bodhisattva perfection;
3. patience is completed through non-anger under extreme injury;
4. morality is simultaneously completed through refusal of retaliation;
5. these perfections can be practically complete while the Bodhisattva still retains afflictive passion.

The pāramitā therefore names perfection of activity, not yet necessarily complete elimination of every affliction.

## 9. Logical Determination

`Compassion(agent) ∧ gives(agent, everyRelevantObject, everyRelevantRecipient) → Completes(agent, GivingPerfection)`

`PossessesPassion(agent) ∧ suffers(agent, ExtremeBodilyHarm) ∧ doesNotBecomeAngry(agent) → Completes(agent, PatiencePerfection) ∧ Completes(agent, MoralDisciplinePerfection)`

Affliction may remain possible without becoming the actual principle of the deed.

## 10. Light Organon Interpretation

The first half gives the positive universality of Bodhisattva activity: all recipients, all possessions, one compassionate ground. The second gives its negative stability: even maximal opposition cannot reverse its governing purpose.

The cutting of limbs is literal division of bodily form, yet the agent’s ethical purpose remains undivided. This is a striking counterpoint to Saṃgha-division: there external speech divides unity, whereas here external division of the body fails to divide ethical unity.
