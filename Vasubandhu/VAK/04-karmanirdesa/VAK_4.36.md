# VAkK 4.36

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

> संवरः सर्वसत्त्वेभ्यो विभाषा त्वङ्गकारणैः ।  
> असंवरस्तु सर्वेभ्यः सर्वाङ्गेभ्यो न कारणैः ॥ ४.३६ ॥

### IAST

> **saṃvaraḥ sarvasattvebhyo vibhāṣā tv aṅgakāraṇaiḥ |**  
> **asaṃvaras tu sarvebhyaḥ sarvāṅgebhyo na kāraṇaiḥ || 4.36 ||**

This verse distinguishes the scope of **saṃvara** and **asaṃvara** along three axes:

- **sattva** — beings
- **aṅga** — limbs or kinds of action
- **kāraṇa** — motivating causes

The word **vibhāṣā** signals variation or alternative determination in the case of restraint.

## Grammar

| Form | Analysis | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **saṃvaraḥ** | nominative singular masculine | restraint |
| **sarvasattvebhyaḥ** | ablative plural | with respect to all beings |
| **vibhāṣā** | nominative singular feminine | variation, alternative determination |
| **tu** | contrastive particle | but |
| **aṅgakāraṇaiḥ** | instrumental plural compound | regarding limbs and causes |
| **asaṃvaraḥ** | nominative singular masculine | non-restraint |
| **sarvebhyaḥ** | ablative plural | with respect to all |
| **sarvāṅgebhyaḥ** | ablative plural | with respect to all limbs |
| **na kāraṇaiḥ** | instrumental plural with negation | not with respect to all causes |

The exact force of the final **na kāraṇaiḥ** is compressed. The natural doctrinal sense is that non-restraint need not be acquired through every possible motivating cause.

## Three dimensions of acquisition

The verse makes the operational record explicit. A restraint or non-restraint is not defined through only one predicate. Its scope is calculated across at least three dimensions:

```text
OBJECT-SCOPE
    toward which beings?

ACTION-SCOPE
    regarding which bodily or verbal limbs?

CAUSE-SCOPE
    through which motivating causes?
```

Thus:

```text
ethical form
    =
relation to beings
    ×
relation to actions
    ×
relation to causes
```

## saṃvaraḥ sarvasattvebhyaḥ

> **Restraint is acquired with respect to all beings.**

This is universal object-scope. A genuine restraint against killing cannot be restricted to favored beings while leaving others eligible as victims. The vow extends impartially across friends, enemies, strangers, humans, and nonhuman beings.

The person may never encounter most of them, but the acquired restraint does not depend upon personal acquaintance.

```text
saṃvara
    → universal with respect to sattvas
```

The restraint concerns a kind of relation, not an inventory of named individuals. When one undertakes non-killing, the determination is that no being is to become an eligible object of intentional killing.

The vow therefore abstracts from affection, hostility, species, social rank, proximity, and utility.

## vibhāṣā tv aṅgakāraṇaiḥ

> **But there is variation with respect to the limbs and causes.**

### vibhāṣā

```text
vi-√bhāṣ
    differentiated statement
    alternative explanation
    variation
```

The restraint is universal regarding beings, but variable regarding action-limbs and motivating causes.

```text
BEING-SCOPE
    all beings

ACTION-SCOPE
    one, several, or all limbs

CAUSE-SCOPE
    one, several, or all motivating causes
```

This carries forward VAkK 4.33: restraint is acquired according to the undertaking.

## aṅga — limbs of action

Here **aṅga** means the relevant components or categories of misconduct, such as killing, taking what is not given, sexual misconduct, and false speech.

A practitioner may undertake one limb, several limbs, or all limbs. Restraint is therefore not necessarily universal with respect to action-limbs.

```text
all beings
    but only regarding non-killing

all beings
    but regarding non-killing and non-stealing

all beings
    regarding the whole undertaken discipline
```

The universality lies within the chosen limb. Non-killing restraint applies to all beings but does not automatically become non-stealing restraint.

## kāraṇa — motivating causes

The causes are the kleśic motives from which harmful action may proceed, especially greed, hatred, and delusion.

The restraint may be acquired with differing causal scope. The Bhāṣya will be needed to determine the precise alternatives intended by **vibhāṣā**, but the structural point is secure: cause-scope is a separately recordable dimension.

The Kośa is not satisfied with recording merely that action did not occur. It asks under what motivating cause the action was restrained.

## asaṃvaras tu sarvebhyaḥ

> **Non-restraint, however, is with respect to all beings.**

Non-restraint is likewise universal in object-scope. A person who has acquired the persistent disposition of a butcher, hunter, executioner, or similar occupation does not possess it merely toward one already selected victim.

The form of non-restraint establishes living beings generally as eligible objects of the harmful occupation.

```text
asaṃvara
    → all beings
```

This is one reason non-restraint is more than an isolated harmful act. An isolated act has a particular object; a persistent non-restraint establishes a generalized practical orientation toward the class of beings.

## sarvāṅgebhyaḥ

> **Non-restraint is acquired with respect to all the limbs.**

This is stronger than the case of restraint. Restraint may be partial by action-limb, but non-restraint is described as acquired with respect to all relevant limbs.

A settled harmful life-form does not merely permit one isolated technical act. It accepts the whole practical complex required by the occupation: preparation, pursuit, capture, binding, striking, killing, concealment, sale, and supporting false speech.

```text
saṃvara
    may be selectively undertaken

asaṃvara
    forms an integrated harmful practice
```

## na kāraṇaiḥ

> **But not with respect to all causes.**

Non-restraint is universal across beings and limbs, but not necessarily across every motivating cause.

A butcher may kill principally from greed for profit rather than hatred toward the animal. An executioner may act from obedience, habit, livelihood, or deluded duty rather than every possible kleśa simultaneously.

Therefore:

```text
OBJECT-SCOPE
    all beings

ACTION-SCOPE
    all limbs

CAUSE-SCOPE
    not necessarily all causes
```

The non-restraint can be complete as a practical form without every kleśic cause being present.

## Literal translation

> **Restraint is acquired with respect to all beings, but there is variation according to the limbs and causes. Non-restraint, however, is acquired with respect to all beings and all limbs, though not with respect to all causes.**

## Philosophical translation

> **A genuine restraint universally excludes every being from the class of permitted victims, although the practitioner may undertake only certain forms of abstention and may acquire them under particular motivating conditions. Persistent non-restraint is likewise universal toward beings, but it embraces the complete practical complex of the harmful activity; it need not, however, arise from every possible kleśic cause.**

## Universal object, variable predicate

The verse describes a precise logical form.

For restraint:

```text
∀ beings x:
    restraint applies to x

but:
    only selected action-predicates
    may be restrained
```

Universality over objects does not entail universality over predicates.

```text
universal extension
    across beings

variable intension
    across action-limbs
```

## Non-restraint as a practical system

The claim that non-restraint includes all limbs shows why it cannot be reduced to intention at one instant. It is a whole practical organization involving occupation, tools, skills, preparation, execution, supporting speech, economic purpose, and repetition.

```text
asaṃvara
    = integrated harmful agency
```

This is why it persists as avijñapti.

## Relation to sarvadharma

The Kośa is treating a dharma almost as a typed relational record:

```text
DHARMA
    kind
    bearer
    acquisition-condition
    object-scope
    action-scope
    causal-scope
    temporal-scope
    persistence-condition
    abandonment-condition
```

Neither saṃvara nor asaṃvara is a vague quality. Each has extension, intension, causal origin, temporal behavior, and relations to other dharmas.

The text is documenting how the dharma operates. This is why Abhidharma discrimination feels computational without being merely formal: it is a record of causal and experiential actuality.

## Relation to kleśa

The distinction between action-limb and cause is crucial.

```text
aṅga
    what form the action takes

kāraṇa
    what kleśic motivation produces it
```

The same external limb may arise through different causes: killing from hatred, greed, or deluded ritual belief. Conversely, the same cause may generate different limbs: greed may produce stealing, lying, or killing.

Thus the Kośa records action and kleśa on distinct but linked axes.

```text
Karma chapter
    records determinate activity

Kleśa chapter
    records the causal afflictive structures
    capable of generating that activity
```

## Ordinary restraint as selective discrimination

The ordinary vow makes a determinate cut:

```text
with respect to all beings,
this action-limb will not be enacted
```

That is already prajñā-like discrimination in practical form. It identifies object-field, prohibited relation, motivational danger, and standing response.

The saintly Path will penetrate the cause itself more radically, but ordinary restraint has already installed a universal determination within the agent.

## Logical determination

The verse presents a matrix:

| Form | Beings | Limbs | Causes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **saṃvara** | all | variable | variable |
| **asaṃvara** | all | all | variable |

The contrast is:

```text
restraint
    universal object
    selective content

non-restraint
    universal object
    integrated harmful content
```

Both remain variable at the causal level because no practical form requires every possible kleśa to be simultaneously operative.

## Light Organon interpretation

The verse distinguishes three moments of an agential determination:

```text
object
    toward whom does the relation hold?

form
    what activity is organized?

ground
    from what motivation does it arise?
```

These dimensions cannot be collapsed. Two agents may perform the same external action while possessing different causal structures; two agents may share the same motivation while organizing different actions; and one standing form may universally determine a field of objects even when no encounter occurs.

This is close to a genuine semantic-operational model:

```text
relation
    + scope
    + predicate
    + ground
```

## Determinate result

VAkK 4.36 establishes:

```text
saṃvara:
    extends to all beings
    but may vary by action-limb
    and motivating cause

asaṃvara:
    extends to all beings
    and all relevant action-limbs
    but need not involve every cause
```

Its central principle is:

```text
a karmic form is a multidimensional determination:

not merely whether action occurs,
but toward whom,
through which limbs,
and from which causal grounds
```
