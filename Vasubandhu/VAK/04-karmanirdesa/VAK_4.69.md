# VAK_4d — VAkK_4.69

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

तदनन्तरसम्भूतेरभिध्याद्यास्त्रिमूलजाः ।
कुशलाः सप्रयोगान्ता अलोभद्वेषमोहजाः ॥ ४.६९ ॥

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

tad-anantara-saṃbhūter abhidhyādyās trimūlajāḥ /
kuśalāḥ sa-prayogāntā alobha-dveṣa-moha-jāḥ // 4.69 //

Source text:

```text
tadanantarasaṃbhūterabhidhyādyāstrimūlajāḥ /
kuśalāḥ saprayogāntā alobhadveṣamohajāḥ //
```

This verse continues the analysis of the causal roots of the karmic process. It first addresses the mental courses beginning with `abhidhyā`, then gives the wholesome counterparts together with their preparatory and subsequent phases.

```text
abhidhyā and the other mental courses
```

The exact reference of `tat` should be checked against the Bhāṣya.

## 4. Grammar

### tad-anantara-saṃbhūteḥ

An ablatival causal expression:

```text
tat
    that

anantara
    immediately following

saṃbhūti
    arising / origination
```

Thus: “because of their arising immediately after that,” or “from arising directly subsequent to that.” The antecedent of `tat` is probably the preparatory undertaking discussed in 4.68, though the Bhāṣya must confirm this.

### abhidhyādyāḥ

Masculine nominative plural compound:

```text
abhidhyā
    covetousness

ādi
    and the others
```

The other two are `vyāpāda` and `mithyādṛṣṭi`.

### trimūlajāḥ

Masculine nominative plural: born from three roots. The three unwholesome roots are `lobha`, `dveṣa`, and `moha`.

### kuśalāḥ

Masculine nominative plural, agreeing with the understood wholesome karmapathas.

### sa-prayogāntāḥ

A compound requiring expansion:

```text
sa
    together with

prayoga
    preparation

anta
    end / posterior phase
```

A doctrinally suitable reading is “together with preparation and aftermath.” Here `anta` likely corresponds to the posterior or concluding phase, close to the `pṛṣṭha` of 4.68.

### alobha-dveṣa-moha-jāḥ

The surface compound must be understood as containing privatives across all three wholesome roots:

```text
a-lobha
a-dveṣa
a-moha
```

Thus: non-greed, non-hatred, and non-delusion.

## 5. Literal Translation

Because they arise immediately after that, covetousness and the others are born from the three roots. The wholesome courses, together with their preparations and concluding phases, arise from non-greed, non-hatred, and non-delusion.

A more cautious rendering:

The mental courses beginning with covetousness can arise from all three unwholesome roots, inasmuch as they emerge immediately after the preceding preparation. The wholesome courses, including their preparatory and subsequent phases, arise from the three wholesome roots: non-greed, non-hatred, and non-delusion.

## 6. Philosophical Translation

Covetousness, ill will, and false view do not emerge as isolated mental events. They arise out of an immediately preceding motivational process and may be generated from greed, hatred, or delusion. Wholesome courses, together with their preparation and completion, are correspondingly grounded in non-greed, non-hatred, and non-delusion.

The verse distinguishes two levels:

```text
specific mental course
    abhidhyā
    vyāpāda
    mithyādṛṣṭi

underlying root
    lobha
    dveṣa
    moha
```

A course is not identical with its root.

```text
root
    generates

course
    specifies
```

## 7. Technical Vocabulary

### abhidhyā

Covetousness; appropriative fixation; desire directed toward another’s possession. It is a specific mental karmapatha, not greed in general.

### vyāpāda

Ill will; hostile intention; desire for harm. It is more determinate than `dveṣa`, the general root of hatred.

### mithyādṛṣṭi

False view; wrong principial determination. It is a specific karmic course involving denial or distortion of morally causal structure.

### mūla

Root; causal ground; generative basis. The root is not simply an accompanying emotion. It is the generative ethical ground from which the course proceeds.

### lobha / dveṣa / moha

Greed or appropriative attachment; hatred or aversive negation; delusion or obscured determination.

### alobha / adveṣa / amoha

Non-greed or non-appropriation; non-hatred or non-hostility; non-delusion or discriminative clarity.

`amoha` should not be treated as merely the absence of confusion. It is the positive wholesome ground of correct discernment.

### prayoga

Preparatory undertaking. The wholesome process has preparation just as the unwholesome process does.

### anta

End, concluding phase, or aftermath. In the present process analysis, it indicates the phase following or completing the principal course.

## 8. Doctrinal Determination

VAK_4.68 established preparation, principal act, and aftermath, and stated that unwholesome preparation may arise from all three unwholesome roots.

VAK_4.69 extends the root analysis.

### Mental karmapathas

```text
abhidhyā
vyāpāda
mithyādṛṣṭi
```

These are specific mental bad conducts. Their roots are `lobha`, `dveṣa`, and `moha`.

The verse provisionally indicates that the mental courses can emerge from the three-root field rather than being reducible immediately to one root each. This should be distinguished from the more precise doctrine of completion roots in 4.70–4.71.

That coming analysis will say, for example:

```text
abhidhyā completed by greed
vyāpāda completed by hatred
mithyādṛṣṭi completed by delusion
```

There is therefore no contradiction if the present verse says that the broader process may involve all three roots.

```text
genesis and preparation
    may involve several roots

principal completion
    may have a characteristic root
```

### Wholesome courses

The wholesome side is grounded in `alobha`, `adveṣa`, and `amoha`. These roots govern the whole process: preparation, principal wholesome course, and aftermath.

Thus wholesome action is not merely the external absence of wrongdoing. It requires a wholesome generative ground.

## 9. Logical Determination

The verse separates root, process, and determinate course.

```text
root
    ↓
preparation
    ↓
specific course
    ↓
aftermath
```

These are distinct logical moments.

The root is the generative ethical ground. Preparation is the mobilization of that ground toward determinate action. The course is the specified form taken by the process. The aftermath is the continuation or stabilization of the completed determination.

This prevents a simplistic identity:

```text
greed = covetousness
hatred = ill will
delusion = false view
```

The more precise structure is:

```text
greed
    general root

covetousness
    specific course
```

Likewise hatred is the general root while ill will is its directed form; delusion is the general root while false view is its determinate principial expression.

The wholesome roots are not merely external prohibitions. They are positive generative grounds: non-greed, non-hatred, and non-delusion. Moral reversal therefore occurs at the level of the ground, not only at the level of observable conduct.

## 10. Light Organon Interpretation

The verse sharpens the moral-processing architecture:

```text
Ground
    motivational root

Process
    preparation and transition

Path
    specific karmic course

Resulting state
    aftermath and persistence
```

A moral processor should not infer the complete ethical ground merely from the final action label.

```text
same outward restraint
    may arise from
fear
calculation
hatred
clarity
benevolence
```

Only some of these constitute wholesome grounding.

This yields a distinction between behavioral conformity and ethical generation. An agent may conform externally while remaining internally grounded in greed, hatred, or delusion. The stronger standard is wholesome output generated by wholesome root.

For an Organon architecture:

```text
MoralRoot
    generates MoralProcess

MoralProcess
    realizes KarmicCourse

KarmicCourse
    establishes PersistentDisposition
```

The three wholesome roots can be interpreted as three general safeguards:

```text
alobha
    prevents object-appropriation

adveṣa
    prevents hostile negation

amoha
    prevents false determination
```

Together they provide a minimal basis for moral agency: non-appropriation, non-hostility, and clear discrimination.
