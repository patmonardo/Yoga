# VAK_4.118

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

```sanskrit
मातृपितृग्लानधार्मकथिकेभ्योऽन्त्यजन्मने ।
बोधिसत्त्वाय चामेया अनार्येभ्योऽपि दक्षिणा ॥ ४.११८ ॥
```

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

```text
mātṛpitṛglānadhārmakathikebhyo ’ntyajanmane |
bodhisattvāya cāmeyā anāryebhyo ’pi dakṣiṇā || 4.118 ||
```

The syntactically natural grouping is:

```text
mātṛ-pitṛ-glāna-dhārma-kathikebhyaḥ
antya-janmane bodhisattvāya ca
```

Thus the recipients are:

1. mother;
2. father;
3. a sick person;
4. a speaker of Dharma;
5. a Bodhisattva in his final birth.

The last is one expression:

```text
antyajanmane bodhisattvāya
    to the Bodhisattva in the final existence
```

rather than two separate recipients.

---

## 3. Padaccheda

```text
mātṛ |
pitṛ |
glāna |
dhārma-kathikebhyaḥ |
antya-janmane |
bodhisattvāya |
ca |
ameyā |
anāryebhyaḥ |
api |
dakṣiṇā
```

Prose order:

```text
mātre, pitre, glānāya, dhārmakathikāya,
antyajanmane bodhisattvāya ca
dakṣiṇā ameyā,
anāryebhyo ’pi.
```

> A gift to one’s mother, one’s father, a sick person, a preacher of Dharma, or the Bodhisattva in his final birth is immeasurable—even when the recipients are not noble ones.

The plural `anāryebhyaḥ` qualifies at least the first group generally: immeasurability does not depend solely upon noble Path attainment.

---

## 4. Grammar

| Term | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `mātṛ` | compound member | mother |
| `pitṛ` | compound member | father |
| `glāna` | compound member | sick or afflicted person |
| `dhārma-kathika` | compound member | speaker or expositor of Dharma |
| `-ebhyaḥ` | dative plural ending | to |
| `antya-janmane` | dative singular | to one in the final birth |
| `bodhisattvāya` | dative singular | to the Bodhisattva |
| `ameyā` | nominative feminine singular | immeasurable |
| `anāryebhyaḥ` | dative plural | to non-noble persons |
| `api` | particle | even |
| `dakṣiṇā` | nominative feminine singular | offering, religious gift |

The agreement is:

```text
dakṣiṇā ameyā
    the offering is immeasurable
```

`Ameyā` means immeasurable in karmic fruit or meritorious magnitude, not physically immeasurable.

---

## 5. Literal translation

> An offering to mother, father, a sick person, a speaker of Dharma, and the Bodhisattva in his final birth is immeasurable, even though [some of these recipients] are non-noble persons.

---

## 6. Philosophical translation

> The karmic magnitude of giving cannot be reduced to the recipient’s formal spiritual rank. Offerings made to one’s parents, to one who is suffering from illness, to one who sustains the transmission of Dharma, or to the Bodhisattva in the final existence bear immeasurable fruit. Even where noble attainment is absent, the recipient may constitute an immeasurable field through prior beneficence, urgent suffering, preservation of Dharma, or assured universal significance.

This verse powerfully confirms the plural theory of `kṣetra` introduced in VAK_4.117.

```text
field-excellence
    is not identical with
noble rank
```

---

# 7. `Dakṣiṇā`

```text
dakṣiṇā
    offering
    honorarium
    religious gift
    gift made to a worthy field
```

`Dakṣiṇā` is more specific than a neutral transfer of property.

It signifies a gift placed into a meaningful ethical or religious relation.

The term emphasizes the gift as directed toward a field recognized as deserving support, gratitude, relief, or honor.

```text
material object
    +
recognition of field
    +
relinquishing activity
    =
dakṣiṇā
```

---

# 8. `Ameyā`

```text
a-meya
    not measurable
    beyond calculation
```

The offering is called immeasurable because its karmic significance cannot be adequately represented by the object’s market value.

A modest gift may be immeasurable when the relation is exceptional.

```text
finite object
    enters
immeasurably significant relation
```

Thus:

```text
quantity of gift
    does not set
the final measure of merit
```

The field can transform the causal magnitude of the act.

---

# 9. Mother and father

```text
mātṛ
    mother

pitṛ
    father
```

The parents are distinguished through:

```text
upakāritva
    prior beneficence
```

They are the paradigmatic historical fields.

The present gift responds to a relation containing:

```text
birth
nurture
protection
sustenance
education
and prolonged care
```

The offering is therefore not an isolated transaction.

It is a practical recognition of a causal history.

```text
benefit formerly received
    →
gratitude made actual as giving
```

---

# 10. The same relation intensifies good and evil

Earlier, injury to one’s parents was exceptionally grave.

Here, giving to them is exceptionally fruitful.

The reason is structurally identical:

```text
the object is not merely
this presently existing person

the object is
this person as prior benefactor
```

Thus historical relation intensifies both poles:

```text
harm to benefactor
    → intensified demerit

benefit to benefactor
    → intensified merit
```

Karma preserves relational history in both directions.

---

# 11. `Glāna`

```text
glāna
    sick
    exhausted
    physically afflicted
```

The sick person is distinguished not through previous service or spiritual attainment but through present suffering.

This is the `duḥkha-kṣetra`.

```text
illness
    creates urgent need

urgent need
    increases the effective benefit
of assistance
```

Medicine, food, care, shelter, or attendance may have exceptional significance when the recipient’s condition is fragile.

The greatness of the field lies in the magnitude of negation that the gift can remove.

---

# 12. The field of suffering

The sick person demonstrates that recipient excellence need not mean superiority.

There are at least two opposed forms of exceptional field:

```text
guṇa-kṣetra
    high through attained excellence

duḥkha-kṣetra
    urgent through suffered deprivation
```

One gives upward in reverence.

One gives toward suffering in compassion.

Both can become immeasurable.

This prevents the doctrine of merit from collapsing into a hierarchy that values only spiritually advanced recipients.

---

# 13. `Dhārma-kathika`

```text
dharma
    determination
    teaching
    liberating Law

kathika
    speaker
    expositor
    one who narrates or explains
```

A `dhārmakathika` is one who communicates and explains Dharma.

The recipient’s significance lies not necessarily in personal noble attainment but in function:

```text
receives Dharma
    →
understands or preserves it
    →
communicates it to others
```

Supporting the Dharma speaker supports the continued appearance of Dharma in the world.

```text
gift to speaker
    →
support of teaching activity
    →
benefit extending to many hearers
```

The gift’s field therefore exceeds the immediate individual.

---

# 14. Functional field

The Dharma speaker introduces a new kind of excellence:

```text
not only
what the recipient is

but
what the recipient enables
```

The field is distinguished by a causal function.

A gift of food, lodging, medicine, or materials permits the speaker to continue teaching.

The act therefore propagates through the recipient:

```text
giver
    supports teacher

teacher
    communicates Dharma

many beings
    receive benefit
```

This is an extended karmic field.

---

# 15. `Antyajanman bodhisattva`

```text
antya
    final

janman
    birth
    existence

antyajanman
    one in the final birth
```

The phrase denotes the Bodhisattva in the existence immediately preceding and culminating in Buddhahood—the final Bodhisattva life.

This continuum is on the threshold of becoming a Buddha.

```text
present Bodhisattva
    +
certain immediate Buddhahood
    =
exceptional future-oriented field
```

The field is distinguished by assured becoming.

This continues the logic of `niyatistha` from VAK_4.106.

---

# 16. The future belongs to the present field

Why is the final-birth Bodhisattva an immeasurable field before Buddhahood is actual?

Because the recipient’s determinate future is already causally present in the continuum.

```text
what the recipient is becoming
    qualifies
what the recipient now is
```

The field includes:

```text
present qualities
past accumulation
assured future function
```

A gift to this Bodhisattva supports the imminent appearance of a Buddha and therefore the welfare of immeasurable beings.

---

# 17. `Anāryebhyo ’pi`

```text
an-ārya
    not noble
    one who has not attained the noble Path

api
    even
```

This is the verse’s doctrinal shock.

The gift can be immeasurable even when the recipient is not an `ārya`.

Therefore:

```text
immeasurable field
    ≠
necessarily noble field
```

The categories of value are not reducible to one scale of spiritual attainment.

A recipient may be immeasurable through:

```text
historical beneficence
present suffering
Dharma function
or assured Buddhahood
```

---

# 18. “Non-noble” is technical, not morally dismissive

`Anārya` here should not be translated as “ignoble” in the ordinary moral sense.

It means:

> one who has not yet directly entered the supramundane noble Path.

A mother may possess no noble attainment and still be an immeasurable benefactor-field.

A sick person may possess no special contemplative qualities and still be an immeasurable suffering-field.

A Dharma speaker may transmit genuine teaching without yet being an ārya.

The classification is technical, not a judgment that these persons lack worth.

---

# 19. Four grounds made concrete

VAK_4.117 gave the abstract grounds:

```text
gati
duḥkha
upakāritva
guṇa
```

VAK_4.118 supplies concrete fields:

| Recipient | Primary field-determination |
|---|---|
| mother | prior beneficence |
| father | prior beneficence |
| sick person | suffering |
| Dharma speaker | Dharma-supporting function and qualities |
| final-birth Bodhisattva | assured Buddhahood and supreme future benefit |

The relation is not perfectly one-to-one, because each recipient may contain several determinations.

But the movement is clear:

```text
abstract field criteria
    →
concrete exemplary recipients
```

# 20. Examination of the instrument

This is precisely one of the astonishing uses of the Kośa’s examination of the instrument.

The instrument of giving is not just the body or speech that transfers an object.

The full instrument is:

```text
giver
gift-object
recipient-field
historical relation
present need
future causal reach
intention
execution
```

VAK_4.118 shows that the recipient-field itself contains temporal depth:

```text
parents
    past benefit

sick person
    present suffering

Dharma speaker
    extended present function

final-birth Bodhisattva
    assured future benefit
```

The instrument spans past, present, and future.

---

# 21. A temporal triad of the field

The verse can be read through three temporal determinations.

## Past

```text
mother and father
    what they have already done for the giver
```

## Present

```text
sick person
    suffering that presently calls for relief

Dharma speaker
    teaching activity presently sustained
```

## Future

```text
final-birth Bodhisattva
    Buddhahood about to become actual
```

Thus the recipient is not a point-object.

The recipient is a temporally structured field.

---

# 22. From individual to distributed benefit

The five recipients also reveal an expanding scope.

```text
parents
    benefit concentrated in a personal history

sick person
    relief of an urgent individual suffering

Dharma speaker
    benefit distributed through teaching

final-birth Bodhisattva
    benefit destined to extend universally
```

The karmic field increases not merely through the recipient’s rank, but through the breadth of causal benefit carried by the recipient.

---

# 23. Doctrinal determination

The verse establishes:

1. offerings to parents bear immeasurable fruit because of prior beneficence;
2. an offering to a sick person is immeasurable through urgent suffering;
3. an offering to a Dharma speaker supports the transmission of Dharma;
4. an offering to the final-birth Bodhisattva supports assured and imminent Buddhahood;
5. these gifts may be immeasurable even when the recipient is technically non-noble;
6. field excellence is therefore relational, temporal, and functional;
7. noble attainment is one ground of excellence, not the sole ground.

---

# 24. Logical determination

A rule schema is:

```text
PriorBenefactor(?r, ?g)
∨ SeverelySuffering(?r)
∨ TransmitsDharma(?r)
∨ FinalBirthBodhisattva(?r)
→ ExceptionalRecipientField(?r)
```

And:

```text
Gives(?g, ?gift, ?r)
∧ ExceptionalRecipientField(?r)
→ ImmeasurablyFruitfulOffering(?event)
```

The important non-entailment is:

```text
ExceptionalRecipientField(?r)
↛ NoblePathAttainment(?r)
```

Recipient-field significance cannot be inferred solely from ārya status.

---

# 25. Organon interpretation

The verse exposes the recipient as a **causal interface** rather than an isolated substance.

A parent carries the giver’s past.

A sick person concentrates a present contradiction requiring resolution.

A Dharma speaker distributes the gift into a network of future hearers.

The final-birth Bodhisattva carries imminent universal transformation.

```text
recipient
    =
present node
of past, present, and future relations
```

The karmic magnitude of the deed arises from the total causal circuit activated through that node.

This is examination of the instrument at a very high level:

> The instrument is not merely what the agent uses; it is the complete relational architecture through which activity acquires meaning and causal reach.
