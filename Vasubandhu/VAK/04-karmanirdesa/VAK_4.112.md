# VAK_4.112

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

तिष्यस्तोत्रेण वीर्यस्य धीसमाध्योरनन्तरम् ।
पुण्यं क्रियाथ तद्वस्तु त्रयं कर्मपथा यथा ॥ ४.११२ ॥

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

*tiṣyastotreṇa vīryasya dhīsamādhyor anantaram |
puṇyaṃ kriyātha tadvastu trayaṃ karmapathā yathā || 4.112 ||*

This verse has two movements:

1. it completes the account of the six perfections begun in VAK_4.111;
2. it begins a new analysis of the three bases of meritorious activity.

## 3. Padaccheda

*tiṣya-stotreṇa | vīryasya | dhī-samādhyoḥ | anantaram | puṇyam | kriyā | atha | tad-vastu | trayam | karma-pathāḥ | yathā*

A cautious prose arrangement is:

*tiṣyastotreṇa vīryasya [pūraṇam]; dhīsamādhyor anantaram [pūraṇam]. atha puṇyaṃ kriyā; tad-vastu trayaṃ, karmapathā yathā.*

> Through the praise of Tiṣya, vigor was completed; immediately afterward, wisdom and concentration were completed. Then: merit is activity, and its bases are three—analogously to the paths of karma.

The exact narrative referent of *anantaram* should be confirmed in the Bhāṣya pass.

## 4. Grammar

- **tiṣya-stotreṇa** — instrumental singular: through the hymn or praise concerning Tiṣya.
- **vīryasya** — genitive singular: of vigor or energetic effort.
- **dhī-samādhyoḥ** — genitive dual: of wisdom and concentration.
- **anantaram** — immediately afterward, without interval.
- **puṇyam** — merit, wholesome causal potency.
- **kriyā** — activity, performance.
- **atha** — then, now.
- **tad-vastu** — its basis or object.
- **trayam** — threefold.
- **karma-pathāḥ** — paths of karma.
- **yathā** — just as, analogously.

The verse joins *vīrya* with *dhī* and *samādhi*, then distinguishes *kriyā* from *vastu*: activity itself from the determinate basis through which it becomes actual.

## 5. Literal translation

> Through the praise of Tiṣya came the completion of vigor; immediately afterward, that of wisdom and concentration. Now merit is activity, and its basis is threefold, just as with the paths of karma.

## 6. Philosophical translation

> The Bodhisattva brought energetic effort to perfection through the extraordinary act of praising Tiṣya; wisdom and meditative concentration were completed directly thereafter. With the six perfections thus accounted for, the analysis turns to merit: merit is not an inert possession but wholesome activity, and such activity is organized through three fundamental bases, analogously to the way karma is articulated through its paths.

## 7. Completion of the six perfections

VAK_4.111 supplied giving, morality, and patience. VAK_4.112 completes the sequence with vigor, concentration, and discriminative wisdom. The verse uses *dhī* rather than the more usual *prajñā*; here it indicates discriminative intelligence or understanding.

## 8. Tiṣya-stotra and vigor

The expression refers to a famous Bodhisattva episode in which Śākyamuni, during his former career, praised Buddha Tiṣya with extraordinary, uninterrupted intensity. The story’s exact details belong to the Bhāṣya, but the kārikā’s doctrinal point is clear: Tiṣya-praise marks the completion of *vīrya*.

Vigor is not mere physical strength. It is energetic constancy, non-retreat from wholesome activity, and sustained effort toward the end. The praise is undistracted, continuous, intentionally unified, and directed toward a supreme Buddha-field.

## 9. Dhī and samādhi

*Samādhi* stabilizes the field of knowing; *dhī* discriminates that field correctly. Concentration without discrimination may remain merely a stable worldly state, while discrimination without concentration may lack the stability required to transform the continuum. Together they form effective contemplative knowledge.

## 10. Anantaram

*Anantaram* means without an interval or immediately afterward. The likely sense is that completion of wisdom and concentration followed directly upon the completion of vigor associated with the Tiṣya praise. The Bhāṣya should determine whether this refers to a specific subsequent episode or to immediate causal succession.

## 11. From pāramitā to puṇya

The particle *atha* marks a transition. The six perfections have been completed as the extraordinary Bodhisattva form of wholesome activity. The text now turns to a general doctrine of merit. This is not disconnected: the Buddha marks were produced from hundreds of merits, and the six perfections showed the qualitative character of that merit.

## 12. Puṇyaṃ kriyā

The most direct reading is: **Merit is activity.** Merit is not fundamentally a substance stored somewhere in the agent, nor an externally awarded credit. It is first the wholesome performance itself. Its causal efficacy may persist after the visible activity has ended, but its origin is practical.

## 13. Puṇyakriyāvastu

The verse is likely unpacking the technical expression *puṇya-kriyā-vastu*: merit, activity producing or constituting merit, and the basis or domain in relation to which the activity is performed. It describes a structured practical relation in which an agent performs wholesome activity with respect to a determinate basis.

## 14. The three bases

The three bases of meritorious activity are traditionally:

1. **dāna** — giving;
2. **śīla** — moral discipline;
3. **bhāvanā** — cultivation.

These organize wholesome activity according to relation to possessions and others, relation to one’s own conduct, and relation to the transformation of one’s continuum.

The six perfections and the three merit-bases operate at different levels of abstraction. The former give the detailed Bodhisattva articulation; the latter give a general classification of wholesome activity.

## 15. Karmapathā yathā

A *karmapatha* is a completed form through which karma operates. Similarly, a *puṇyakriyāvastu* is the practical basis through which merit-producing activity becomes determinate. Karma becomes determinate through its paths; meritorious activity becomes determinate through its basis. The exact grammatical force of the plural should be checked in the Bhāṣya.

## 16. Activity and basis

The verse distinguishes but relates:

- **kriyā** — what the agent does;
- **vastu** — the domain in which the doing acquires its determination.

Merit is therefore not activity in abstraction. It is activity situated in a meritorious basis.

## 17. Doctrinal determination

The verse establishes:

1. praise of Tiṣya marks the completion of vigor;
2. wisdom and concentration are completed immediately thereafter;
3. the six Bodhisattva perfections are thereby complete;
4. merit is fundamentally wholesome activity;
5. meritorious activity has three principal bases;
6. these bases organize merit analogously to the paths through which karma becomes determinate.

## 19. Organon interpretation

The first half completes the internal organization of the Bodhisattva agent: outward universality in giving, practical stability in morality and patience, self-sustaining movement in vigor, collected inward unity in samādhi, and discriminative knowing in dhī.

The second half returns these perfected capacities to the category of activity. Virtue is not an inert quality; it is a stable form of activity. The *vastu* is the determinate field in which that activity becomes actual. Universal capacity plus determinate field yields the actual meritorious deed.
