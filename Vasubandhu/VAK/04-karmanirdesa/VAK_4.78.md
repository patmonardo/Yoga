# VAK_4.78

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

व्यापादः सत्त्वविद्वेषो नास्तिदृष्टिः शुभाशुभे ।  
मिथ्यादृष्टिस्त्रयो ह्यत्र पन्थानः सप्त कर्म च ॥ ४.७८ ॥

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

**vyāpādaḥ sattva-vidveṣo nāsti-dṛṣṭiḥ śubhāśubhe |**  
**mithyā-dṛṣṭis trayo hy atra panthānaḥ sapta karma ca || 4.78 ||**

Local source:

```text
vyāpādaḥ sattvavidveṣaḥ nāstidṛṣṭiḥ śubhāśubhe /
mithyādṛṣṭiḥ trayo hyatra panthānaḥ sapta karma ca // VAkK_4.78 //
```

The verse completes the definitions of the three mental unwholesome karmapathas:

```text
abhidhyā
    covetousness

vyāpāda
    ill will

mithyādṛṣṭi
    false view
```

It then distinguishes the three mental courses from the seven bodily and verbal courses:

```text
three
    are karmapathas

seven
    are both karma and karmapathas
```

> Among these ten, the three are paths of karma; the seven are both karma and paths of karma.

The exact force of the final contrast belongs centrally to the Bhāṣya, but the basic doctrinal distinction is already clear.

## 4. Grammar

### vyāpādaḥ

Masculine nominative singular.

```text
vi-ā-√pad
```

In Buddhist technical usage:

```text
vyāpāda
    ill will
    malicious intention
    desire for harm or destruction
```

It is one of the three mental unwholesome karmapathas.

### sattva-vidveṣaḥ

Masculine nominative singular compound:

```text
sattva
    living being
    sentient being

vidveṣa
    hostility
    aversion
    hatred
```

The compound functions as the definition of `vyāpāda`.

### nāsti-dṛṣṭiḥ

Feminine nominative singular compound:

```text
na asti
    does not exist

dṛṣṭi
    view
    principial standpoint
```

Thus:

```text
nāsti-dṛṣṭi
    a view of nonexistence
    nihilistic denial
```

It is not denial of existence in general, but denial of the relevant practical distinction named in the next term.

### śubhāśubhe

Locative dual:

```text
śubhe
    in relation to the good, wholesome, favorable

aśubhe
    in relation to the bad, unwholesome, unfavorable
```

The locative may be rendered:

> concerning good and bad.

The compound is not merely aesthetic. It concerns the objective distinction between wholesome and unwholesome action and, by implication, their results.

### mithyā-dṛṣṭiḥ

Feminine nominative singular:

```text
mithyā
    falsely
    wrongly
    contrary to the proper determination

dṛṣṭi
    view
    standpoint
```

Canonical translation:

```text
false view
```

### trayaḥ

Masculine nominative plural:

```text
three
```

The referents are the three mental karmapathas:

```text
covetousness
ill will
false view
```

### hi

Explanatory particle:

```text
indeed
for
because
```

It marks the final statement as a doctrinal clarification.

### atra

Locative adverb:

```text
here
among these
in this classification
```

### panthānaḥ

Masculine nominative plural of `panthan`:

```text
paths
ways
courses
```

This is the semantic core of `karmapatha`:

```text
karma
    activity

patha / panthan
    path
    course
```

### sapta

Nominative plural:

```text
seven
```

The three bodily and four verbal karmapathas.

### karma ca

```text
karma
    activity

ca
    also
```

The seven are not merely paths through which karma develops; they are themselves manifest activity.

## 5. Literal Translation

> Ill will is hostility toward living beings. The view that good and bad do not exist is false view. Here the three are paths, while the seven are also karma.

A more explicit rendering:

> Ill will consists in hatred directed toward living beings. False view is the denial that wholesome and unwholesome determination exist. Among the ten courses, the three mental ones are paths of karma, whereas the seven bodily and verbal ones are both karma and paths of karma.

## 6. Philosophical Translation

> Ill will is the practical negation of another living being. False view is the principial denial of the distinction between wholesome and unwholesome activity. The three mental courses do not themselves constitute manifest bodily or verbal action; rather, they are the decisive pathways through which action proceeds. The seven bodily and verbal courses are both enacted activity and pathways that conduct the series toward further results.

The verse completes a threefold practical corruption:

```text
covetousness
    improper appropriation

ill will
    hostile negation

false view
    false principial determination
```

These three do not merely accompany bodily and verbal action. They constitute the practical orientations through which action becomes morally determinate.

## 7. Technical Vocabulary

### vyāpāda

```text
vyāpāda
    ill will
    malicious practical orientation
```

It is more determinate than momentary irritation.

Its structure is:

```text
living being exists

agent practically posits:
    may this being be harmed,
    destroyed,
    or cease to flourish
```

It is therefore an incipient act of negation.

### sattva

```text
sattva
    living being
```

The objective basis of ill will is not an abstract proposition but another living process.

### vidveṣa

```text
vidveṣa
    hostility
    hatred
    active aversion
```

This is not merely the absence of liking. It is opposition that practically takes the other’s harm as an end.

### nāsti-dṛṣṭi

```text
nāsti-dṛṣṭi
    denial that something exists
```

In this verse, what is denied is the objective distinction:

```text
wholesome
unwholesome
```

and likely their efficacy.

### śubha

```text
śubha
    good
    wholesome
    favorable
```

Within the Karma-nirdeśa, the term must be read practically:

```text
a determination capable of producing
a favorable karmic result
```

### aśubha

```text
aśubha
    bad
    unwholesome
    unfavorable
```

Again, not merely moral disapproval, but a practical causal classification.

### mithyādṛṣṭi

```text
mithyādṛṣṭi
    false view
```

This course is especially powerful because it attacks the principle governing the entire practical field.

Covetousness misrelates to property.

Ill will misrelates to living beings.

False view misrelates to the very distinction by which activity is judged.

### karmapatha

```text
karma
    activity

patha
    path
    course
```

A karmapatha is a determinate path through which activity proceeds, becomes established, and bears further consequence.

The verse distinguishes:

```text
mental path
```

from:

```text
manifest activity that is also a path
```

## 8. Doctrinal Determination

### The three mental karmapathas

The completed mental set is now:

```text
abhidhyā
    covetousness

vyāpāda
    ill will

mithyādṛṣṭi
    false view
```

They correspond to the three unwholesome roots:

```text
greed
    → covetousness

hatred
    → ill will

delusion
    → false view
```

Yet root and karmapatha are not identical.

The root is the general afflictive ground.

The mental karmapatha is the determinate practical form the root assumes.

```text
greed
    general root

covetousness
    greed determined toward another’s possession
```

```text
hatred
    general root

ill will
    hatred determined toward the harm of a being
```

```text
delusion
    general root

false view
    delusion determined as denial of practical truth
```

### False view as denial of practical order

The phrase `nāsti-dṛṣṭiḥ śubhāśubhe` indicates more than holding one incorrect doctrine.

It denies the distinction upon which the classification of action rests:

```text
no wholesome
no unwholesome
```

By implication:

```text
no objective moral efficacy
no meaningful causal distinction among actions
```

This is why false view is completed specifically through delusion.

It destroys the principial basis of practical judgment.

### Three paths, seven actions

The ten karmapathas divide into:

```text
three mental
seven bodily and verbal
```

The seven are manifest activities:

```text
three bodily
four verbal
```

They are therefore:

```text
karma
    actual activity

and

karmapatha
    a course conducting further consequence
```

The mental three are called `panthānaḥ`—paths—but not `karma` in the same manifest sense.

This likely reflects the earlier Sarvāstivāda definition:

```text
karma
    fundamentally intention
    and that which is produced by intention
```

Yet here the term `karma` is being used in a narrower contrast, especially for manifest bodily and verbal acts.

The Bhāṣya must establish the exact technical scope.

## 9. Logical Determination

The verse completes a hierarchy:

```text
Root
    ↓
Mental Karmapatha
    ↓
Bodily or Verbal Karmapatha
    ↓
Further Result
```

The three mental courses are called paths because they mediate the transition from general affliction into determinate activity.

### Covetousness

```text
greed
    becomes
projected appropriation
```

### Ill will

```text
hatred
    becomes
projected harm
```

### False view

```text
delusion
    becomes
denial of practical law
```

These are not merely mental contents. They are orientations of activity.

### Ill will as practical negation

The existing determination is:

```text
S is a living being
```

Ill will practically counter-posits:

```text
may S be harmed
may S be destroyed
```

Thus:

```text
recognition of being
    becomes
orientation toward negation of being
```

### False view as meta-level negation

False view differs from the other two because it does not only misrelate to one object.

It negates the governing distinction of the practical domain:

```text
Wholesome ≠ Unwholesome
```

becomes:

```text
Wholesome / Unwholesome distinction
    is denied
```

This is a second-order corruption.

It attacks not merely one relation, but the validity of practical classification itself.

### Path versus activity

The three mental courses are:

```text
determinative orientations
```

The seven bodily and verbal courses are:

```text
realized expressions
```

So:

```text
mental course
    gives direction

bodily/verbal course
    gives execution
```

The same structure later appears architecturally as:

```text
non-manifest practical determination
    ↓
manifest activity
```

But we should not yet identify this too quickly with `avijñapti` and `vijñapti`; the kārikā presently establishes the functional distinction.

## 10. Light Organon Interpretation

This verse yields a very clean Rational Agent architecture.

A practical system must distinguish three levels:

```text
AfflictiveRoot
    general bias or governing defect

MentalCourse
    determinate practical orientation

ManifestAction
    bodily or verbal execution
```

A possible model:

```text
PracticalProcess {
    root
    orientation
    intendedTransition
    manifestAction
    completionEvent
    persistentEffect
}
```

### Covetous orientation

```text
object belongs to Other

agent projects:
    object should become Mine
```

### Hostile orientation

```text
being exists and flourishes

agent projects:
    being should be harmed
```

### False principial orientation

```text
system distinguishes:
    wholesome / unwholesome

agent asserts:
    distinction has no validity
```

These are three distinct failure operators:

```text
AppropriativeProjection
HostileNegation
PracticalLawDenial
```

### The technological significance

A Rational Agent cannot be governed solely by output filtering.

It must inspect the practical orientation from which outputs are generated.

A statement may appear harmless while participating in:

```text
appropriative strategy
hostile strategy
denial of valid governing distinctions
```

Therefore:

```text
action evaluation
    cannot begin only at output
```

It must include:

```text
root
orientation
planned transition
manifest realization
```

This is precisely why the Kośa keeps separating mental paths from bodily and verbal karma.

### Pure Practical Reason

The mental courses should not be modeled as hidden inner objects.

They are better represented as policies of transition:

```text
Covetousness
    unauthorized acquisition policy

IllWill
    harm-directed policy

FalseView
    invalidation of the normative causal schema
```

That keeps the framework practical rather than psychological.
