# VAK_4.117

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

```sanskrit
गतिदुःखोपकारित्वगुणैः क्षेत्रं विशिष्यते ।
अग्रं मुक्तस्य मुक्ताय बोधिसत्त्वस्य चाष्टमम् ॥ ४.११७ ॥
```

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

```text
gatiduḥkhopakāritvaguṇaiḥ kṣetraṃ viśiṣyate |
agraṃ muktasya muktāya bodhisattvasya cāṣṭamam || 4.117 ||
```

This verse develops the third determinant of giving announced in VAK_4.114:

```text
kṣetra-viśeṣa
    distinction of the recipient-field
```

The first half is structurally clear. The compressed second half introduces a hierarchy whose full enumeration continues into VAK_4.118 and should be confirmed in the Bhāṣya.

## 3. Padaccheda

```text
gati-duḥkha-upakāritva-guṇaiḥ |
kṣetram |
viśiṣyate |

agram |
muktasya |
muktāya |
bodhisattvasya |
ca |
aṣṭamam
```

A cautious prose arrangement is:

```text
kṣetraṃ
gatyā, duḥkhena, upakāritvena, guṇaiś ca
viśiṣyate.

muktasya muktāya [dānam] agram;
bodhisattvasya ca aṣṭamam.
```

The most secure reading of the first sentence is:

> A recipient-field is distinguished by destiny, suffering, beneficence, and qualities.

The second may provisionally be rendered:

> The foremost gift is that of a liberated person to one who is liberated; the Bodhisattva’s is the eighth.

But `bodhisattvasya cāṣṭamam` is highly compressed. Its exact relation to the eight categories completed in VAK_4.118 requires the Bhāṣya.

## 4. Grammar

| Term | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `gati` | compound member | destiny or mode of rebirth |
| `duḥkha` | compound member | suffering, affliction, distress |
| `upakāritva` | compound member | the condition of having benefited another |
| `guṇaiḥ` | instrumental plural | through qualities or excellences |
| `kṣetram` | nominative neuter singular | field, recipient-field |
| `viśiṣyate` | passive, third singular | is distinguished, becomes excellent |
| `agram` | nominative/accusative neuter singular | foremost, highest |
| `muktasya` | genitive singular | of one who is liberated |
| `muktāya` | dative singular | to one who is liberated |
| `bodhisattvasya` | genitive singular | of the Bodhisattva |
| `aṣṭamam` | ordinal neuter singular | the eighth |

The instrumental compound lists the grounds through which a recipient becomes karmically distinctive:

```text
gati
duḥkha
upakāritva
guṇa
```

## 5. Literal translation

> The field is distinguished by destiny, suffering, beneficence, and qualities. The foremost is [the gift] of a liberated person to one who is liberated; and that of the Bodhisattva is the eighth.

Because the second half depends upon an enumerative explanation continuing into VAK_4.118, this translation remains provisional.

## 6. Philosophical translation

> A recipient becomes a distinctive field of merit for four different reasons: because of the recipient’s condition of rebirth, because of the suffering presently borne, because of benefits previously bestowed upon the giver, or because of spiritual qualities actually possessed. At the summit stands a gift in which liberated agency gives to a liberated field. The verse then situates the Bodhisattva within an eightfold hierarchy whose precise arrangement is supplied by the commentary and the next kārikā.

The recipient is thus not judged according to one linear scale.

The field may be exceptional through:

```text
need
relation
attainment
or existential condition
```

## 7. `Kṣetra`

```text
kṣetra
    field
    recipient-field
    causal field in which an act is planted
```

The recipient is called a field because the karmic activity of giving develops differently according to the relation into which it enters.

The field does not mechanically “create” the giver’s karma.

Rather, it determines the object and significance of the giver’s volition.

```text
giving to one relation
    is not karmically identical with
giving to another relation
```

The field is therefore a constitutive moment of the deed.

## 8. Four determinations of the field

The first half gives four grounds of distinction:

```text
1. gati
    destiny

2. duḥkha
    suffering

3. upakāritva
    prior beneficence

4. guṇa
    spiritual qualities
```

These are not four mutually exclusive classes. One recipient may embody several simultaneously.

For example, a parent may be:

```text
a prior benefactor
    +
currently sick
    +
possessed of moral qualities
```

The field then becomes multiply determined.

## 9. `Gati`

```text
gati
    destination
    mode of rebirth
    existential course
```

A being’s destiny can distinguish the field.

This may refer to recipients whose mode of existence involves exceptional deprivation or limitation.

The karmic meaning is relational:

```text
the same object
    has different practical significance
in different destinies
```

Food, protection, or instruction may carry special weight where access to benefit is restricted.

`Gati` therefore describes the recipient’s general existential situation.

## 10. `Duḥkha`

```text
duḥkha
    suffering
    pain
    distress
```

Suffering itself can make a being an exceptional field.

This is distinct from spiritual excellence.

A sick, injured, hungry, threatened, or otherwise afflicted being may possess no extraordinary attainment, yet the urgency of suffering intensifies the practical significance of assistance.

```text
great need
    →
great capacity to be benefited
```

The field is distinguished because the gift removes or diminishes an actual negation.

## 11. Need as a positive karmic determination

The suffering recipient is not a “lower-quality” field merely because the recipient lacks spiritual attainments.

The field’s excellence arises here from need.

```text
guṇa-field
    distinguished by what the recipient possesses

duḥkha-field
    distinguished by what the recipient suffers
```

Both can intensify giving, but through opposite structures.

One honors actuality of excellence.

The other responds to actuality of deprivation.

## 12. `Upakāritva`

```text
upakāra
    benefit
    assistance
    service rendered

upakāritva
    the condition of having been beneficial
```

A person who has previously benefited the giver becomes a distinguished field.

Paradigmatic cases include:

```text
mother
father
teacher
caregiver
protector
```

The gift now possesses the structure of gratitude or repayment.

```text
prior benefit received
    →
present obligation of recognition
```

This connects directly with the gravity assigned earlier to harming one’s parents.

The same historical relation that intensifies injury also intensifies benefaction.

## 13. The field remembers history

`Upakāritva` shows again that karmic relations are historical.

The recipient is not evaluated only through present intrinsic predicates.

The relevant question is also:

```text
What has this being been
in relation to this giver?
```

A parent is an exceptional field because the present relation contains an accumulated history of support.

```text
past benefit
    persists as
present practical determination
```

The field includes relational memory.

## 14. `Guṇa`

```text
guṇa
    quality
    excellence
    spiritual attainment
```

A recipient may be distinguished by realized qualities such as:

```text
ethical discipline
meditative attainment
Path attainment
freedom from affliction
Buddhahood
```

This is the familiar `guṇakṣetra`:

```text
field of qualities
```

Giving toward such a field functions especially as `pūjā`—honor directed toward excellence.

The superior quality does not merely increase the recipient’s worth as a person. It changes the meaning of the giver’s act.

## 15. Four field-relations

We can now organize the first half:

| Ground | Recipient distinguished as | Form of giving |
|---|---|---|
| `gati` | being in a special existential destiny | support appropriate to condition |
| `duḥkha` | one presently suffering | compassionate relief |
| `upakāritva` | former benefactor | gratitude and repayment |
| `guṇa` | possessor of excellent qualities | honor and reverence |

Thus the field can be exceptional from below, behind, or above:

```text
duḥkha
    need calling for assistance

upakāritva
    prior benefit calling for gratitude

guṇa
    excellence calling for honor
```

`Gati` supplies the general existential placement within which these relations occur.

## 16. `Muktasya muktāya`

```text
muktasya
    of a liberated giver

muktāya
    to a liberated recipient
```

This is an extraordinary conjunction.

The giver is liberated.

The recipient is liberated.

Therefore neither side is governed by ordinary acquisitive attachment.

The gift becomes maximally purified on both poles:

```text
giver free from grasping
    →
recipient free from grasping
```

The act is no longer primarily structured by:

```text
hope of reward
need for possession
social competition
or craving for enjoyment
```

It is the circulation of benefit through freedom.

## 17. Why would one liberated person give to another?

Liberation does not make practical relations disappear.

A liberated person may still:

```text
offer requisites
honor qualities
support bodily continuity
express gratitude
assist teaching activity
```

The difference is that the deed is no longer governed by possessiveness.

Similarly, the liberated recipient may use the gift without appropriation or craving.

Thus the object circulates without bondage at either end.

```text
use
    without
appropriative attachment
```

## 18. `Agram`

```text
agram
    foremost
    highest
    at the summit
```

The highest giving therefore appears to involve excellence of both:

```text
giver
and
field
```

This is significant because the preceding verses treated these separately.

VAK_4.117 now combines them:

```text
distinguished giver
    +
distinguished recipient-field
    =
foremost giving
```

The karmic event reaches its highest form when both poles are purified.

## 19. `Bodhisattvasya cāṣṭamam`

```text
bodhisattvasya
    of the Bodhisattva

aṣṭamam
    eighth
```

The phrase is too compressed to settle securely from the kārikā alone.

VAK_4.118 immediately lists exceptional recipients:

```text
mother
father
sick person
Dharma speaker
one in the final birth
Bodhisattva
```

This suggests that VAK_4.117–118 together explain an eightfold set of immeasurably fruitful gifts or fields.

Possible interpretation:

```text
the Bodhisattva occupies the eighth position
within an enumerated series of exceptional fields or gifts
```

However, we should not force the exact count or ranking before reading the Bhāṣya.

The secure kārikā-level point is that the Bodhisattva is treated as an exceptional field, distinct from the ordinary noble hierarchy.

## 20. Relation to VAK_4.114–4.116

The sequence is now complete:

### VAK_4.114

```text
giving differs through:
    giver
    gift-object
    recipient-field
```

### VAK_4.115

```text
giver-quality
```

### VAK_4.116

```text
gift-object quality
```

### VAK_4.117

```text
recipient-field quality
```

Thus the analysis of giving has traversed the whole practical syllogism:

```text
giver
    →
gift-object
        →
recipient-field
```

No moment alone determines the complete karmic value.

## 21. Field excellence is plural

A major conceptual achievement of the verse is that “excellent field” has several meanings.

It does not mean only:

```text
the most spiritually advanced recipient
```

The sick person can be a distinguished field through suffering.

A parent can be distinguished through prior benefit.

A liberated being is distinguished through qualities.

The field is therefore determined through the specific relation activated by giving.

```text
field-excellence
    =
relation-specific significance
```

## 22. Doctrinal determination

The verse establishes:

1. the recipient-field is the third determinant of giving;
2. a field may be distinguished through destiny;
3. it may be distinguished through suffering;
4. it may be distinguished through prior beneficence;
5. it may be distinguished through spiritual qualities;
6. the highest conjunction joins a liberated giver with a liberated recipient;
7. the Bodhisattva belongs to an exceptional eightfold classification completed in the following verse;
8. the precise internal order of that classification remains for the Bhāṣya.

## 23. Logical determination

A recipient-field can be modeled:

```text
RecipientField {
    destiny
    suffering
    priorBenefitToGiver
    realizedQualities
}
```

Field distinction:

```text
DistinguishedField(?f)
    ←
ExceptionalDestiny(?f)
    ∨ SevereSuffering(?f)
    ∨ PriorBenefactor(?f)
    ∨ ExcellentQualities(?f)
```

The supreme conjunction:

```text
LiberatedGiver(?g)
∧ LiberatedRecipient(?r)
∧ Gives(?g, ?gift, ?r)
→ ForemostGivingEvent(?event)
```

This does not imply that freedom is produced by the gift. It means that the activity is purified at both relational poles.

## 24. Organon interpretation

The recipient is not a passive terminus.

The field actively determines what the giver’s activity means.

```text
to the suffering
    giving is relief

to the benefactor
    giving is gratitude

to the excellent
    giving is honor

to the liberated
    giving becomes free circulation
```

The identical external transfer acquires a different Concept according to its relation.

This is a powerful example of determinate relation:

```text
karma
    is not the isolated deed

karma
    is the deed as qualified
by the complete relational field
```

At the summit, liberated giver and liberated recipient form a relation in which the object no longer mediates bondage.

The object is used, transferred, and received, but neither pole is constituted through possession.

That is almost a model of **reciprocity released from appropriation**.
