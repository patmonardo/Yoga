# VAK_4.03 — Configuration Problems and Verbal Manifestation

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

```sanskrit
न कस्यचिदहेतोः स्याद्धेतुः स्याच्च विनाशकः ।
द्विग्राह्यं स्यान्न चाणौ तद्वाग्विज्ञप्तिस्तु वाग्ध्वनिः ॥
```

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

```iast
na kasyacid ahetoḥ syād hetuḥ syāc ca vināśakaḥ |
dvigrāhyaṃ syān na cāṇau tad vāg-vijñaptis tu vāg-dhvaniḥ ||
```

## 3. Source Caution

Source reading:

```iast
na kasyacidahetoḥ syāt hetuḥ syācca vināśakaḥ /
dvigrāhyaṃ syāt na cāṇau tat vāgvijñaptistu vāgdhvaniḥ // VAkK_4.3 //
```

Light separation:

```iast
na kasyacit ahetoḥ syāt |
hetuḥ syāt ca vināśakaḥ |

dvi-grāhyam syāt |
na ca aṇau tat |

vāk-vijñaptiḥ tu vāk-dhvaniḥ ||
```

This is an unusually compressed verse. The first three pādas appear to continue the controversy concerning momentariness and the reality of bodily configuration. The final pāda then gives a comparatively straightforward determination of verbal manifestation.

For the first-pass kārikā analysis, distinguish:

```text
secure determination:
    verbal manifestation is vocal sound

probable argumentative material:
    destruction and its cause
    the double apprehension of configuration
    the absence of configuration in a single atom
```

The exact argumentative assignments should remain open until the Bhāṣya pass.

## 5. Grammar

### na kasyacid ahetoḥ syāt

This segment is grammatically compressed and lacks an explicit subject.

```text
na
    not

kasyacit
    of anything / for anything

a-hetoḥ
    without a cause

syāt
    would be
```

The understood subject may be supplied from the preceding discussion, most plausibly something such as destruction or arising. The cautious literal form is:

> It would not occur for anything without a cause.

We should not yet decide whether the occurrence under discussion is arising, perishing, or a particular change of configuration.

### hetuḥ syāc ca vināśakaḥ

```text
hetuḥ
    a cause or ground

syāt
    would be

ca
    and

vināśakaḥ
    destructive
```

Literal structure:

> And the cause would be destructive.

`vināśaka` is an agent noun or adjective from `vi + √naś`, to perish or be destroyed. The line appears to formulate a consequence concerning a cause of destruction:

```text
if destruction has a cause,
    that cause must function as a destroyer
```

The Bhāṣya will be needed to establish whether this is Vasubandhu’s inference, an opponent’s objection, or a compressed reply.

### dvigrāhyaṃ syāt

```text
dvi
    two / twice

grāhya
    to be apprehended
    apprehensible

syāt
    would be
```

Possible renderings:

> It would be apprehensible twice.

or:

> It would be apprehensible in two ways.

The subject is again unstated. The most probable referent is `saṃsthāna`, bodily configuration, carried forward from VAK_4.02. The precise meaning of the double apprehension should not yet be fixed.

### na cāṇau tat

```text
na ca
    and it is not

aṇau
    in an atom

tat
    that
```

The locative singular `aṇau` means in a single atom or at the atomic level. `tat` most naturally refers to the configuration under discussion:

> And that does not exist in an atom.

This gives a substantial challenge to treating configuration as an independently real material determination.

### vāg-vijñaptis tu vāg-dhvaniḥ

This pāda is clear:

```text
vāk-vijñaptiḥ
    verbal manifestation

tu
    but / whereas

vāk-dhvaniḥ
    vocal sound
```

> Verbal manifestation, however, is vocal sound.

The particle `tu` marks a transition or contrast. After the difficult analysis of bodily manifestation, verbal manifestation is directly identified with sound.

## 6. Literal Translation

It would not occur for anything without a cause, and the cause would be destructive. It would be apprehensible twice, and that does not exist in an atom. Verbal manifestation, however, is vocal sound.

Because the subjects of the first and third statements are compressed, this literal translation intentionally preserves their indeterminacy.

## 7. Philosophical Translation

Nothing undergoes the relevant change without a ground; if destruction depends upon a ground, that ground must function destructively. Moreover, bodily configuration faces the difficulties that it would be apprehended doubly and that no such configuration exists in a single atom. Verbal manifestation, by contrast, consists in vocal sound.

The supplied subjects in the first two sentences are interpretive and must be checked against the commentary.

## 8. Technical Vocabulary

| Sanskrit | Working Translation | Determination |
|---|---|---|
| ahetu | without a ground / cause | groundlessness denied |
| vināśaka | destructive | that which brings about destruction |
| dvi-grāhya | doubly apprehensible | apprehensible twice or in two ways |
| aṇu | atom | minimal material unit |
| vāk-vijñapti | verbal manifestation | expressive vocal action |
| vāk-dhvani | vocal sound | manifest form of verbal karma |

The distinction between `vināśa`, perishing, and `vināśaka`, the destroyer or destructive ground, may become important for the momentariness argument.

The statement concerning the atom introduces a difficult ontological question:

```text
Can something that exists only at the level of an aggregate
be a real dharma in its own right?
```

## 9. Doctrinal Determination

VAK_4.02 provisionally identified bodily manifestation as:

```text
saṃsthāna
    configuration
```

VAK_4.03 immediately subjects this determination to difficulties:

```text
configuration
    → doubly apprehensible?
    → absent from the single atom
```

Thus the text does not simply present an unproblematic doctrine of bodily action as shape. It begins exposing the ontological pressure within that doctrine.

The contrast is:

```text
bodily manifestation
    disputed ontological analysis

verbal manifestation
    vocal sound
```

Speech has a readily identifiable manifest material determination: sound. Body is more difficult: not enduring motion; configuration is proposed; but configuration itself raises problems.

## 10. Logical Determination

The sequence VAK_4.02–4.03 now appears as:

```text
bodily action cannot be continuous motion
    because conditioned existence is momentary

therefore bodily manifestation is proposed as configuration

but configuration is problematic:
    it may involve double apprehension
    it is not found in the individual atom
```

Thus the movement is not:

```text
motion rejected
    → configuration securely established
```

It is instead:

```text
motion rejected
    → configuration proposed
    → configuration questioned
```

The Theory of Karma begins by discovering that the apparent bodily act cannot easily be located in a single ontological substrate. It is neither straightforwardly an enduring movement nor unproblematically an atomic shape.

## 11. Interpretive Note

This verse warns us not to identify `vijñapti` too quickly with a simple physical form.

The bodily manifestation appears to depend upon:

```text
a plurality of momentary material determinations
an arrangement among them
an apprehension of that arrangement
```

But the arrangement is not present in an individual atom.

Therefore the manifest act may already have a relational status:

```text
not a thing added beside the atoms

but a determination of their arrangement
```

That is a suggestive possibility, not yet a settled translation or doctrine.

## 12. Light Organon Interpretation

The textual pressure can be stated without imposing a full interpretation:

```text
motion
    cannot be the persistent bearer of action

configuration
    cannot be simply located in one material atom

verbal manifestation
    can be located in produced sound
```

The bodily act therefore confronts us with a problem of form as relation:

```text
the configuration is real enough to manifest volition

yet it is not contained
    in any isolated atomic determination
```

“Principle Pure Moment” may later become relevant, but it is best held in reserve until the text determines how momentary material plurality, manifest action, and non-manifest action are connected.
