# VAK_4.121

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

```sanskrit
चैत्ये त्यागान्वयं पुण्यं मैत्र्यादिवदगृह्णति ।
कुक्षेत्रेऽपीष्टफलता फलबीजाविपर्ययात् ॥ ४.१२१ ॥
```

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

```text
caitye tyāgānvayaṃ puṇyaṃ maitryādivad agṛhṇati |
kukṣetre ’pīṣṭaphalatā phalabījāviparyayāt || 4.121 ||
```

The local electronic source reads:

```text
caitye tyāgānvayaṃ puṇyaṃ maitryādivadagṛṇhati /
kukṣetre 'pīṣṭaphalatā phalabījāviparyayāt //
```

The crucial segmentation is:

```text
maitryādivat | agṛhṇati
```

where `agṛhṇati` is understood as:

```text
a-gṛhṇati
    when no one receives
    in the absence of a recipient who takes possession
```

The verse answers two objections:

1. How can an offering to a shrine produce merit when no living recipient receives it?
2. How can giving produce a desirable result when the recipient-field is poor or corrupt?

## 3. Padaccheda

```text
caitye |
tyāga-anvayam |
puṇyam |
maitrī-ādi-vat |
a-gṛhṇati |

ku-kṣetre |
api |
iṣṭa-phalatā |
phala-bīja-aviparyayāt
```

Prose order:

```text
caitye, agṛhṇati api,
maitryādivat tyāgānvayaṃ puṇyam [bhavati].

kukṣetre api iṣṭaphalatā [bhavati],
phalabījayor aviparyayāt.
```

> In relation to a shrine, even though no one receives the gift, merit associated with relinquishment arises, just as with loving-kindness and the other immeasurables. Even when the field is poor, there is a desirable fruit, because seed and fruit are not inverted.

## 4. Grammar

| Term | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `caitye` | locative singular | at or with respect to a shrine |
| `tyāga-anvayam` | nominative/accusative neuter singular | connected with relinquishment |
| `puṇyam` | nominative neuter singular | merit |
| `maitryādi-vat` | comparative indeclinable | as with loving-kindness and the others |
| `a-gṛhṇati` | locative absolute-like expression | when no one receives |
| `ku-kṣetre` | locative singular | in a bad or inferior field |
| `api` | particle | even |
| `iṣṭa-phalatā` | nominative feminine singular | possession of desirable fruit |
| `phala-bīja` | compound | fruit and seed |
| `aviparyayāt` | ablative singular | because of non-inversion or non-reversal |

The verse turns upon two compounds:

```text
tyāgānvaya
    connected with relinquishment

phalabījāviparyaya
    non-inversion of fruit and seed
```

## 5. Literal translation

> At a shrine, merit connected with relinquishment arises even when no one receives the gift, just as with loving-kindness and the other states. Even in a bad field, there is desirable fruit, because fruit and seed are not inverted.

## 6. Philosophical translation

> The merit of giving does not depend exclusively upon a recipient’s act of taking possession. An offering made at a shrine produces merit through the giver’s actual relinquishment, just as loving-kindness produces wholesome causal efficacy without transferring an object to someone who receives it. Nor does an inferior recipient reverse the moral nature of a wholesome gift: the field may diminish or modify its magnitude, but a wholesome seed still issues in a desirable fruit, because the causal character of seed and result is not turned into its opposite.

This verse sharply limits the power of the external field.

```text
the field conditions magnitude

but does not arbitrarily reverse
the moral quality of the seed
```

# 7. The first problem: no receiving subject

The preceding verses defined giving through a complete relation:

```text
giver
gift-object
recipient-field
```

That naturally raises an objection.

When an offering is made to a `caitya`—a shrine, reliquary, or monument associated with the Buddha—there may be no living recipient who personally receives and enjoys the object.

So one might argue:

```text
no receiver
    →
no completed giving
    →
no merit
```

VAK_4.121 denies this inference.

# 8. `Caitya`

```text
caitya
    shrine
    sacred monument
    reliquary
    object or place of reverential commemoration
```

An offering to a caitya may include:

```text
flowers
lamps
incense
banners
ornaments
construction
repair
maintenance
```

The offering is directed toward the Buddha as an honored field, even though the Buddha does not physically receive or consume it.

The practical relation is one of:

```text
pūjā
    reverence and honor
```

rather than ordinary material assistance.

# 9. `Tyāga`

```text
tyāga
    relinquishment
    abandonment
    letting go
```

The decisive factor is the giver’s relinquishment.

The giver changes the practical status of the object:

```text
private possession
    →
object dedicated beyond private use
```

The object is no longer retained for personal enjoyment.

This objective surrender is sufficient to constitute the relevant meritorious activity, even when no living individual takes possession.

```text
receipt by another
    is one form of completion

genuine dedication and relinquishment
    is another
```

# 10. `Tyāgānvayaṃ puṇyam`

```text
tyāga-anvaya
    following upon relinquishment
    connected with relinquishment
    belonging to the act of letting go
```

The merit adheres to the act insofar as it embodies release from possessiveness.

The causal point is not:

```text
an object physically moves
from hand A to hand B
```

but:

```text
appropriative control is intentionally surrendered
```

Thus the core of generosity is not mere displacement of matter.

It is transformation of the agent’s possessive relation.

# 11. Why compare it to loving-kindness?

```text
maitrī
    loving-kindness
    benevolent orientation toward beings
```

Loving-kindness produces wholesome causal efficacy without requiring that another person receive a material object.

The practitioner cultivates:

```text
May beings be happy.
May beings possess the causes of happiness.
```

The wholesome transformation occurs in the practitioner’s continuum through the formation of a universal benevolent orientation.

Likewise:

```text
maitrī
    does not require material reception

caitya-giving
    does not require personal reception
```

Both are efficacious through the agent’s genuine practical determination.

# 12. `Maitryādi`

The `ādi` most naturally includes the other immeasurable states:

```text
karuṇā
    compassion

muditā
    sympathetic joy

upekṣā
    equanimity
```

These states establish wholesome relations toward beings even when no specific recipient consciously acknowledges or accepts them.

The comparison therefore identifies a broader principle:

```text
wholesome activity
    need not depend upon
the recipient’s awareness or acceptance
```

The causal transformation can occur in the agent’s orientation itself.

# 13. Recipient acceptance is not the sole causal switch

The verse rejects a crude transactional model:

```text
merit begins only when
the recipient accepts the object
```

That model would make the receiver sovereign over the giver’s moral act.

But the Kośa instead identifies several distinct moments:

```text
intention
relinquishment
objective dedication
field
acceptance
use
```

Not every form of giving requires every moment in precisely the same way.

A shrine offering is complete through dedication and relinquishment, though ordinary assistance may require effective delivery to a living recipient.

# 14. The instrument can close without consumption

This is important for our examination of the instrument.

The giving apparatus does not always terminate in consumption.

There are at least two circuits:

## Beneficial transfer

```text
giver
    →
gift
        →
recipient’s use
```

## Reverential dedication

```text
giver
    →
gift
        →
symbolic-sacred field
```

The first realizes `anugraha`.

The second realizes `pūjā`.

In both cases, the giver’s possessive relation is transformed.

# 15. The second problem: the bad field

```text
ku-kṣetra
    bad field
    inferior field
    corrupt or unworthy recipient-field
```

The second objection is almost the reverse of the first.

If the field affects karmic magnitude, perhaps a corrupt field could turn wholesome giving into an undesirable result.

The verse denies this.

```text
kukṣetre ’pi
    even in a bad field
```

there remains:

```text
iṣṭaphalatā
    a desirable fruit
```

# 16. What can make a field “bad”?

A field may be inferior because the recipient:

```text
lacks ethical qualities
uses the gift poorly
is ungrateful
is morally corrupt
has little spiritual attainment
```

The exact Bhāṣya examples will refine this.

The secure principle is that the field lacks the excellences that would magnify the gift.

It is a `kukṣetra`, not necessarily because the giver’s action is itself corrupt, but because the receiving relation is deficient.

# 17. Field affects measure, not basic polarity

The previous verses established:

```text
excellent field
    →
greater karmic result
```

VAK_4.121 now guards against an overextension:

```text
inferior field
    does not necessarily make
a wholesome gift unwholesome
```

The field can affect:

```text
strength
breadth
magnitude
special form of maturation
```

But it does not automatically reverse:

```text
wholesome
    into
unwholesome
```

This preserves the causal contribution of the giver’s own activity.

# 18. `Iṣṭaphalatā`

```text
iṣṭa
    desired
    agreeable
    favorable

phala
    fruit

iṣṭaphalatā
    possession of a desirable fruit
```

The wholesome gift still yields a favorable maturation.

The result may be smaller than one produced in an excellent field, but it remains desirable in basic quality.

Thus we must distinguish:

```text
quality of fruit
    wholesome or unwholesome polarity

magnitude of fruit
    weak or intense
```

A poor field can diminish the second without reversing the first.

# 19. `Phala-bīja`

```text
bīja
    seed
    causal potential

phala
    fruit
    matured result
```

The verse employs an agricultural causal model already implicit in `kṣetra`.

```text
seed
    the morally qualified activity

field
    the recipient relation

fruit
    the maturation
```

All three matter, but they have different functions.

The field conditions the seed’s development.

It does not simply replace the seed with its opposite.

# 20. `Aviparyaya`

```text
a-viparyaya
    non-inversion
    non-reversal
    absence of transformation into the contrary
```

The causal order is not inverted:

```text
wholesome seed
    does not become
an unwholesome fruit merely through a poor field
```

Nor does:

```text
unwholesome seed
    become wholesome
merely through an excellent field
```

The field can amplify karmic gravity, but it cannot independently purify a corrupt intention.

This is causal non-confusion.

# 21. Seed, field, and fruit

The verse gives a precise division of causal labor.

## Seed

```text
the agent’s morally qualified activity
```

determines the basic character of the result.

## Field

```text
the relational environment
```

conditions magnitude and specific development.

## Fruit

```text
the matured expression
```

corresponds to the seed as modified, but not morally reversed, through the field.

Therefore:

```text
Fruit =
    SeedQuality
    developed through
    FieldConditions
```

not:

```text
Fruit =
    FieldQuality alone
```

# 22. A necessary correction to field theory

The sequence VAK_4.114–4.118 might tempt us to absolutize the field.

Those verses showed astonishing differences according to:

```text
parents
the sick
Dharma speakers
Bodhisattvas
liberated beings
```

VAK_4.121 restores balance.

```text
the field is constitutive

but the field is not omnipotent
```

The giver’s intention and relinquishment retain their own causal determination.

The complete act is relational, but relationality does not erase the distinct contribution of each moment.

# 23. The bad recipient does not own the giver’s virtue

Suppose a person gives sincerely to relieve suffering, but the recipient later misuses the gift.

The recipient’s later conduct does not retroactively become the giver’s original volition.

```text
giver’s wholesome intention
    remains the giver’s seed

recipient’s misuse
    becomes the recipient’s activity
```

There may be complications if misuse was foreseeable or intentionally supported, but the fundamental rule remains:

> One continuum does not simply inherit the moral determination produced by another continuum’s later choice.

The instrument has distinct causal ownership at each node.

# 24. The shrine does not need to “receive”

The first half and second half belong together.

## First half

```text
recipient acceptance is not necessary
for merit grounded in relinquishment
```

## Second half

```text
recipient deficiency is not sufficient
to reverse wholesome merit
```

Together they limit dependence on the external recipient.

```text
the recipient matters

but neither creates
nor wholly controls
the giver’s karmic quality
```

This is an exact calibration of the relational model.

# 25. Examination of the instrument

VAK_4.121 identifies which components of the giving instrument are essential and which are variable.

Essential for shrine merit:

```text
wholesome orientation
genuine relinquishment
appropriate sacred field
objective dedication
```

Not essential:

```text
a living receiver’s act of acceptance
```

For giving in a bad field:

```text
field quality may be deficient
```

yet the wholesome seed remains capable of favorable maturation.

Thus the Kośa performs something like dependency analysis:

```text
Which component determines existence of merit?

Which component determines magnitude?

Which component determines moral polarity?
```

# 26. Three different causal questions

The verse requires us to distinguish:

## Does merit arise?

```text
through genuine wholesome relinquishment
```

## How great is the merit?

```text
conditioned by giver, object, field,
intensity, and accompaniment
```

## What basic kind of fruit arises?

```text
determined by the wholesome
or unwholesome quality of the seed
```

Confusing these questions produces doctrinal errors.

# 27. Instrumental modularity

In Organon terms, the verse tells us that the giving system is modular.

```text
Relinquishment module
    can complete merit
without a live acceptance event

Field module
    can scale the result
without reversing seed polarity
```

So we should not build the evaluator as one undifferentiated score.

We need separate determinations for:

```text
act completion
moral quality
field multiplier
causal magnitude
result type
```

This is exactly the kind of distinction needed in an evaluative architecture.

# 28. Karma is not consequentialism

The verse also rules out a simple result-only ethics.

If the recipient is bad or fails to use the gift well, the giver’s wholesome act does not thereby become unwholesome.

```text
later external outcome
    is not the sole definition
of the original karma
```

Yet the Kośa is not indifferent to consequences, because:

```text
field
object
foreseeability
effective benefit
```

all matter.

Its position is more articulated:

> Karma is determined by the entire causal structure, but each component contributes according to its own function.

# 29. Karma is not intention-only ethics

Nor is the doctrine reducible to:

```text
good intention
    is all that matters
```

The preceding verses established the importance of:

```text
completion
object suitability
field
execution
accompaniment
```

VAK_4.121 says that relinquishment can be sufficient without personal reception in a specific kind of reverential act.

It does not say that objective execution never matters.

Thus the rule is context-sensitive, not subjectivist.

# 30. Relation to VAK_4.119–4.120

VAK_4.119 analyzed karmic intensity through the complete instrument.

VAK_4.120 analyzed causal accumulation.

VAK_4.121 now clarifies causal invariants.

```text
VAK_4.119
    Which factors strengthen the deed?

VAK_4.120
    Which factors consolidate the deed?

VAK_4.121
    Which relations can vary
    without reversing the seed’s basic nature?
```

This is a remarkably systematic final movement.

# 31. Doctrinal determination

The verse establishes:

1. an offering to a shrine can produce merit without a living recipient’s acceptance;
2. the relevant merit is connected with genuine relinquishment;
3. loving-kindness provides an analogy for wholesome efficacy without material reception;
4. the recipient-field is not the sole source of the giver’s merit;
5. a poor field may diminish or modify karmic magnitude;
6. a poor field does not automatically reverse wholesome giving into undesirable fruit;
7. the moral character of the fruit corresponds to the seed;
8. seed and fruit are not causally inverted by the field.

# 32. Logical determination

Shrine offering:

```text
WholesomeReverentialIntention(g)
∧ Relinquishes(g, object)
∧ DedicatesTo(g, object, caitya)
→ MeritoriousGiving(g)
```

No receiver is required:

```text
MeritoriousShrineOffering(event)
↛ LivingRecipientAccepts(event)
```

Bad field:

```text
WholesomeGivingSeed(event)
∧ InferiorRecipientField(event)
→ DesirableFruit(event)
```

while:

```text
InferiorRecipientField(event)
→ mayReduce(KarmicMagnitude(event))
```

but not:

```text
InferiorRecipientField(event)
→ reverses(WholesomeSeed, UnwholesomeFruit)
```

# 33. Organon interpretation

This verse gives us a precise theory of causal interfaces.

The receiver is not the producer of the giver’s moral determination. The receiver is one conditioning node within the complete circuit.

```text
giver’s orientation
    →
relinquishment
    →
field relation
    →
maturation
```

The shrine case shows that a symbolic-objective field can close the act without a consuming subject.

The bad-field case shows that the external node can scale but cannot arbitrarily rewrite the originating determination.

In computational terms:

```text
field modifies parameters

field does not mutate
the semantic type of the seed
into its contrary
```

This is almost a type-preservation rule for karmic causality.
