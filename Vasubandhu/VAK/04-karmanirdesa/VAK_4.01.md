# VAK_4.01 — Karma and the Differentiation of the World

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

```sanskrit
कर्मजं लोकवैचित्र्यं चेतना तत्कृतं च तत् ।
चेतना मानसं कर्म तज्जं वाक्कायकर्मणी ॥
```

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

```iast
karmajaṃ lokavaicitryaṃ cetanā tatkṛtaṃ ca tat /
cetanā mānasaṃ karma tajjaṃ vākkāyakarmaṇī //
```

## 3. Source Caution

External source reference: `VAkK_4.1`.

Source reading:

```iast
karmajaṃ lokavaicitryaṃ cetanā tatkṛtaṃ ca tat /
cetanā mānasaṃ karma tajjaṃ vākkāyakarmaṇī //
```

Useful syntactic segmentation:

```iast
karmajaṃ loka-vaicitryam /
cetanā tat-kṛtaṃ ca tat /
cetanā mānasaṃ karma /
tat-jaṃ vāk-kāya-karmaṇī //
```

The final pāda is compressed. `tajjaṃ` is neuter singular, while `vākkāyakarmaṇī` is neuter dual. The syntax supplies an understood singular `karma`:

```text
tat-jaṃ [karma]
    =
verbal action and bodily action
```

## 5. Grammar

| Term | Form | Function |
|---|---|---|
| karma-jam | neuter nominative singular adjective | predicate of `loka-vaicitryam` |
| loka-vaicitryam | neuter nominative singular | subject: the differentiation of the world |
| cetanā | feminine nominative singular | first member of the definition of karma |
| tat-kṛtam | neuter nominative singular participial compound | what is produced by volition |
| tat | neuter nominative singular pronoun | refers back to karma |
| mānasam karma | neuter nominative singular phrase | mental action |
| tat-jam | neuter nominative singular adjective | action born from volition |
| vāk-kāya-karmaṇī | neuter nominative dual | verbal action and bodily action |

The verse gives two intersecting classifications.

First, by causal structure:

```text
karma
    = cetanā
    + cetanā-kṛta
```

Second, by domain:

```text
mental action
verbal action
bodily action
```

The two divisions intersect asymmetrically: volition itself is mental karma, while verbal and bodily karma are produced by volition.

## 6. Literal Translation

The differentiation of the world is born from karma. That karma is volition and what is produced by it. Volition is mental action; the action born from it is verbal and bodily action.

## 7. Philosophical Translation

The manifold differentiation of the world is generated through karma. Karma consists both in volition and in the activity produced by volition. Volition is action in the mental domain; from it arise verbal and bodily action.

## 8. Technical Vocabulary

| Sanskrit | Working Translation | Determination |

| karma-ja | born from karma | generated through karmic activity |
| loka | world | field of beings and experienced existence |

### `loka-vaicitrya`

The compound does not indicate merely that many different things happen in the world. It indicates the differentiated constitution of the world itself:

```text
different destinies
different bodies
different environments
different capacities
different forms of experience
different results
```

### `cetanā`

The primary literal equivalent remains **volition**. It is not merely a wish or an explicit decision. It is the mental determination by which action is organized and directed.

A provisional philosophical formulation is:

```text
cetanā
    = cognition in its action-directing determination
```

The verse itself gives the stricter determination:

```text
cetanā
    = mental action
```

### `tat-kṛta`

This establishes the causal dependence of verbal and bodily action upon volition:

```text
cetanā
    → cetanā-kṛta
```

The verse begins with volition and derives manifest action from it. It does not begin with visible behavior and infer an intention behind it.

## 9. Doctrinal Determination

The first classification is causal:

```text
karma
├── cetanā
└── cetanā-kṛta
```

The second classification is by domain:

```text
karma
├── mental action
│   └── cetanā
├── verbal action
│   └── produced by cetanā
└── bodily action
    └── produced by cetanā
```

The threefold division is therefore not a flat list.

```text
mental karma
    is the determining activity

verbal and bodily karma
    are activities generated by that determination
```

The verse also joins Chapter 4 directly to the preceding chapter:

```text
Lokanirdeśa
    differentiated world

Karmanirdeśa
    activity that generates that differentiation
```

## 10. Logical Determination

The verse moves through four determinations:

```text
1. result
    loka-vaicitrya
    differentiated world

2. ground
    karma
    world-generating activity

3. inward determination
    cetanā
    mental action

4. produced expression
    vāk-kāya-karma
    verbal and bodily action
```

The expository order begins with the result and moves backward to its ground:

```text
differentiated world
    ← karma

karma
    = volition
    + what volition produces
```

The genetic order runs in the opposite direction:

```text
cetanā
    → verbal and bodily action
    → karmic efficacy
    → differentiated world
```

The remaining chapter must supply the mediations between manifest action and world-generating result.

## 11. Interpretive Note

The verse does not posit a substantial agent that first exists and subsequently possesses volition.

It says instead:

```text
volition itself is mental action
```

Thus active subjectivity is initially presented through a structure of activity rather than through a separately posited substance.

Karma is therefore more than an externally observable deed:

```text
karma
    begins as inward determining activity
    externalizes itself through speech and body
    generates differentiated actuality
```

## 12. Organon Interpretation

A provisional Organon determination is:

```text
karma
    = self-externalizing determination
```

This distinguishes a Theory of Karma from a merely static Theory of Forms:

```text
form
    says what a determination is

karma
    says how determination acts,
    expresses itself,
    persists,
    and generates actuality
```

The opening sequence is:

```text
cetanā
    inward volitional determination

vāk / kāya
    expressed activity

loka-vaicitrya
    world differentiated through karmic efficacy
```

VAK_4.02 must now determine the ontological status of bodily and verbal action through `vijñapti` and `avijñapti`.
