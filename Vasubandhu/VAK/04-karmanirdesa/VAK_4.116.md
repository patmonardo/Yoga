# VAK_4.116

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

```sanskrit
वर्णादिसम्पदा वस्तु सुरूपत्वं यशस्वि वा ।
प्रियता सुकुमारर्तुसुखस्पर्शाङ्गता ततः ॥ ४.११६ ॥
```

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

```text
varṇādisampadā vastu surūpatvaṃ yaśasvi vā |
priyatā sukumārartusukhasparśāṅgatā tataḥ || 4.116 ||
```

The electronic source reads `yaśasvi vā`. Its exact syntax should be checked against the critical edition and Bhāṣya.

## 3. Padaccheda

```text
varṇa-ādi-sampadā | vastu |
su-rūpatvam | yaśasvi | vā |
priyatā | su-kumāra | ṛtu-sukha-sparśa | aṅgatā | tataḥ
```

A cautious prose reconstruction is:

```text
vastu varṇādisampadā [viśiṣyate].
tataḥ surūpatvam, yaśasvitā vā, priyatā,
sukumārāṅgatā, ṛtusukhasparśāṅgatā [bhavati].
```

## 4. Grammar

- `varṇādisampadā`: instrumental singular, through excellence of color and the other sensible qualities.
- `vastu`: the object or substance given.
- `surūpatvam`: beauty or excellence of form.
- `yaśasvi`: famous, distinguished, possessing renown.
- `priyatā`: attractiveness, being pleasing.
- `sukumāra`: delicate, tender, refined.
- `ṛtusukhasparśa`: pleasant bodily contact adapted to the seasons.
- `aṅgatā`: the state of possessing bodily members of that kind.
- `tataḥ`: from that, consequently.

## 5. Literal Translation

> The gift-object is excellent through perfection of color and the other qualities. From this arise beauty of form, fame, attractiveness, delicacy, and bodily members whose touch is pleasant according to the seasons.

## 6. Philosophical Translation

> Giving is enhanced when the object offered is not defective, disagreeable, or carelessly chosen, but possesses excellent sensible qualities—good appearance and the other features that make it genuinely useful and pleasing. Such giving matures in a correspondingly excellent embodiment: beauty, favorable reputation, attractiveness to others, bodily delicacy, and a bodily constitution experienced as pleasantly adapted to changing conditions.

## 7. Technical Vocabulary

- `vastu-viśeṣa`: excellence of the gift-object.
- `varṇa-ādi`: color and the remaining sensible qualities.
- `sampad`: excellence, completeness, successful possession of good qualities.
- `surūpatva`: beauty of bodily form.
- `yaśas`: fame or favorable public recognition.
- `priyatā`: agreeableness or attractiveness.
- `sukumāra`: bodily delicacy or refinement.
- `ṛtu-sukha-sparśa`: pleasant tactile condition appropriate to seasonal variation.

## 8. Doctrinal / Logical Determination

VAK_4.114 established three determinants of giving: giver, gift-object, and recipient-field. VAK_4.116 develops the second. The gift-object is the objective middle through which the giver’s purpose becomes actual for the recipient.

The verse establishes a structural correspondence:

```text
excellent sensible object given
    →
excellent sensible embodiment received
```

The gift is not judged only by monetary quantity. A modest object may be karmically superior when it is clean, suitable, timely, and genuinely beneficial. The quality of the object preserves itself in the qualitative structure of maturation.

Possible correspondences are:

- excellent appearance → beautiful form;
- distinguished or admirable object → renown;
- pleasing object → attractiveness;
- soft or refined object → bodily delicacy;
- seasonally suitable object → pleasant bodily contact under changing conditions.

The exact syntax and pairing around `yaśasvi vā` remain provisional pending the Bhāṣya.

## 9. Organon Interpretation

The gift-object is the objective middle:

```text
giver-purpose
    →
appropriately formed object
    →
recipient-benefit
```

If the middle is defective, compassionate purpose remains abstract. The sensible form of the object is therefore the mode in which care becomes objectively available.

Karma then returns this objective form to the agent:

```text
form bestowed
    →
form embodied
```

The giver shapes another’s world through an excellent object, and that shaped world returns as the sensible quality of the giver’s future embodiment. Karma becomes visible as form.

- distinguish genuine beneficial suitability from mere material transfer;
