# VAK_4.31

## Source reference and caution

Source: `VAkK_4.31`.

The verse answers a difficulty generated by the preceding account of upāsaka restraint: if all upāsakas acquire restraint, why are some called practitioners of only one part, several parts, or the whole?

## Literal translation

> If all are restrained, how can there be those who practise one part and so forth? They are said to be such because of their guarding of those parts, just as mind is described as weak and so forth.

## Philosophical translation

> Although lay restraint is formally acquired as a whole, its bearers are distinguished according to the extent to which they preserve it in practice. “One-part practitioner,” “partial practitioner,” and “complete practitioner” designate degrees of actual realization, just as one and the same mind is characterized as weak, intermediate, or strong according to its operative intensity.

## Technical analysis

### Acquisition and observance

The verse distinguishes:

```text
saṃvara-lābha
    acquisition of restraint

saṃvara-pālana
    preservation or observance of restraint
```

Complete acquisition does not entail uniformly complete practical preservation.

### `tatpālanāt`

`pālana` means guarding, preserving, or maintaining. The undertaking institutes the form; avijñapti supplies persistence; smṛti and apramāda keep it practically present; pālana realizes it in continuing conduct.

### `mṛdvāditvaṃ yathā manaḥ`

Mind is called weak, intermediate, or strong according to the force of its operative condition. These names do not imply different substances. Likewise, one acquired restraint may be described as one-part, partial, predominantly complete, or complete according to actual preservation.

## Doctrinal determination

1. Upāsakas may acquire lay restraint as a whole.
2. They differ in the degree to which they preserve its limbs.
3. Their practical designation follows the degree of observance.
4. Imperfect observance does not automatically equal total absence of restraint.
5. Violation of a limb must be distinguished from abandonment of the standing restraint itself.

## Relation to kleśa

Ordinary restraint creates a real counter-determination while latent kleśa remains capable of opposing it:

```text
persistent restraint
    versus
persistent susceptibility
```

This explains why partial observance is possible without reducing the vow to fiction.

## Light Organon interpretation

A vow creates a universal practical form, but the individual must make that form actual through preservation of its differentiated limbs.

```text
status
    gives the person a determination

practice
    gives the determination actuality
```

The whole is genuinely acquired, yet it exists practically only through its limbs.
