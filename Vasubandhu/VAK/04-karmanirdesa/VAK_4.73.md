# VAK_4d — VAkK_4.73

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

प्राणातिपातः संचिन्त्य परस्याभ्रान्तिमारणम् ।
अदत्तादानमन्यस्वस्वीकृया बलचौर्यतः ॥ ४.७३ ॥

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

prāṇātipātaḥ saṃcintya parasyābhrānti-māraṇam /
adattādānam anya-sva-svīkriyā bala-cauryataḥ // 4.73 //

Source text:

prāṇātipātaḥ saṃcintya parasyābhrāntimāraṇam /
adattādānamanyasvasvīkriyā balacauryataḥ //

This verse begins the formal definitions of the bodily karmapathas: destruction of life and taking what is not given.

## 4. Grammar

- `prāṇātipātaḥ`: masculine nominative singular compound, “destruction of life.”
- `saṃcintya`: absolutive from `sam-√cint`, “having considered,” hence “deliberately.”
- `parasya`: genitive singular, “of another.”
- `abhrānti-māraṇam`: neuter nominative singular, “non-mistaken killing.”
- `adattādānam`: neuter nominative singular, “taking what is not given.”
- `anya-sva`: “another’s property.”
- `svīkriyā`: feminine nominative singular, “making one’s own,” appropriation.
- `bala-cauryataḥ`: ablatival expression, “through force or stealth.”

`saṃcintya` establishes prior intentional determination. `abhrānti` establishes the relevant continuity between intended and actual object. `svīkriyā` shows that theft is not mere displacement but practical appropriation.

## 5. Literal Translation

Destruction of life is the deliberate, non-mistaken killing of another. Taking what is not given is the appropriation of another’s property by force or theft.

## 6. Philosophical Translation

Killing becomes the completed course of destroying life only where the agent deliberately directs lethal action toward another living being and the intended object is not confused with another. Theft is not merely the displacement of an object; it is the practical appropriation of what belongs to another, whether by open force or concealed seizure.

Constitutive structures:

living other + deliberate intention + correct object-identification + death = completed destruction of life

another’s possession + absence of giving + appropriative taking + force or stealth = completed taking of what is not given

## 7. Technical Vocabulary

### prāṇa

Life or living vitality. In `prāṇātipāta`, it designates the destruction of a living being, not physiological breath alone.

### atipāta

Striking down, overthrow, or destruction. The karmapatha reaches completion when life is destroyed.

### saṃcintya

Deliberately, after intentional consideration. This excludes purely accidental death from the completed karmapatha and distinguishes causal production of death from intentional killing.

### bhrānti

Error, confusion, or mistaken identification. `Abhrānti` means that the intended and actual object are correctly connected.

### māraṇa

Causing death or killing. The act is completed at the death-event, as clarified in 4.72.

### adatta

Not given: no legitimate transfer or permission has occurred.

### ādāna

Taking, acquisition, or seizure.

### sva / anya-sva

`Sva` means one’s own or property; `anya-sva` is what belongs to another.

### svīkriyā

Making one’s own, appropriation. The object is taken under the practical determination “this is now mine.”

### bala / caurya

`Bala` is force or coercive power; `caurya` is theft, stealth, or concealed appropriation. Open seizure and covert taking instantiate the same karmapatha.

## 8. Doctrinal Determination

The earlier verses analyzed roots, phases, completion, objective bases, and collective agency. This verse now asks what conditions make a bodily act this particular karmapatha.

### Destruction of life

Provisional constitutive conditions:

1. A living being exists.
2. The agent recognizes another living being.
3. The agent forms a lethal intention.
4. Action is directed toward that being.
5. No relevant mistake interrupts the object-relation.
6. The being dies.

Thus intention alone is not completed killing; attack alone is not completed killing; death alone is not necessarily karmic killing. Deliberate, correctly directed causing of death is `prāṇātipāta`.

### Taking what is not given

Provisional constitutive conditions:

1. An object belongs to another.
2. It has not been given.
3. The agent recognizes it as another’s.
4. The agent appropriates it as their own.
5. Appropriation occurs by force or stealth.

The center is `svīkriyā`: another’s property becomes practically posited as mine.

From VAK_4.70, taking what is not given is completed through greed because the act’s formal structure is appropriation.

## 9. Logical Determination

The verse gives two relational violations.

### Killing: negation of the living other

The agent recognizes a living other, intends destruction, and successfully negates life. The formal structure is intentional negation mediated by correct object-reference, not mere physical causation.

### Theft: negation of another’s ownership

The object belongs to another; the agent practically cancels that relation and posits the object as mine. Theft is therefore an illegitimate relational transformation.

Both definitions require correct relation to an object. The same bodily motion may have different moral identity depending on intention, object-recognition, ownership relation, and result.

## 10. Light Organon Interpretation

VAK_4.73 requires constitutive-condition checking rather than simple event labeling.

A moral processor should represent killing as requiring:

- living target
- lethal intention
- correct target-linkage
- causal execution
- death result

Taking what is not given requires:

- prior ownership by another
- absence of permission
- knowledge of that relation
- appropriative intention
- acquisition by force or stealth

General form:

Agent actsWithIntention upon ObjectiveBasis under a RecognitionState, producing a CompletionEvent.

Two failure patterns emerge:

- `prāṇātipāta`: wrongful negation of life
- `adattādāna`: wrongful appropriation of ownership

The inclusion of `abhrānti` is especially important: the processor must distinguish intended target, actual target, and recognized target.
