# VAK_4.113

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

दीयते येन तद्दानं पूजानुग्रहकाम्यया ।
कायवाक्कर्म सोत्थानं महाभोग्यफलं च तत् ॥ ४.११३ ॥

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

dīyate yena tad dānaṃ pūjānugrahakāmyayā |
kāyavākkarma sotthānaṃ mahābhogyaphalaṃ ca tat || 4.113 ||

This verse defines the first of the three `puṇyakriyāvastūni` introduced in VAK_4.112:

`dāna` — the basis of meritorious activity consisting in giving.

## 3. Padaccheda

`dīyate | yena | tat | dānam | pūjā-anugraha-kāmyayā | kāya-vāk-karma | sa-utthānam | mahā-bhogya-phalam | ca | tat`

Prose arrangement:

`pūjānugrahakāmyayā yena dīyate, tad dānam. tat kāyavākkarma sotthānam, mahābhogyaphalaṃ ca.`

That through which something is given, from the desire to honor or benefit, is giving. It is bodily and verbal karma together with that which sets it in motion, and it has great enjoyment as its fruit.

## 4. Grammar

- `dīyate`: present passive, third singular — is given.
- `yena`: instrumental relative pronoun — by which, through which.
- `tat`: correlative pronoun — that.
- `dānam`: nominative neuter singular — giving.
- `pūjā`: honor, worship, reverence.
- `anugraha`: benefit, assistance, favor.
- `kāmyayā`: instrumental feminine singular — through the desire for.
- `kāya-vāk-karma`: bodily and verbal karma.
- `sa-utthānam`: together with its activation or motivating arising.
- `mahā-bhogya-phalam`: having great enjoyment as its fruit.

The relative–correlative construction is:

`yena dīyate tad dānam`

That by means of which giving occurs is giving.

This distinguishes the karmic act of giving from the material object transferred.

## 5. Literal translation

That by which something is given, through the desire to honor or to benefit, is giving. It is bodily and verbal karma together with its motivating activation, and it has great enjoyment as its fruit.

## 6. Philosophical translation

Giving is not fundamentally the object handed over but the karmic activity through which transfer is effected, motivated either by reverence for an excellent field or by the wish to benefit a recipient. It consists in bodily or verbal activity together with the volitional impulse that sets that activity in motion, and its maturation yields abundant resources and enjoyment.

## 7. `Dīyate yena tad dānam`

The verse does not define `dāna` as the thing given. It defines it as that through which the thing is given.

The food or other object is `deya`, the object to be given. The giving is `dāna`, the karmically qualified activity through which ownership or availability is relinquished.

Thus:

`gift-object ≠ giving-karma`

The external object is the material basis; the giving is the activity that determines its transfer.

## 8. Giving as effective relinquishment

The passive `dīyate` emphasizes that something actually becomes given. A private intention to give in the future is not yet the completed giving described here.

The activity must effect a relation:

`possessor relinquishes object → recipient gains access to object`

Thus giving is not merely inward benevolence. It is volition becoming effective through body or speech.

## 9. `Pūjā`

`pūjā` means honor, reverence, or worship.

One gives for the sake of honoring an excellent field such as Buddha, Dharma, Saṃgha, teacher, parent, arhat, or stūpa.

In such giving, the recipient is apprehended primarily through excellence:

`quality of field → reverential giving`

This connects directly with the earlier discussion of `guṇakṣetra`, the field of qualities.

## 10. `Anugraha`

`anugraha` means assistance, support, benefit, or favor.

One may give because the recipient is in need. The gift may relieve hunger, illness, poverty, danger, lack of shelter, or lack of means for practice.

Thus the two principal intentions are:

- `pūjā`: honoring excellence.
- `anugraha`: relieving need.

## 11. Two recipient-relations

The compound distinguishes two ways in which the recipient becomes a meritorious field:

- `pūjā`: reverence toward superior qualities.
- `anugraha`: compassionate benefit toward need.

The direction differs: honor rises toward excellence, whereas assistance descends toward need. Both become giving because both culminate in relinquishment for the recipient’s sake.

## 12. `Kāmyayā`

The instrumental indicates the motive: because of the desire to honor or benefit.

This is purposive intention rather than necessarily afflictive craving. The recipient-related end determines the transfer.

The same external movement of an object may not constitute meritorious giving when motivated by fear, coercion, deception, commercial exchange, domination, or disposal of an unwanted burden.

## 13. `Kāyavākkarma`

Giving may be completed bodily by handing over an object, delivering food, constructing lodging, or physically transferring property.

It may also be completed verbally by declaring that an object belongs to the recipient, authorizing access, promising and legally effecting transfer, or instructing another to deliver it.

The external form varies, but the intention must become communicatively and practically effective.

## 14. Why verbal karma can constitute giving

Speech can alter the normative relation to an object:

`verbal determination → change of entitlement → gift completed`

Thus verbal giving is performative karmic activity rather than merely a description of bodily action.

## 15. `Sotthānam`

Sandhi:

`sa-utthānam → sotthānam`

`Utthāna` suggests rising up, activation, or setting into motion. The likely doctrinal sense is the motivating activity that gives rise to the bodily or verbal deed.

Giving therefore includes external bodily or verbal karma together with the volitional activation producing it.

The exact Abhidharma referent should be checked in the Bhāṣya pass.

## 16. Giving is not body or speech alone

The verse avoids two reductions:

- intention alone does not complete a gift;
- external transfer alone lacks the same karmic determination if accidental or compelled.

Complete giving requires:

`recipient-oriented volition + activation + bodily or verbal execution + effective relinquishment`

Thus `dāna` is a coordinated activity-complex.

## 17. `Mahābhogyaphala`

`mahā-bhogya-phala` means a result consisting in abundant enjoyment or resources.

The causal correspondence is:

`making resources available to others → future availability of resources to oneself`

Karma reproduces the relational form of the deed.

## 18. `Bhoga` is not liberation

Great enjoyment remains a worldly fruit, not noble Path-fruit.

Giving can produce excellent worldly conditions and those conditions may support practice, but they do not by themselves eliminate affliction.

Merit improves the field of becoming; wisdom is required to end becoming.

## 19. Relation to VAK_4.112

VAK_4.112 distinguished `puṇyakriyā`, meritorious activity, from its `vastu`, determinate basis.

VAK_4.113 begins unpacking the giving basis through motive, activation, execution, effective relinquishment, and maturation.

Thus a `puṇyakriyāvastu` is an organized practical relation rather than one isolated element.

## 20. Giving and karmic field

The karmic magnitude of giving depends not solely upon the material quantity transferred, but also upon giver, intention, gift-object, recipient-field, and manner of transfer.

VAK_4.114 will develop these differentiations explicitly.

## 21. Doctrinal determination

The verse establishes:

1. giving is the karmic activity through which an object becomes given;
2. the object given is not identical with the act of giving;
3. giving is motivated by reverence or the wish to benefit;
4. it is effected through bodily or verbal karma;
5. bodily and verbal execution are accompanied by an activating volitional basis;
6. its characteristic maturation is abundant enjoyment and resources.

## 22. Logical determination

A minimal rule is:

`IntendsToHonorOrBenefit(a, r) ∧ Relinquishes(a, o) ∧ MakesAvailableTo(o, r) → GivingKarma(a)`

The full structure is:

`Giving { giver, recipient, giftObject, motive, activation, bodilyOrVerbalExecution, effectiveTransfer }`

And:

`GivingKarma(a) → tendsToProduce(a, AbundantEnjoyment)`

## 23. Organon interpretation

Giving is the middle between subjective purpose and objective relation.

The giver’s end—honor or benefit—activates body or speech. The action then changes the objective distribution of a resource: what was privately possessed becomes available to another. Karma preserves the relational form of that deed and returns it as a future capacity for enjoyment.

The circuit is:

`recipient-oriented purpose → activated bodily/verbal performance → objective transfer → future resource-field`

Giving transforms private possession into recipient-access.
