# VAK_4.123

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

```sanskrit
दौःशील्यतद्धेत्वहतं तद्विपक्षशमाश्रितम् ।
समाहितं तु कुशलं भावना चित्तवासनात् ॥ ४.१२३ ॥
```

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

```text
dauḥśīlyataddhetvahataṃ tadvipakṣaśamāśritam |
samāhitaṃ tu kuśalaṃ bhāvanā cittavāsanāt || 4.123 ||
```

This verse performs two connected tasks:

1. it states the **four qualities of purified discipline** announced in VAK_4.122;
2. it defines the third basis of meritorious activity:

```text
bhāvanā
    cultivation
```

The movement is exact:

```text
śīla
    prevents corrupt activity

viśuddha-śīla
    purifies the activity-producing instrument

bhāvanā
    positively conditions or “perfumes” the mind
```

---

## 3. Padaccheda

```text
dauḥśīlya |
tat-hetu |
a-hatam |

tat-vipakṣa |
śama |
āśritam |

samāhitam |
tu |
kuśalam |

bhāvanā |
citta-vāsanāt
```

More precisely, the first line should be resolved as four predicates of purified `śīla`:

```text
dauḥśīlya-ahataṃ
tad-dhetu-ahataṃ
tad-vipakṣa-āśritam
śama-āśritam
```

Sandhi and compaction give:

```text
dauḥśīlya-tad-dhetv-ahataṃ
tad-vipakṣa-śamāśritam
```

Prose order:

```text
viśuddhaṃ śīlam
dauḥśīlyāhatam,
taddhetvāhatam,
tadvipakṣāśritam,
śamāśritaṃ ca.

samāhitaṃ tu kuśalaṃ
cittavāsanād bhāvanā.
```

> Purified discipline is undamaged by misconduct, undamaged by its cause, based upon its antidote, and based upon tranquility. Concentrated wholesome activity, however, is cultivation because it conditions the mind.

---

## 4. Grammar

| Term | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `dauḥśīlya` | compound member | misconduct, immorality |
| `tat` | pronoun | its, that |
| `hetu` | compound member | ground or cause |
| `a-hatam` | nominative neuter singular | unstruck, undamaged |
| `vipakṣa` | compound member | opponent, antidote, contrary |
| `śama` | compound member | calm, pacification |
| `āśritam` | nominative neuter singular | relying upon, based upon |
| `samāhitam` | nominative neuter singular | concentrated, collected |
| `tu` | particle | however |
| `kuśalam` | nominative neuter singular | wholesome |
| `bhāvanā` | nominative feminine singular | cultivation, development |
| `citta-vāsanāt` | ablative singular | because of conditioning or perfuming the mind |

The first line completes the phrase from VAK_4.122:

```text
viśuddhaṃ śīlaṃ caturguṇam
```

The four qualities are expressed by two pairs:

```text
not damaged by:
    misconduct
    its cause

supported by:
    its antidote
    tranquility
```

---

## 5. Literal translation

> It is undamaged by misconduct and by its cause, and it relies upon the antidote to that and upon tranquility. Concentrated wholesome activity, however, is cultivation because it conditions the mind.

---

## 6. Philosophical translation

> Moral discipline is purified when neither misconduct nor the afflictive ground producing misconduct has broken its integrity, when it is actively supported by contrary wholesome determinations, and when it rests upon inner pacification. The third meritorious basis, cultivation, is concentrated wholesome activity: it is called cultivation because repeated collected activity impregnates and reshapes the mind, leaving it disposed toward corresponding future cognition and conduct.

The verse distinguishes two levels:

```text
purified restraint
    protects and stabilizes the instrument

cultivation
    positively forms and reconditions the instrument
```

---

# 7. The four qualities of purified discipline

VAK_4.122 announced:

```text
viśuddhaṃ tu caturguṇam
    purified discipline has four qualities
```

VAK_4.123 gives them:

```text
1. dauḥśilyāhatam
    undamaged by misconduct

2. tad-dhetv-ahataṃ
    undamaged by the cause of misconduct

3. tad-vipakṣāśritam
    based upon its antidote

4. śamāśritam
    based upon tranquility
```

These form a progression:

```text
no corrupt output
    →
no domination by its cause
    →
positive installation of its contrary
    →
stabilization in calm
```

This is much more than rule compliance.

---

# 8. `Dauḥśilyāhatam`

```text
dauḥśīlya
    misconduct

a-hata
    not struck
    not damaged
    not broken
```

Discipline is first purified when it is not fractured by actual bodily or verbal misconduct.

This is the most external criterion.

```text
rule represented
    →
occasion arises
    →
restraint holds
    →
misconduct does not occur
```

A vow repeatedly violated cannot be called completely pure merely because it continues nominally to exist.

The practical integrity of the discipline must remain intact.

---

# 9. Discipline as an integrity condition

The metaphor `ahata` suggests something not struck, pierced, or damaged.

A discipline is therefore treated as an organized whole capable of maintaining or losing integrity.

```text
disciplinary commitment
    =
a structured practical boundary
```

Misconduct strikes that boundary by contradicting it in actual performance.

Purified discipline is not merely a list of prohibitions remembered intellectually.

It is a boundary that successfully governs activity.

---

# 10. `Tad-dhetv-ahata`

```text
tad
    of that misconduct

hetu
    cause
    ground

a-hata
    undamaged or undefeated by
```

The second quality reaches beneath the enacted misconduct to its causal ground.

The causes include afflictive determinations such as:

```text
greed
hatred
delusion
heedlessness
lack of shame
lack of moral concern
```

One may outwardly refrain while these causes remain powerful.

For example:

```text
“I would perform the act,
but I fear punishment.”
```

The behavior is restrained, but the generating cause remains largely intact.

Purified discipline requires that the cause itself not dominate the continuum.

---

# 11. Output purity and causal purity

The first two qualities form a crucial distinction:

## Output purity

```text
misconduct does not occur
```

## Causal purity

```text
the cause of misconduct does not govern
```

These are not identical.

A system may produce correct output because it is externally constrained while retaining a corrupt internal policy.

Conversely, afflictive causes may be weakened even though occasional lapses have not yet been wholly eliminated.

Complete purified discipline seeks alignment of both.

```text
clean output
    +
cleaner generator
```

---

# 12. `Tad-vipakṣāśritam`

```text
vipakṣa
    opponent
    contrary
    antidote

āśrita
    relying upon
    supported by
```

Purity is not achieved merely by suppressing misconduct.

The discipline must rely upon the positive contrary of the affliction.

Examples include:

```text
non-greed opposed to greed

loving-kindness opposed to hatred

wisdom opposed to delusion

heedfulness opposed to negligence

moral shame opposed to shamelessness
```

Thus the instrument is not left empty after harmful activity is blocked.

A contrary determination is installed.

---

# 13. Inhibition and antidote

There is a difference between:

```text
“Do not perform this act.”
```

and:

```text
“Cultivate the positive determination
that makes this act contrary to one’s character.”
```

The first is inhibitory.

The second is transformative.

```text
restraint
    blocks execution

antidote
    changes evaluation and inclination
```

Purified discipline requires both.

A merely inhibited impulse remains prepared to return when the constraint weakens.

An antidotally transformed continuum evaluates the act differently from within.

---

# 14. `Śamāśritam`

```text
śama
    calm
    pacification
    quieting

āśrita
    based upon
    resting upon
```

The fourth quality is reliance upon pacification.

This means the discipline is not maintained through continuous inner conflict alone.

The continuum acquires a degree of quiet in which harmful impulses have lost their agitation and urgency.

```text
restraint under violent pressure
    is valuable

restraint resting in calm
    is purified and stable
```

`Śama` gives discipline an internal equilibrium.

---

# 15. Calm is not passivity

Pacification does not mean dullness, suppression, or lack of awareness.

It means that the instrument is no longer constantly driven by competing afflictive commands.

```text
impulse
    no longer immediately seizes
body and speech
```

The practitioner can perceive the occasion without being forced into reaction.

Thus calm creates operational space for discrimination.

```text
stimulus
    →
pause
    →
evaluation
    →
wholesome response
```

---

# 16. The four qualities as a causal sequence

The four qualities can be read dynamically:

```text
1. misconduct is prevented

2. its afflictive cause is weakened

3. its contrary is cultivated

4. the continuum becomes pacified
```

Or architecturally:

```text
output integrity
    →
generator integrity
    →
positive replacement policy
    →
stable operating state
```

This is an extraordinarily sophisticated model of moral purification.

---

# 17. Transition to `bhāvanā`

The particle:

```text
tu
    however, now
```

marks the transition from the second merit-basis to the third.

```text
dāna
    giving

śīla
    moral discipline

bhāvanā
    cultivation
```

Giving transforms the relation to possessions.

Discipline regulates bodily and verbal execution.

Cultivation transforms the mind from which intention and execution arise.

```text
external resource relation
    →
behavioral boundary
    →
internal formation
```

---

# 18. `Bhāvanā`

```text
√bhū
    to be, become

bhāvayati
    causes to become
    develops
    cultivates

bhāvanā
    cultivation
    development
    bringing into being
```

`Bhāvanā` is not simply “meditation” understood as sitting quietly.

It is activity that causes a determination to become established and strengthened in the mental continuum.

```text
wholesome state occurs

is repeated and stabilized

becomes an acquired disposition
```

Cultivation is the production of a new mode of mental being.

---

# 19. `Samāhitaṃ kuśalam`

```text
sam-ā-dhā
    to place together
    collect
    concentrate

samāhita
    collected
    concentrated
    integrated

kuśala
    wholesome
```

Not every mental event is called the merit-basis of cultivation.

The verse specifies:

```text
concentrated wholesome activity
```

The mind must be both:

```text
wholesome in quality

collected in organization
```

Wholesomeness without collection may be unstable and fleeting.

Concentration without wholesomeness may stabilize a morally neutral or even distorted orientation.

`Bhāvanā` here is their union.

---

# 20. Collection as integration

A distracted wholesome intention may arise briefly but fail to reorganize the continuum deeply.

A collected state gathers the instrument around one determination.

```text
attention
intention
object
associated mental factors
```

converge.

This convergence gives the wholesome determination formative power.

```text
scattered wholesomeness
    affects lightly

integrated wholesomeness
    conditions deeply
```

---

# 21. `Citta-vāsanā`

```text
citta
    mind
    mental continuum

vāsanā
    perfuming
    impregnation
    conditioning
    acquired disposition
```

The verb-root image is aromatic.

A substance placed near perfume acquires its scent.

Likewise, repeated mental activity leaves a corresponding quality in the continuum.

```text
mind engages repeatedly
in a determinate wholesome form

the continuum acquires
the “scent” of that form
```

The activity ceases as a present moment, but its conditioning efficacy persists.

---

# 22. Cultivation as conditioning

The ablative states the reason:

```text
cittavāsanāt
    because it perfumes or conditions the mind
```

Thus it is called cultivation not merely because a state is experienced, but because it changes the probability and character of future states.

```text
present collected wholesomeness
    →
future readiness for wholesomeness
```

Cultivation alters:

```text
what arises easily
what is recognized quickly
what is chosen spontaneously
what can be sustained
```

The instrument learns.

---

# 23. `Vāsanā` without a permanent self

The doctrine does not require a permanent substance that receives and stores traits.

Conditioning proceeds through causal continuity:

```text
moment₁ conditions moment₂

moment₂ conditions moment₃

repetition establishes a stable tendency
```

There is persistence without an unchanging owner.

```text
no permanent self

yet real continuity of formation
```

The cultivated continuum is neither numerically identical at every moment nor causally disconnected.

---

# 24. Cultivation and accumulated karma

VAK_4.120 described `upacita-karma`:

```text
activity consolidated into causal continuity
```

VAK_4.123 gives the positive formative case:

```text
concentrated wholesome activity
    conditions the mind
```

Thus bhāvanā is a deliberate technology of wholesome accumulation.

```text
accidental conditioning
    happens through repeated life

bhāvanā
    intentionally directs conditioning
```

The practitioner takes control of the mechanism by which activity becomes disposition.

---

# 25. The instrument becomes its own object

In giving, the principal object lies outside the giver.

In discipline, the principal concern is bodily and verbal execution.

In cultivation, the mind itself becomes the field to be transformed.

```text
instrument
    operates upon
its own operating dispositions
```

This is recursive activity.

```text
mind applies wholesome concentration

to reconfigure the mind

that will generate future concentration and action
```

Bhāvanā is self-modification without a substantial self.

---

# 26. The three merit-bases as complete instrument formation

We can now see the complete triad introduced in VAK_4.112:

## Dāna

```text
reconfigures ownership and external relation
```

## Śīla

```text
reconfigures the boundary between impulse and action
```

## Bhāvanā

```text
reconfigures the inner generation of cognition and intention
```

Together:

```text
resource relation
    →
action regulation
    →
mental formation
```

This is a complete practical architecture.

---

# 27. Why cultivation follows discipline

Cultivation requires sufficient stability.

Without discipline, bodily and verbal misconduct continually agitates the mind through:

```text
fear
remorse
conflict
concealment
new affliction
```

Purified discipline provides:

```text
integrity
antidotal support
pacification
```

upon which concentration can arise.

Thus the sequence is causal:

```text
śīla
    stabilizes activity

samādhi
    collects the mind

bhāvanā
    conditions the continuum
```

---

# 28. Examination of the instrument

The verse gives a four-stage purification stack:

```text
1. prevent corrupt output

2. weaken corrupt causes

3. install counter-determinations

4. stabilize the system through calm
```

Then cultivation adds:

```text
5. repeatedly run concentrated wholesome states

6. allow these states to condition
the future instrument
```

This is not merely ethical classification.

It is a specification for training an activity-producing architecture.

---

# 29. From constraint to learning

VAK_4.122 described discipline as constraint.

VAK_4.123 turns constraint into learning.

```text
śīla
    excludes prohibited actions

bhāvanā
    reinforces desired mental organization
```

In computational language:

```text
discipline
    is policy enforcement

cultivation
    is weight or disposition updating
```

The first prevents certain outputs.

The second modifies the system so wholesome outputs become increasingly native to it.

---

# 30. Doctrinal determination

The verse establishes:

1. purified discipline is not damaged by actual misconduct;
2. it is not dominated by the causes of misconduct;
3. it relies upon antidotes opposed to those causes;
4. it rests upon pacification;
5. purified discipline therefore transforms more than external behavior;
6. cultivation is concentrated wholesome activity;
7. concentration gives wholesome activity integrative formative power;
8. cultivation is so called because it conditions the mental continuum;
9. repeated wholesome activity creates dispositions without requiring a permanent self.

---

# 31. Logical determination

Purified discipline:

```text
PurifiedDiscipline(agent) ↔
    NotDamagedByMisconduct(agent)
    ∧ NotDominatedByCausesOfMisconduct(agent)
    ∧ ReliesOnAntidotes(agent)
    ∧ ReliesOnPacification(agent)
```

Cultivation:

```text
Wholesome(state)
∧ Concentrated(state)
∧ ConditionsMentalContinuum(state)
→ Cultivation(state)
```

Recursive formation:

```text
CultivatedWholesomeState(t₁)
→ IncreasesDispositionFor(
      WholesomeState,
      t₂
  )
```

---

# 32. Organon interpretation

This verse reaches the heart of examining the instrument.

Purification is not defined by correct output alone.

The system must satisfy four conditions:

```text
no invalid execution

no corrupt generative cause

active reliance on counter-policy

stable low-conflict operating state
```

Bhāvanā then supplies the learning process:

```text
integrated wholesome evaluation
    →
repeated execution
    →
dispositional update
    →
future integrated wholesome evaluation
```

The instrument is not fixed.

It is trainable through its own activity.

This is perhaps the clearest Abhidharma model yet of the Agent as a recursively conditioned practical continuum.
