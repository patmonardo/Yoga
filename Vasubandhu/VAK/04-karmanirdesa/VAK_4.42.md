# VAkK 4.42

## 1. Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

> कामाप्तं कुशलारूपं मूलच्छेदोर्ध्वजन्मतः ।  
> प्रतिपक्षोदयात् क्लिष्टमरूपं तु विहीयते ॥ ४.४२ ॥

### IAST

> **kāmāptaṃ kuśalārūpaṃ mūlacchedordhvajanmataḥ |**  
> **pratipakṣodayāt kliṣṭam arūpaṃ tu vihīyate || 4.42 ||**

The project source reads:

```text
kāmāptaṃ kuśalārūpaṃ mūlacchedordhvajanmataḥ /
pratipakṣodayāt kliṣṭamarūpaṃ tu vihīyate // VAkK_4.42 //
```

The verse completes the present account of abandonment by treating two further classes of **arūpa**, nonmaterial dharma:

```text
wholesome nonmaterial form
    belonging to the desire realm

defiled nonmaterial form
```

Here **arūpa** does not mean the formless realm. It means **nonmaterial**, and in this context points to avijñapti or comparable nonmaterial karmic form.

## 4. arūpa here means nonmaterial

The term must be distinguished from **ārūpya**, the formless domain or its attainments.

```text
ārūpya
    the formless domain
    or formless meditative attainment

arūpa
    not material
    lacking visible material form
```

The present subject is therefore not:

> wholesome dharmas of the formless realm.

It is:

> wholesome and defiled nonmaterial karmic forms.

This fits the whole section on **avijñapti**, which is classified as rūpa in the Sarvāstivāda system yet is not perceptible material form. The kārikā’s compressed **arūpa** requires Bhāṣya confirmation regarding its exact referential range, but it clearly contrasts nonmanifest form with visible bodily and verbal manifestation.

---

## 5. kāmāptaṃ kuśalārūpam

> **Wholesome nonmaterial form belonging to the desire realm**

This most naturally includes wholesome avijñapti belonging to the desire realm that is not already exhausted by the preceding specific classifications.

It may be generated through:

```text
giving
worship
construction of a religious support
a limited wholesome undertaking
earnest beneficial activity
```

without necessarily constituting a lifelong prātimokṣa vow.

It is:

```text
kuśala
    wholesome

arūpa
    nonmanifest or nonmaterial

kāmāpta
    belonging to the desire-realm continuum
```

Its conditions of loss follow from its realm and its wholesome causal root.

---

## 6. mūlaccheda — severance of the root

> **It is lost through severance of the root.**

For a wholesome nonmaterial dharma, the relevant roots are the wholesome roots:

```text
alobha
    non-greed

adveṣa
    non-hatred

amoha
    non-delusion
```

A wholesome avijñapti depends upon the continuum’s capacity for wholesome determination.

```text
wholesome root operative
    → wholesome nonmaterial form supported

wholesome root severed
    → wholesome form no longer supported
```

This is not an ordinary lapse.

It concerns a radical destruction of the causal basis through which wholesome dharmas can continue to be possessed.

---

## 7. Root-severance is deeper than contrary action

The person may commit an unwholesome act while wholesome dharmas remain present elsewhere in the continuum.

VAkK 4.39 already showed that opposed karmic records can coexist.

```text
wholesome restraint
    +
grave unwholesome offense
```

does not necessarily mean:

```text
all wholesome form destroyed
```

But **mūlaccheda** is different.

```text
contrary action
    adds an opposed karmic determination

root-severance
    destroys the generative support
    of a class of wholesome determinations
```

The verse therefore distinguishes contradiction within experience from destruction of a causal capacity.

---

## 8. ūrdhvajanmataḥ — through birth above

> **[It is also lost] through birth in a higher domain.**

## ūrdhva

```text
ūrdhva
    above
    higher
```

## janman

```text
janman
    birth
    mode of arising into a realm
```

The wholesome nonmaterial form in question belongs specifically to the desire realm.

When the being is reborn above the desire realm—into the form realm or formless realm—the desire-realm form is no longer possessed.

```text
desire-realm support
    → desire-realm wholesome avijñapti

birth in higher realm
    → former realm-support terminated
    → desire-realm avijñapti relinquished
```

This is loss through **realm transition**.

---

## 9. A dharma is indexed to its domain

The principle parallels VAkK 4.40:

```text
dhyāna-acquired dharma
    indexed to meditative bhūmi

desire-realm wholesome arūpa
    indexed to kāmadhātu
```

A change of domain changes the set of dharmas that can be presently possessed.

```text
DHARMA
    type = wholesome nonmaterial
    realm-index = desire
```

When:

```text
realm-index changes
```

the old realm-bound possession terminates.

This does not mean its past karmic efficacy becomes nonexistent. Rather:

```text
present possession
    ceases

historical karma and maturation-capacity
    must be separately determined
```

---

## 10. Birth is an ontological reconfiguration

Birth into a realm is not mere relocation.

As discussed earlier, a **dhātu** is a domain of conditioning.

```text
birth in kāmadhātu
    → one configuration of body,
      cognition, kleśa, and karma

birth above kāmadhātu
    → another configuration
```

Thus **ūrdhvajarman** changes the experiential architecture of the continuum.

A desire-realm-specific dharma can cease because the domain in which it was operable is no longer the present domain.

This confirms the earlier insight:

> one is born into a dhātu together with its determinate field of conditioning.

The reverse is now explicit: departure from that dhātu also terminates determinations indexed specifically to it.

## 11. kliṣṭam arūpam

> **Defiled nonmaterial form**

## kliṣṭa

From √*kliś*:

```text
kliṣṭa
    afflicted
    defiled
    affected by kleśa
```

A **kliṣṭa arūpa** is a nonmaterial determination carrying an afflicted character.

It may be:

```text
an unwholesome avijñapti

a persistent nonmanifest form
generated under kleśic motivation

a causal disposition belonging
to the intermediate category
```

The exact class should remain cautious until the Bhāṣya, but its defining feature is clear:

```text
its persistence is supported by afflictive causality
```

## 12. pratipakṣodaya — arising of the counteragent

> **It is abandoned through the arising of its counteragent.**

## pratipakṣa

```text
prati
    against, opposite to

pakṣa
    side, position, factor

pratipakṣa
    opposing factor
    antidote
    counteragent
```

## udaya

```text
udaya
    arising
    emergence
    becoming operative
```

The defiled nonmaterial dharma is lost when a dharma specifically opposed to it becomes actual.

```text
defiled form D
    presently operative

counteragent C
    arises

C is incompatible with D
    → D is abandoned
```

This is stronger than merely declining to act upon the defilement.

The counteragent directly opposes the causal form.

---

## 13. Counteragent is not mere external obstruction

A harmful action can fail because circumstances prevent it.

```text
desire to act
    remains

opportunity absent
    → action does not occur
```

That is not **pratipakṣa** in the strong technical sense.

A counteragent is an internally opposed cognitive or ethical determination:

```text
non-greed
    counteracts greed

non-hatred
    counteracts hatred

correct understanding
    counteracts delusion

meditative attainment
    counteracts a lower afflictive operation

noble Path
    counteracts and abandons
    determinate kleśas
```

Thus:

```text
obstruction
    prevents manifestation

counteragent
    transforms the causal relation
```

---

## 14. The technical importance of pratipakṣa

This term leads directly toward Chapter 5 and Chapter 6.

A kleśa is not abandoned merely because:

```text
it is currently absent

it has no object available

a stronger distraction suppresses it

the person vows not to act upon it
```

Abandonment requires the relevant opposing factor.

```text
kliṣṭa dharma
    ↔ determinate pratipakṣa
```

The system must therefore record not only:

```text
what is defiled?
```

but:

```text
what precisely counteracts it?
at what stage?
with what degree of efficacy?
does it suppress or abandon?
```

This is the beginning of a formal theory of antidotal cognition.

## 15. Literal translation

> **Wholesome nonmaterial form belonging to the desire realm is abandoned through severance of the roots or through birth above. Defiled nonmaterial form, however, is abandoned through the arising of its counteragent.**

---

## 16. Philosophical translation

> **A wholesome nonmanifest determination belonging specifically to desire-realm existence ceases when its wholesome causal roots are destroyed or when the continuum is reborn into a higher domain incompatible with that realm-specific possession. A defiled nonmanifest determination ceases differently: it is abandoned when the cognitive or ethical factor specifically opposed to it becomes actual.**

---

## 17. Three different logics of loss

VAkK 4.42 distinguishes three modes:

## Destruction of causal capacity

```text
mūlaccheda
    supporting wholesome root destroyed
```

## Transition of experiential domain

```text
ūrdhvajarman
    birth in a higher realm
```

## Active antidotal opposition

```text
pratipakṣodaya
    counteragent arises
```

These correspond to different dimensions:

```text
causal ground
domain of existence
operative opposition
```

A single predicate “lost” would conceal these radically different transformations.

---

## 18. Wholesome and defiled forms cease asymmetrically

The wholesome form is lost through:

```text
loss of support

or

departure from its realm
```

The defiled form is lost through:

```text
arising of an opposed dharma
```

This asymmetry reflects the different organization of their persistence.

```text
wholesome desire-realm form
    supported by wholesome roots
    and desire-realm existence

defiled form
    sustained until a counteragent
    actively displaces or abandons it
```

The system does not impose one generic cessation rule upon every dharma.

---

## 19. Connection with VAkK 4.41

VAkK 4.41 said:

```text
asaṃvara
    lost through acquisition of saṃvara
```

VAkK 4.42 generalizes the structure:

```text
kliṣṭa arūpa
    lost through arising of pratipakṣa
```

Acquisition of restraint is one instance of counteractive transformation.

```text
saṃvara
    functions as practical counter-form
    to asaṃvara
```

But **pratipakṣa** is broader. It includes the cognitive and meditative antidotes that oppose afflictive dharmas at different depths.

---

## 20. Ordinary restraint and the true counteragent

We should preserve the hierarchy:

```text
ordinary restraint
    counteracts enactment

meditative restraint
    suppresses operation
    on a transformed bhūmi

noble Path
    abandons the corresponding kleśa
    according to its Path-stage
```

All may be called oppositional in a broad sense, but they do not have identical depth.

```text
behavioral counteragent
meditative counteragent
Path counteragent
```

The Abhidharma must record exactly which one is present.

This is where the distinction between ordinary vows and saintly Path-vows becomes technically decisive.

---

## 21. The saint and latent kleśa

The saint does not merely place a standing prohibition against kleśa-generated activity.

The noble Path functions as a **pratipakṣa** to the afflictive ground itself.

```text
ordinary vow:
    kleśa → action blocked

meditative attainment:
    kleśa → manifestation suppressed

noble Path:
    kleśa → corresponding root abandoned
```

VAkK 4.42 therefore supplies the formal term for what had already been recognized:

> the saint goes against even the latent kleśas.

The saintly transformation is the arising of cognition that is genuinely incompatible with the continued possession of the targeted affliction.

## 22. Logic of Experience

This verse gives a powerful final determination for the thread.

A System of Dharma must not merely record positive states. It must encode the **rules of incompatibility**.

```text
Dharma D
    has support S
    belongs to domain R
    has counteragent C
```

Then:

```text
destroy S
    → D lost

leave R
    → realm-indexed D lost

actualize C
    → opposed D abandoned
```

The full record becomes:

```text
[ DHARMA
  QUALITY
  REALM
  SUPPORTING-ROOT
  COUNTERAGENT
  ACQUISITION-CONDITIONS
  PERSISTENCE-CONDITIONS
  LOSS-CONDITIONS ]
```

That is unmistakably a Logic of Experience.

## 23. Sarvadharma as a system of lawful opposition

The system of all dharmas cannot be merely a list because each dharma must be placed in a network:

```text
what produces it?
what supports it?
what coexists with it?
what is incompatible with it?
what suppresses it?
what abandons it?
what domain permits it?
```

**Pratipakṣa** introduces a particularly strong relation:

```text
COUNTERAGENT-OF(D1, D2)
```

This is not external negation.

It is a lawful transformative relation internal to experience.

```text
D2 arises
    precisely as the factor
    through which D1 ceases
```

## 24. A transition from Karma to Kleśa

VAkK 4.24–4.42 has analyzed persistent action-form.

But 4.42 ends by pointing beyond action toward its afflictive ground and counteragent.

```text
Karma:
    what persistent form has activity acquired?

Kleśa:
    what afflictive cause sustains that activity?

Path:
    what counteragent abandons that affliction?
```

So the structural movement is:

```text
avijñapti
    persistent karmic form

kliṣṭa
    afflictive character

pratipakṣa
    transformative opposition

prahāṇa
    abandonment
```

The next chapters are already latent inside this vocabulary.

## 25. Light Organon interpretation

The counteragent is not an abstract “not-D.”

It is a positive determination whose actuality negates D.

```text
abstract negation:
    D is simply absent

determinate negation:
    C arises as the actual counter-form of D
```

Thus:

```text
pratipakṣa
    = positive negation
    = operative contrary
```

The defiled form is not argued out of existence. A new experiential determination becomes actual and reorganizes the continuum.

This gives a concrete conception of negation:

```text
negation
    as transformation of the operative record
```

## 28. Determinate result

VAkK 4.42 establishes:

```text
wholesome nonmaterial dharma
belonging to the desire realm:
    lost through severance
    of the wholesome roots

    or through birth
    in a higher realm

defiled nonmaterial dharma:
    lost through the arising
    of its determinate counteragent
```

Its central principle is:

```text
a dharma may cease because
its causal support is destroyed,
its experiential domain is left behind,
or a positively opposed determination
becomes actual
```

This is an excellent final verse for the thread, because it completes the lifecycle analysis with **pratipakṣa**: the System of Dharma is not only a record of arising and persistence, but a system of lawful, experiential transformation.
