# VAK_4.110

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

एकैकं पुण्यशतजमसंख्येयत्रयान्त्यजाः ।
विपश्यी दीपकृद् रत्नशिखी शाक्यमुनिः पुरा ॥ ४.११० ॥

## 3. Padaccheda

`eka-ekam | puṇya-śata-jam | asaṃkhyeya-traya-antya-jāḥ | vipaśyī | dīpa-kṛt | ratna-śikhī | śākya-muniḥ | purā`

## 4. Literal Translation

> Each individual mark is born from one hundred merits. The former career of Śākyamuni, extending through three incalculable periods, is articulated by the Buddhas Vipaśyin, Dīpaṃkara, and Ratnaśikhin.

## 5. Philosophical Translation

> Each bodily mark of a Buddha is the matured result of a hundred distinct accumulations of merit. This formation belongs to a Bodhisattva career extending across three incalculable periods and mediated through past Buddha-fields, through which the one later appearing as Śākyamuni progressively determined his Buddha-form.

## 6. Technical Vocabulary

- **ekaikam** — each one individually; each Buddha mark separately.
- **puṇyaśatajam** — generated by one hundred merits.
- **asaṃkhyeya-traya** — three incalculable periods.
- **Vipaśyin, Dīpaṃkara, Ratnaśikhin** — past Buddhas articulating the Bodhisattva career.
- **Śākyamuni purā** — Śākyamuni in his former Bodhisattva history.

## 7. Doctrinal Determination

Each Buddha mark has its own determinate causal ground, consisting of a hundred merits. The hundred-kalpa period of concentrated mark-production belongs within the much larger career of three incalculable periods. Past Buddhas function as exceptional fields and exemplars within that history.

The Buddha-body is therefore not an adventitious vessel. It is the organized maturation of the Bodhisattva’s accumulated activity.

## 8. Logical Determination

The verse exhibits the transition:

`quantitative accumulation → qualitative bodily determination`

A hundred merits do not remain externally juxtaposed. They are synthesized into one visible mark. Each singular form concentrates a multiplicity of prior activities.

## 9. Light Organon Interpretation

The Buddha mark is a qualitative compression of quantitative history. Merit becomes appearance: activity is accumulated, integrated, and finally manifested as determinate form. The Buddha-body is an archive that embodies rather than merely records its causal history.
