# VAkK_7.01 — Knowledge, View, and Acceptance

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

```text
नामलाः क्षान्तयो ज्ञानं क्षयानुत्पादधीर्न दृक् ।
तदन्योभयथार्या धीः अन्या ज्ञानं दृशश्च षट् ॥ ७.१ ॥
```

### IAST

```text
nāmalāḥ kṣāntayo jñānaṃ kṣayānutpādadhīr na dṛk /
tadanyobhayathāryā dhīḥ anyā jñānaṃ dṛśaś ca ṣaṭ // VAkK_7.1 //
```

## Source Caution

The source transmits the opening as:

```text
nāmalā kṣāntayo jñānaṃ
```

This is resolved as:

```text
na amalāḥ kṣāntayo jñānam
```

The negative `na` governs `jñānam`: the uncontaminated acceptances are not knowledge.

## Grammar

The verse is an extremely compressed classification of `dhī`, discriminative cognition or principle-intelligence.

The first pāda excludes the uncontaminated acceptances (`amalāḥ kṣāntayaḥ`) from `jñāna`.

The second excludes `kṣayajñāna` and `anutpādajñāna` from `dṛṣṭi`.

The third states that the remaining noble cognition is both knowledge and view.

The fourth states that the remaining cognition is knowledge, while six forms of it are also views.

The understood subject throughout is `prajñā` or `dhī`, classified according to whether it has the determination of:

```text
jñāna

dṛṣṭi

both
```

## Literal Translation

The uncontaminated acceptances are not knowledge.
The cognition of exhaustion and non-arising is not view.
The other noble cognition is both.
The other cognition is knowledge, and six are views.

## Philosophical Translation

The stainless acceptances do not yet count as accomplished knowledge. The cognitions of exhaustion and non-arising are accomplished knowledge but no longer function as view. Every other noble discriminative cognition is both knowledge and view. All remaining discriminative cognition counts as knowledge, and six of its forms also count as views.

## Technical Vocabulary

### kṣānti

```text
kṣānti
    = acceptance
    = irreversible receptivity to the truth
```

Here `kṣānti` is not patience in an ethical or emotional sense. It is the path-cognition that receives and accepts the truth immediately before the corresponding knowledge arises.

The uncontaminated acceptances belong to the Path of Seeing, but they are not classified as `jñāna` because their work is still directed toward the abandonment of what is to be abandoned. They are active penetrations rather than completed ascertainments.

### jñāna

```text
jñāna
    = determinate knowledge
```

Knowledge is cognition that has ascertained its object. It is not merely the presence of a cognitive act, but the accomplished determination of what is known.

### dṛṣṭi

```text
dṛṣṭi
    = view
    = inspecting or decisively regarding cognition
```

View is a mode of prajñā that inspects, judges, or takes its object under a determinate aspect.

The six views referred to in the final pāda are:

```text
five afflicted views
    plus
worldly right view
```

### kṣayajñāna

```text
kṣayajñāna
    = knowledge of exhaustion
```

This is the determinate knowledge that the task of knowing, abandoning, realizing, and cultivating with respect to the Four Truths has been accomplished.

### anutpādajñāna

```text
anutpādajñāna
    = knowledge of non-arising
```

This is the determinate knowledge that what has been exhausted will not arise again and that the completed task need not be performed again.

### dhī

```text
dhī
    = discriminative cognition
    = operative intelligence
```

The verse uses `dhī` as the general cognitive substrate whose determinate forms are being classified as knowledge, view, or both.

## Doctrinal Determination

The verse establishes four principal cases.

### 1. Acceptance without knowledge

```text
uncontaminated kṣānti
    = not jñāna
```

The acceptances are noble and uncontaminated, but their function is still the immediate penetration and abandonment of the relevant defilements. They precede the completed cognition called knowledge.

### 2. Knowledge without view

```text
kṣayajñāna
anutpādajñāna
    = jñāna
    ≠ dṛṣṭi
```

These are final ascertainments. They no longer investigate or inspect their object as a view does. Their work has been completed.

### 3. Noble cognition as both

```text
remaining noble dhī
    = jñāna
    = dṛṣṭi
```

The other uncontaminated cognitions both know their object and inspect it under the aspects of the truths.

### 4. Remaining cognition

```text
remaining dhī
    = jñāna

six forms
    = dṛṣṭi also
```

All other discriminative cognition is broadly knowledge. Within it, the five afflicted views and worldly right view possess the additional character of view.

## Logical Determination

The verse does not merely list kinds of cognition. It constructs a crossing of two determinations:

```text
knowledge
view
```

The resulting field is:

| Cognitive form | Knowledge | View |
|---|---:|---:|
| uncontaminated acceptances | no | yes |
| exhaustion and non-arising cognitions | yes | no |
| remaining noble cognition | yes | yes |
| remaining cognition | yes | only in six cases |

This establishes that `jñāna` and `dṛṣṭi` are neither identical nor mutually exclusive.

```text
jñāna
    = accomplished determination

dṛṣṭi
    = inspecting determination
```

Acceptance is not yet accomplished knowledge. Final knowledge is no longer inspection. Between these extremes, noble cognition is both.

## Organon Light

The verse opens Jñānanirdeśa by treating cognition according to its logical function rather than merely its object.

```text
kṣānti
    immediate acceptance and penetration

jñāna-dṛṣṭi
    determinate knowing that still inspects

kṣaya / anutpāda
    completed knowing beyond inspection
```

A light Organon sequence is therefore:

```text
acceptance
    ↓
knowledge as view
    ↓
knowledge of accomplished exhaustion
```

The important point is functional differentiation within `dhī` itself. One and the same general power of discriminative cognition appears as acceptance, inspection, and completed ascertainment.

This gives a first contact with the DHI/JÑĀ architecture:

```text
DHĪ
    operative discriminative cognition

JÑĀNA
    DHĪ determined as accomplished knowing
```

No larger identification is required in the first kārikā pass.
