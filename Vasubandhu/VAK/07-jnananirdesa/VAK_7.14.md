# VAK_7.14 — Nature and Spheres of the Knowledges

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

```text
त्रिधाद्यं कुशलान्यन्यानि आद्यं सर्वासु भूमिषु ।
धर्माख्यं षट्सु नवसु त्वन्वयाख्यं तथैव षट् ॥ ७.१४ ॥
```

### IAST

```text
tridhādyaṃ kuśalāny anyāni ādyaṃ sarvāsu bhūmiṣu /
dharmākhyaṃ ṣaṭsu navasu tv anvayākhyaṃ tathaiva ṣaṭ // VAK_7.14 //
```

## Source Caution

The verse is highly compressed and changes the axis of analysis. The preceding verses examined the knowledges chiefly by aspect and object. This verse now treats:

```text
moral nature
    and
sphere of acquisition
```

The final `tathaiva ṣaṭ` refers to the six knowledges of the Four Truths together with the knowledges of exhaustion and non-arising. These six occur in six spheres when functioning as dharmajñāna and in nine when functioning as anvayajñāna.

## Grammar

`ādyam` refers to the knowledge named first in the original tenfold list: conventional knowledge (`saṃvṛtijñāna`).

It is `tridhā`, threefold by moral quality:

```text
wholesome
unwholesome
neutral
```

`kuśalāny anyāni` states that the other nine knowledges are wholesome.

The second half assigns spheres of acquisition:

```text
saṃvṛtijñāna
    all spheres

dharmajñāna
    six spheres

anvayajñāna
    nine spheres
```

The final `tathaiva ṣaṭ` extends the same six/nine distribution to six other knowledges:

```text
knowledge of suffering
knowledge of origin
knowledge of cessation
knowledge of path
knowledge of exhaustion
knowledge of non-arising
```

These occur in six spheres insofar as they are constituted by dharmajñāna, and in nine insofar as they are constituted by anvayajñāna.

## Literal Translation

The first is threefold; the others are wholesome. The first exists in all spheres. That called knowledge of dharmas exists in six, while that called anvaya knowledge exists in nine; likewise the six.

## Philosophical Translation

Conventional knowledge may be wholesome, unwholesome, or neutral, whereas the other nine knowledges are exclusively wholesome. Conventional knowledge is available throughout all levels of existence. Dharma-knowledge is acquired in six meditative spheres; anvaya-knowledge in nine. The six knowledges of the Four Truths, exhaustion, and non-arising follow the same distribution according to whether they participate in dharma-knowledge or anvaya-knowledge.

## Technical Vocabulary

### tridhā

```text
tridhā
    = in three ways
    = wholesome / unwholesome / neutral
```

The verse does not yet divide knowledge by truth, object, or aspect. It asks what moral character a cognition can possess.

### kuśala

```text
kuśala
    = wholesome
    = beneficial within the Path
```

The nine non-conventional knowledges are not morally indeterminate instruments. Their very occurrence belongs to the wholesome structure of the Path.

### bhūmi

```text
bhūmi
    = sphere
    = level or ground of meditative acquisition
```

Here `bhūmi` does not mean an abstract logical ground. It identifies the level in which a knowledge can be obtained or supported.

### dharmajñāna in six spheres

The six are:

```text
anāgamya
    preliminary concentration

dhyānāntara
    intermediate concentration

four dhyānas
```

### anvayajñāna in nine spheres

Anvayajñāna occurs in those same six plus the first three formless attainments.

```text
six concentration spheres
    +
three ārūpya spheres
    =
nine
```

It does not extend to the summit of existence (`bhavāgra`) as a sphere of acquisition.

## Doctrinal Determination

The verse distinguishes three questions that must not be collapsed:

| Question | Answering determination |
|---|---|
| What moral quality can this knowledge possess? | wholesome, unwholesome, neutral |
| In which sphere can it be acquired? | all, six, or nine spheres |
| Through which structural knowledge does it function? | dharmajñāna or anvayajñāna |

Conventional knowledge has the broadest distribution:

```text
all moral types
all spheres
```

Yet this breadth does not make it superior. Its range is broad because conventional cognition accompanies ordinary and cultivated existence across all levels.

The pure knowledges are narrower in moral nature:

```text
wholesome only
```

but more exact in their path-function.

Dharmajñāna and anvayajñāna are then distinguished not by a new object-definition here, but by the spheres in which they can be acquired.

## Logical Determination

The verse introduces a new form of grounded distinction:

```text
same knowledge-system
    differentiated by
moral nature
    and
sphere of support
```

This is still Reflection, but increasingly articulated.

A cognition is not completely specified by saying what it knows. One must also state:

```text
what quality it has
where it can arise
where it can be acquired
which wider knowledge-structure it instantiates
```

The verse also prevents a simple identification of breadth with purity:

```text
conventional knowledge
    widest distribution
    morally threefold

pure knowledges
    restricted distribution
    wholesome only
```

Thus universality of occurrence and purity of cognition are distinct determinations.

## Organon Light

VAK 7.14 is useful for the developing hypothesis about anvaya, but it does not yet prove a transcendental interpretation.

Textually, anvayajñāna has the greater vertical range:

```text
dharmajñāna
    six spheres

anvayajñāna
    nine spheres
```

This suggests that anvaya carries the truth-structure into higher meditative domains. It is therefore reasonable, provisionally, to hear it as a cognition that follows, extends, or carries forward a principial determination.

But the Kośa states this through the concrete architecture of bhūmis, not yet through a doctrine of Absolute Reflection.

The immediate lesson is simpler:

> Knowledge must be specified by the level of reality in which it can be acquired.

That is a strong reflective determination. The same named truth-knowledge may have a sixfold or ninefold sphere depending on whether it belongs to dharmajñāna or anvayajñāna.

This gives us a disciplined working hypothesis:

```text
dharmajñāna
    principial cognition tied to six supports

anvayajñāna
    extension of the same truth-structure
    through nine supports
```

No Dharma-megha or Absolute Reflection claim is imposed in this first pass.
