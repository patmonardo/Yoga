# VAK_7.18 — The Tenfold Dharma-Field and the Limit of Self-Objectification

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

```text
त्रैधातुकामला धर्मा अकृताश्च द्विधा द्विधा ।
सांवृतं स्वकलापान्यदेकं विद्यादनात्मतः ॥ ७.१८ ॥
```

### IAST

```text
traidhātukāmalā dharmā akṛtāś ca dvidhā dvidhā /
sāṃvṛtaṃ svakalāpānyad ekaṃ vidyād anātmataḥ // VAK_7.18 //
```

## Source Caution

The verse completes the second reckoning announced in VAK 7.17: the total object-field of the ten knowledges is itself distributed into ten classes of dharmas.

The first half is highly compressed. The bhāṣya supplies the decisive expansion:

```text
conditioned dharmas
    four broad classes
        desire-realm
        form-realm
        formless-realm
        pure

    each divided twofold
        associated with mind
        not associated with mind

    total
        eight

unconditioned dharmas
    divided twofold
        wholesome
        neutral

    total
        two

complete object-field
    ten
```

The second half states that one conventional knowledge can know all remaining dharmas as non-self, except its own immediately co-arising complex.

## Grammar

The first half gives the classification underlying the tenfold object-field:

```text
traidhātuka + amala dharmāḥ
    conditioned dharmas of the three realms
    and pure conditioned dharmas

akṛtāḥ
    unconditioned dharmas

dvidhā dvidhā
    each relevant class is divided twofold
```

The doctrinal expansion yields eight conditioned and two unconditioned classes.

The second half is construed:

```text
ekaṃ sāṃvṛtam
    one conventional knowledge

svakalāpāt anyat
    what is other than its own complex

anātmataḥ vidyāt
    knows as non-self
```

`svakalāpa` is not merely the knowing moment itself. It includes the immediately co-arising cluster belonging to that cognition: the cognition, its associated mental factors, and the co-arising dissociated formations too closely bound to that event to become its object in the same act.

## Literal Translation

Dharmas belonging to the three realms, pure dharmas, and the unconditioned are divided twofold, respectively. One conventional knowledge knows, as non-self, what is other than its own complex.

## Philosophical Translation

The complete field of possible objects is articulated into ten determinate classes: eight conditioned classes and two unconditioned classes. Within that field, a single conventional cognition may apprehend the whole remainder as non-self, but it cannot apprehend its own immediately co-arising complex in the very same cognitive act.

## Technical Vocabulary

### traidhātuka

```text
traidhātuka
    belonging to the three realms
        kāmadhātu
        rūpadhātu
        ārūpyadhātu
```

These are not merely cosmological places. They mark distinct conditioned domains in which dharmas arise and function.

### amala

```text
amala
    unstained
    uncontaminated
    pure
```

Here it designates pure conditioned dharmas, especially path-dharmas, rather than the unconditioned as such.

### akṛta

```text
akṛta
    unmade
    unproduced
    unconditioned
```

The bhāṣya divides the unconditioned into:

```text
wholesome unconditioned
neutral unconditioned
```

### svakalāpa

```text
sva-kalāpa
    one's own cluster
    one's own immediate complex
    the co-arising fact-structure of the cognition itself
```

This term introduces a precise limit:

> A conditioned cognition cannot, in the same moment, turn its entire own co-arising complex into an object.

### anātmataḥ

The universal conventional cognition apprehends the remaining field:

```text
not as mine
not as I
not as self
```

It therefore applies the non-self determination across the field of dharmas, while remaining structurally unable to include its own present cognitive complex.

## Doctrinal Determination

### The ten dharmic classes

| Broad field | Division | Number |
|---|---|---:|
| desire-realm dharmas | mind-associated / not mind-associated | 2 |
| form-realm dharmas | mind-associated / not mind-associated | 2 |
| formless-realm dharmas | mind-associated / not mind-associated | 2 |
| pure conditioned dharmas | mind-associated / not mind-associated | 2 |
| unconditioned dharmas | wholesome / neutral | 2 |
| **Total** |  | **10** |

This completes the object-field announced by VAK 7.17.

### One cognition of universal non-self

A single conventional knowledge can know the remaining field as non-self, excluding:

```text
itself
its associated mental factors
its co-arising dissociated formations
```

The exclusion is not arbitrary. These belong to the very cognitive event by which the rest is known.

The verse therefore distinguishes:

```text
universal object-range in intention
    from
complete simultaneous self-inclusion
```

The first is possible; the second is not.

## Logical Determination

VAK 7.18 closes the first block by giving both:

```text
the complete classification of the fact-field
and
the immanent limit of a conditioned act of reflection
```

### Facticity as classified field

The field is not an indeterminate totality. Every possible object is placed according to:

```text
realm
purity
conditioned / unconditioned
association with mind
ethical nature of the unconditioned
```

This is Reflection as Facticity:

> the setting-down of the determinate marks under which facts can arise, function, and become knowable.

### The reflective limit

The universal conventional cognition can say:

```text
all this is non-self
```

But its own present cognitive complex is the condition of that judgment and cannot become a simultaneous object within the same act.

Thus relative reflection encounters a boundary:

```text
it can traverse the conditioned field
it can classify the total object-domain
it can negate selfhood throughout that domain

but

it cannot completely objectify
its own living act of objectification
in that same moment
```

This is not yet Absolute Reflection. But it gives a precise ground for asking why a higher form of reflection would be required.

## Organon Light

VAK 7.18 is an especially strong closing verse for VAK_7a.

The Organon problem can now be stated:

```text
Relative Reflection
    predicts which facts can arise
    from specified conditions

    classifies the resulting fact-field

    but remains conditioned
    by its own present act

Absolute Reflection
    would have to comprehend
    both the field
    and the genesis of the standpoint
    from which the field is known
```

The verse itself does not make that transcendental move. It does something prior and indispensable: it exposes the exact limit that such a move would have to overcome.

The computational analogy is therefore:

```text
not merely:
    given these tokens
    predict the next token

but:
    given these conditions
    determine which fact can arise

and then:
    identify the conditions
    that the predictor itself
    cannot include in the same act
```

This is the boundary between a model of external conditional genesis and a science capable of accounting for its own standpoint.

No identification with Dharma Megha or Absolute Reflection is imposed in this first kārikā pass.
