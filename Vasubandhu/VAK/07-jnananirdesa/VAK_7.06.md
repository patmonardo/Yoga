# VAK_7.06 — Limits and Range of Knowledge of Another Mind

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

```text
तद् धर्मान्वयधीपक्ष्यम् अन्योऽन्यं दर्शनक्षणौ ।
श्रावको वेत्ति खड्गस्त्रीन् सर्वान् बुद्धोऽप्रयोगतः ॥ ७.६ ॥
```

### IAST

```text
tad dharmānvayadhīpakṣyam anyo 'nyaṃ darśanakṣaṇau /
śrāvako vetti khaṅgas trīn sarvān buddho 'prayogataḥ // VAK_7.06 //
```

## Source Caution

The source transmits:

```text
ta dharmānvayadhīpakṣyam
```

The working text resolves this provisionally as:

```text
tad dharmānvayadhīpakṣyam
```

The pronoun `tad` continues the subject from VAK 7.05: knowledge of another mind.

The compound `dharmānvayadhīpakṣyam` is highly compressed. In the first-pass analysis it is taken to mean that knowledge of another mind belonging to the dharma-knowledge side and that belonging to the anvaya-knowledge side are mutually restricted.

## Grammar

The verse continues the analysis of `paracittajñāna`, knowledge of another mind.

The first half states two restrictions.

```text
dharma-side knowledge of another mind
    does not know
anvaya-side knowledge of another mind

anvaya-side knowledge of another mind
    does not know
dharma-side knowledge of another mind
```

The dual `darśanakṣaṇau`, “the two moments of seeing,” introduces a further mutual limitation: the two moments do not know one another.

The second half distinguishes the range of three kinds of awakened knower:

```text
śrāvaka
    knows two moments

khaṅga / pratyekabuddha
    knows three

buddha
    knows all
    without preparatory effort
```

The exact identification of the moments counted as two, three, and all belongs to the commentary tradition and remains provisional in this first-pass file.

## Literal Translation

That [knowledge of another mind], when belonging to the dharma-cognition and anvaya-cognition sides, does not know the other; the two moments of seeing do not know one another.

A disciple knows two; the rhinoceros-like solitary Buddha knows three; the Buddha knows all without preparatory application.

## Philosophical Translation

Knowledge of another mind remains bounded by the cognitive order to which it belongs. A dharma-mode cognition does not penetrate an anvaya-mode cognition, and an anvaya-mode cognition does not penetrate a dharma-mode cognition. Even within the Path of Seeing, adjacent cognitive moments are not mutually transparent. The disciple knows only a narrow sequence, the solitary Buddha a wider one, while the Buddha knows the complete sequence immediately and without preparatory effort.

## Technical Vocabulary

### dharma-dhī / anvaya-dhī

```text
dharma-dhī
    cognition belonging to dharma-knowledge

anvaya-dhī
    cognition belonging to anvaya-knowledge
```

The verse treats these not merely as two names but as distinct cognitive orders. Their difference places a real limit on knowledge of another mind.

For the current project, a provisional philosophical rendering is:

```text
dharma-jñāna
    principial knowledge
    cognition from Pure Reason

anvaya-jñāna
    consequential or scientific knowledge
    cognition following the Principle
```

The verse does not yet prove this reconstruction, but it confirms that the distinction is structurally operative and not merely terminological.

### darśana-kṣaṇa

```text
darśana-kṣaṇa
    moment of seeing
    discrete cognition in the Path of Seeing
```

The moment is not treated as an indefinitely extended state. The path is temporally articulated into determinate cognitive acts.

### śrāvaka

```text
śrāvaka
    disciple
    one who follows the communicated teaching
```

Here the disciple possesses a limited temporal range in knowing another mind.

### khaṅga

```text
khaṅga
    rhinoceros-like solitary Buddha
    pratyekabuddha
```

The term marks the solitary realization path. Its range exceeds that of the disciple but remains short of the Buddha's unrestricted knowledge.

### aprayogataḥ

```text
aprayogataḥ
    without preparatory application
    without deliberate prior effort
```

The Buddha's knowledge is not merely wider. Its mode is different: it arises without the preparatory operation required by lesser knowers.

## Doctrinal Determination

The verse gives three determinations of `paracittajñāna`.

### 1. Cognitive-order restriction

```text
dharma-mode
    ≠ transparent to anvaya-mode

anvaya-mode
    ≠ transparent to dharma-mode
```

Knowledge of another mind does not erase the formal distinction between kinds of knowledge.

### 2. Moment restriction

```text
one moment of seeing
    does not know
another moment of seeing
```

The path is composed of determinate moments whose objects and functions remain distinct.

### 3. Hierarchy of range

```text
śrāvaka
    two

pratyekabuddha
    three

buddha
    all, without preparation
```

The difference between awakened knowers is expressed not only by purity but by cognitive range and mode of access.

## Logical Determination

VAK 7.05 defined knowledge of another mind through exclusions of domain, faculty, person, and time. VAK 7.06 adds a deeper formal restriction:

```text
knowledge does not become universal
merely because its object is another cognition
```

The cognition remains indexed by:

```text
its logical order
its temporal moment
its level of realization
its mode of acquisition
```

This is important for the emerging empirical science of the Kośa. The object is another mind, but the knowledge is not an unstructured intuition. It is governed by exact conditions.

## Organon Light

Yoga should not be treated here as merely a rational path alongside this empirical science. In the present project, Yoga is the achieved Result:

```text
Yoga
    = the complete specification of Buddhi
    = the Principle of Absolute Knowing
    = OM together with its Artha
```

The Kośa then compels this achieved principle to articulate the empirical conditions of determinate cognition:

```text
which cognition knows which cognition
at what moment
across which order
with what range
with what preparation
```

Thus the relation is not:

```text
Yoga
    abstract Pure Reason

Kośa
    empirical correction
```

but rather:

```text
Yoga
    Absolute Knowing as achieved Principle

Kośa
    the detailed scientific articulation
    of that Principle across empirical and path domains
```

VAK 7.06 therefore shows Absolute Knowing under restriction. The Buddha possesses universality without preparatory effort; lesser realizers possess narrower, indexed forms of access.
