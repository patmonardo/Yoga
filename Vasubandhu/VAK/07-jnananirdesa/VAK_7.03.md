# VAK_7.03 — The Object-Ranges of the Three Knowledges

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

```text
सांवृतं सर्वविषयं कामदुःखादिगोचरम् ।
धर्माख्यम् अन्वयज्ञानं तूर्ध्वदुःखादिगोचरम् ॥ ७.३ ॥
```

### IAST

```text
sāṃvṛtaṃ sarvaviṣayaṃ kāmaduḥkhādigocaram /
dharmākhyam anvayajñānaṃ tūrdhvaduḥkhādigocaram // VAK_7.03 //
```

## Padaccheda

| Pada | Analysis | Working sense |
|---|---|---|
| sāṃvṛtam | nominative singular neuter | conventional knowledge |
| sarva-viṣayam | nominative singular neuter | having every object; all-object knowledge |
| kāma-duḥkha-ādi-gocaram | nominative singular neuter | ranging over suffering and the other truths in the desire domain |
| dharma-ākhyam | nominative singular neuter | called dharma-knowledge |
| anvaya-jñānam | nominative singular neuter | subsequent or homologous knowledge |
| tu | conjunction | but; whereas |
| ūrdhva-duḥkha-ādi-gocaram | nominative singular neuter | ranging over suffering and the other truths in the higher domains |

## Grammar

The verse supplies the object-range of each knowledge named in VAK 7.02.

The first clause is complete in itself:

```text
sāṃvṛtaṃ jñānam
    sarvaviṣayam
```

Conventional knowledge has all objects as its range.

The next expression must be construed with an understood `jñānam`:

```text
kāma-duḥkha-ādi-gocaraṃ jñānam
    dharma-ākhyam
```

Knowledge whose range is suffering and the remaining truths in the desire domain is called dharma-knowledge.

The final clause contrasts this with subsequent knowledge:

```text
anvayajñānaṃ tu
    ūrdhva-duḥkha-ādi-gocaram
```

Subsequent knowledge has suffering and the remaining truths in the higher domains as its range.

The particle `ādi`, “and so forth,” extends `duḥkha` to the complete fourfold truth-series:

```text
duḥkha
samudaya
nirodha
mārga
```

## Literal Translation

Conventional knowledge has every object.
Knowledge whose range is suffering and the other truths in the desire domain is called dharma-knowledge.
Subsequent knowledge, however, has suffering and the other truths in the higher domains as its range.

## Philosophical Translation

Conventional knowledge ranges over the entire field of possible objects. Uncontaminated knowledge is more exactly determined: when the four truths are known with respect to the desire domain, the cognition is called dharma-knowledge; when those same truths are known with respect to the higher domains, the cognition is subsequent knowledge.

## Technical Vocabulary

### sarvaviṣaya

```text
sarva-viṣaya
    = having all objects
    = unrestricted with respect to object-kind
```

The claim is broad: conventional knowledge can take conditioned and unconditioned objects, worldly and path-related objects, as its object. Its conventional character does not arise from being confined to one narrow empirical class.

The verse does not yet subdivide this all-object range. It leaves conventional knowledge extensionally broad.

### gocara

```text
gocara
    = range
    = field of operation
    = object-domain
```

`Gocara` is more dynamic than a bare object. It indicates the field within which a cognition moves and operates.

### kāma

```text
kāma
    = the desire domain
```

Here `kāma` qualifies the four truths according to the domain in which they are known. It does not mean that dharma-knowledge knows only desire as an isolated object.

### ūrdhva

```text
ūrdhva
    = higher
    = above the desire domain
```

The higher domains are the form and formless domains. They are gathered together as one side of the division opposite the desire domain.

### dharmajñāna

```text
dharmajñāna
    = uncontaminated knowledge of the four truths
      with respect to the desire domain
```

The chapter gives `dharma` a precise technical determination here. Its meaning is fixed by the object-range of the cognition rather than by a generic translation of `dharma` as doctrine or phenomenon.

### anvayajñāna

```text
anvayajñāna
    = uncontaminated knowledge of the corresponding truths
      with respect to the higher domains
```

`Anvaya` indicates continuation, correspondence, or following according to the same order. The truth-determination first established with respect to the desire domain is extended homologously to the higher domains.

## Doctrinal Determination

VAK 7.02 supplied the formal division:

```text
jñāna
    ├── saṃvṛtijñāna
    └── anāsrava-jñāna
            ├── dharmajñāna
            └── anvayajñāna
```

VAK 7.03 now supplies the corresponding object-ranges:

```text
saṃvṛtijñāna
    → all objects

dharmajñāna
    → four truths in the desire domain

anvayajñāna
    → four truths in the higher domains
```

The verse therefore distinguishes two different principles of range.

### Conventional knowledge

```text
range
    = universal with respect to objects
```

Conventional knowledge is not limited by the desire-domain / higher-domain opposition.

### Uncontaminated knowledge

```text
range
    = exactly divided by domain
```

The same four truths are known on each side, but the domain differs:

```text
desire domain
    /
higher domains
```

This difference is sufficient to generate two distinct uncontaminated knowledges.

## Logical Determination

The verse is notable for what it does **not** do.

It does not begin by empirically enumerating the countless possible objects of conventional knowledge. Instead it gives conventional knowledge the universal determination:

```text
sarvaviṣaya
    all-object
```

The exact division appears only within uncontaminated knowledge:

```text
four truths in the desire domain
    /
four truths in the higher domains
```

This is a dichotomy relative to domain:

```text
kāma
    / 
ūrdhva
```

The second member gathers both form and formless domains under one determination, `ūrdhva`. The verse therefore avoids making the three realms into three coordinate species of uncontaminated knowledge.

Its architecture is:

```text
conventional knowledge
    universal object-range

uncontaminated knowledge
    one truth-structure
    divided into two domain-ranges
```

This supports the observation that the Division of Jñāna proceeds through complete oppositions rather than through merely empirical lists.

## Organon Light

VAK 7.02 divided knowledge according to contamination. VAK 7.03 shows that this formal division is also a division of cognitive range.

```text
saṃvṛti
    all-object extension

anāsrava
    exact truth-determination
```

But uncontaminated knowledge does not divide according to two different truth-systems. The content remains the same four truths. The division concerns where that truth-system is applied:

```text
same truth-form
    ×
contrasted domain
```

Thus:

```text
dharmajñāna
    immediate truth-determination in the desire domain

anvayajñāna
    homologous extension of that determination upward
```

A light logical formula is:

```text
one content
    four truths

two opposed ranges
    kāma / ūrdhva

two uncontaminated knowledges
    dharma / anvaya
```

The empirical manifold is not allowed to dictate the primary division. The division is produced by the Concept of the cognitive function and its complete object-domain.

## Interpretive Note

The verse gives us an early reason to watch the chapter’s method carefully:

```text
saṃvṛti
    can know everything as object

anāsrava
    knows through an exact division of the truth-field
```

Universal extension alone is therefore not yet liberating knowledge. Liberation requires the determinate organization of the field according to the truths and their domains.

At the same time, the higher domains are not treated as unrelated empirical worlds. They are gathered as the corresponding continuation of the same truth-order. This is the force provisionally assigned to `anvaya`.
