# VAK 6.11

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

अतिक्रान्तमनस्कारो भ्रूमध्ये चित्तधारणात् ।
अलोभो दशभूः कामदृश्यालम्बा नृजाशुभा ॥ ६.११ ॥

### IAST

**atikrānta-manaskāro bhrūmadhye citta-dhāraṇāt |**
**alobho daśabhūḥ kāma-dṛśyālambā nṛjāśubhā || 6.11 ||**

## Literal Translation

Through fixing cognition between the eyebrows, one becomes one who has surpassed the act of attention. The aśubha cultivation is non-greed, belongs to ten levels, has visible form in the desire-domain as its object-support, and is generated by humans.

## Philosophical Translation

When the practitioner can sustain cognition upon the final residual support between the eyebrows, mastery of the constructed attention has been achieved. The unattractive cultivation is intrinsically non-appropriative; it can arise on ten meditative levels, takes visible bodily form in the desire-domain as its support, and is produced within human embodiment.

## Technical Determination

- **atikrānta-manaskāra**: mastery over the act of attention, not transcendence of cognition as such.
- **bhrūmadhye citta-dhāraṇā**: sustaining cognition upon the minimal residual support between the eyebrows.
- **alobha**: non-greed or non-appropriation; this confirms that aśubha is not aversion.
- **daśabhū**: the practice may occur on ten meditative levels.
- **kāma-dṛśyālambana**: its object-support is visible form belonging to the desire-domain.
- **nṛja**: generated by a human practitioner.

## Analysis

VAK 6.09 diagnosed desire and assigned aśubha; VAK 6.10 described expansion, contraction, and progressive removal; VAK 6.11 completes the mastery sequence and classifies the practice scientifically.

The crucial doctrinal point is that aśubha is **alobha**, not dveṣa. It does not replace attraction with hatred. It transforms the appropriative relation through which the body is grasped as desirable, mine, or for me.

The first half gives maximal cognitive mastery through minimal object-support:

```text
large constructed field
    ↓
skeleton
    ↓
half-skull
    ↓
minimal support between the eyebrows
```

The second half then delimits the practice by intrinsic nature, level, object, and producer. The more powerful the practice becomes, the more precisely it must be classified. Mastery of a preparation is not completion of the Path.
