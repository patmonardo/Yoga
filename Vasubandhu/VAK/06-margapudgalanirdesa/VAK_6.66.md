# VAK 6.66

## Sanskrit

ध्यानेषु मार्गः प्रतिपत्सुखा दुःखान्यभूमिषु ।
दन्धाभिज्ञा मृदुमतेः क्षिप्राभिज्ञेतरस्य तु ॥

*dhyāneṣu mārgaḥ pratipat sukhā duḥkhānyabhūmiṣu /
dhandhābhijñā mṛdumateḥ kṣiprābhijñetarasya tu //*

## Source Caution

The kārikā-only electronic text reads `dhanyābhijñā`, but the doctrinal context requires `dhandhābhijñā`, slow realization, in contrast with `kṣiprābhijñā`, quick realization.

## Padaccheda

- *dhyāneṣu* — in the dhyānas
- *mārgaḥ* — Path
- *pratipat* — mode of progress; course
- *sukhā* — easy
- *duḥkhā* — difficult
- *anya-bhūmiṣu* — in the other grounds
- *dhandha-abhijñā* — slow realization
- *mṛdu-mateḥ* — for one of mild faculty
- *kṣipra-abhijñā* — quick realization
- *itarasya* — for the other

## Literal Translation

In the dhyānas the Path is an easy mode of progress; in the other grounds it is difficult. For one of mild faculty realization is slow, but for the other it is quick.

## Philosophical Translation

When grounded in the principal dhyānas, the Path proceeds with ease. In the remaining contemplative grounds it proceeds through difficulty. For the practitioner whose knowing-faculty is mild, realization develops slowly; for one of sharp faculty, realization is rapid.

## Four Modes of Progress

Two independent dyads are combined:

```text
sukhā / duḥkhā
    easy / difficult progress

dhandhābhijñā / kṣiprābhijñā
    slow / quick realization
```

This yields four concrete modes:

```text
difficult + slow
difficult + quick
easy + slow
easy + quick
```

## Ground and Faculty

The verse distinguishes two causal determinations:

```text
bhūmi
    determines ease or difficulty

mati / indriya
    determines slowness or quickness
```

The difficulty of the Path belongs to the contemplative ground. The speed of realization belongs to the faculty.

Therefore:

```text
easy ground
    does not imply quick realization

difficult ground
    does not imply slow realization
```

## Dhyāna as Easy Ground

The principal dhyānas support an easy course because calm and discriminative seeing are more evenly established. `sukha` here means structurally supported and readily traversed, not merely pleasurable.

The other grounds are comparatively difficult because the balance required for liberating progress is less complete.

## Relation to VAK 6.65


The same universal Path-form becomes differently actual according to particular ground and singular faculty.

## Determinate Summary

VAK 6.66 distinguishes the objective difficulty of the Path from the subjective speed of realization. The contemplative ground determines whether progress is easy or difficult; the faculty determines whether realization is slow or quick. Their combination yields four concrete modes of Path-progress and prepares the transition to the internal powers of the Path named `bodhipakṣya`.
