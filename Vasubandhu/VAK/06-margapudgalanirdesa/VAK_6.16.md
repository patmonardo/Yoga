# VAK 6.16

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

स धर्मस्मृत्युपस्थाने समस्तालम्बने स्थितः ।
आनित्यदुःखतः शून्यानात्मतस्तान् विपश्यति ॥ ६.१६ ॥

### IAST

**sa dharma-smṛtyupasthāne samastālambane sthitaḥ |**  
**ānitya-duḥkhataḥ śūnyānātmatas tān vipaśyati || 6.16 ||**

## Grammar

- **saḥ** — nominative singular masculine pronoun: “he,” referring to the practitioner.
- **dharma-smṛtyupasthāne** — locative singular neuter compound: “in the Foundation of Recollection concerning dharmas.”
- **samasta-ālambane** — locative singular neuter agreeing with *dharma-smṛtyupasthāne*: “whose object-support is the totality.”
- **sthitaḥ** — nominative singular masculine past passive participle of √sthā: “established,” “having taken a stand.”
- **ānityataḥ, duḥkhataḥ, śūnyataḥ, anātmataḥ** — adverbial ablative formations: “as impermanent,” “as suffering/unstable,” “as empty,” “as not-self.”
- **tān** — accusative plural masculine pronoun: “those,” referring to the previously examined objects or fields.
- **vipaśyati** — 3rd person singular present active of vi-√paś: “he sees distinctly,” “he examines penetratively.”

## Literal Translation

Established in the Dharma-Foundation of Recollection, whose object-support is the totality, he sees those objects distinctly as impermanent, suffering, empty, and not-self.

## Philosophical Translation

Established in the Foundation of Recollection concerning dharmas, the practitioner takes the total field as object and sees its determinations under the universal characteristics of impermanence, instability, emptiness, and non-self.

## Analysis

VAK 6.14 introduced examination according to intrinsic and universal characteristics. VAK 6.15 identified Prajñā as the intrinsic operation of the Foundations and distinguished associated factors and object-supports. VAK 6.16 now states the synthetic stage: the Dharma-Foundation has **the totality as its object-support**.

The first three Foundations examine restricted fields:

- body,
- feeling,
- cognition.

The Dharma-Foundation gathers these fields together with the relevant dharmas and examines them as a total object. This is not an undifferentiated totality. The field has already been analyzed; it is now recollected and examined as an articulated whole.

The word **sthitaḥ** is important. The practitioner is not merely entertaining a doctrine. He is established in the Dharma-Foundation: the standpoint of examination has become stable.

The verb **vipaśyati** indicates discriminative seeing. The prefix *vi-* marks distinction and analysis. The four universal determinations are therefore not added as vague reflections; they are the forms under which the total field is examined:

- **anityatā** — conditioned things arise and cease;
- **duḥkhatā** — contaminated and conditioned things are unstable and cannot provide secure satisfaction;
- **śūnyatā** — dharmas are empty of independent self-grounding existence;
- **anātmatā** — no dharma is an independent self or owner.

Thus the verse marks the passage from differentiated examination to comprehensive vision.

## Determinate Summary

VAK 6.16 teaches that the practitioner, established in the Dharma-Foundation, takes the total field as object and sees it distinctly according to four universal characteristics: impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self.
