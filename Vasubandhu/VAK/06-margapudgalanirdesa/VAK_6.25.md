# VAK 6.25

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

श्रुतचिन्तामयं त्रीणि कर्माण्याक्षिप्यते नृषु ।  
लौकिकेभ्योऽग्रधर्मेभ्यो धर्मक्षान्तिरनास्रवा ॥ ६.२५ ॥

### IAST

**śruta-cintā-mayaṃ trīṇi karmāṇy ākṣipyate nṛṣu |**  
**laukikebhyo 'gradharmebhyo dharma-kṣāntir anāsravā || 6.25 ||**

## Source Caution

The kārikā continues the subject introduced in VAK 6.24:

```text
mokṣa-bhāgīya
    wholesome root conducive to liberation
```

The first half should therefore be read as a compressed series of determinations of that wholesome root:

```text
mokṣabhāgīyam
    śruta-cintā-mayam
    trīṇi karmāṇi
    nṛṣu ākṣipyate
```

The second half begins the account of the Path of Seeing.

## Padaccheda

## Grammar

- **śruta-cintā-mayam** — nominative singular neuter, agreeing with the understood **mokṣa-bhāgīyam**: “consisting of hearing and reflection.”
- **trīṇi karmāṇi** — nominative plural neuter: “the three actions,” namely bodily, vocal, and mental action; an elliptical predication of the liberation-conducive root.
- **ākṣipyate** — third-person singular present passive of **ā-√kṣip**: “is projected,” “is initiated,” or “is planted.”
- **nṛṣu** — locative plural of **nṛ**: “among human beings” or “in human existences.”
- **laukikebhyaḥ agra-dharmebhyaḥ** — ablative plural: “from the Highest Mundane Dharmas.”
- **dharma-kṣāntiḥ** — nominative singular feminine: “Dharma-Acceptance”; more fully, the acceptance associated with Dharma-knowledge.
- **anāsravā** — nominative singular feminine: “uncontaminated,” agreeing with **dharma-kṣāntiḥ**.

## Literal Translation

The liberation-conducive root consists of hearing and reflection; it is the three actions and is projected among human beings. From the Highest Mundane Dharmas arises the uncontaminated Dharma-Acceptance.

## Philosophical Translation

The wholesome root conducive to liberation is formed through hearing and reflective consideration. It may take the form of bodily, vocal, or mental action—although mental intention is primary—and it is planted within a human existence. Immediately after the Highest Mundane Dharmas, the first uncontaminated moment arises: Dharma-Acceptance.

## The Liberation-Conducive Root

VAK 6.24 introduced:

```text
mokṣabhāgīya
    conducive to liberation
```

VAK 6.25 now determines its constitution.

### Hearing and reflection

```text
śruta
    hearing the Dharma

cintā
    reflective consideration

śruta-cintā-maya
    formed from hearing and reflection
```

The kārikā does not classify this root as **bhāvanā-maya**, formed from cultivation. It belongs to the preparatory mundane orientation toward liberation.

### Three actions

```text
kāya-karma
    bodily action

vāk-karma
    vocal action

mano-karma
    mental action
```

The Bhāṣya makes mental action primary because bodily and vocal acts become liberation-conducive through the intention or aspiration directing them toward liberation.

Thus even a limited act of giving or discipline may plant this root when it is governed by the wish for liberation.

### Human support

```text
nṛṣu
    among humans
    in human existence
```

The liberation-conducive root is planted on the human support. Human existence is therefore the field in which the orientation toward liberation is first karmically established.

## From the Mundane to the Uncontaminated

The decisive transition is:

```text
laukika agra-dharmāḥ
    Highest Mundane Dharmas
        ↓

dharma-kṣāntiḥ anāsravā
    uncontaminated Dharma-Acceptance
```

The Highest Mundane Dharmas remain **laukika**, mundane. They are the final preparatory moment.

Dharma-Acceptance is **anāsrava**, uncontaminated. It is the first moment of the supramundane Path of Seeing.

The relation is immediate:

```text
Highest Mundane Dharmas
    final mundane preparation

Dharma-Acceptance
    first uncontaminated path-moment
```

## Dharma-Kṣānti

Here **kṣānti** should not be reduced to ordinary patience. It is acceptance or receptivity to the truth immediately realized by the path.

VAK 6.26 will specify its first object:

```text
suffering
    in the desire realm
```

The fuller doctrinal designation is therefore:

```text
duḥkhe dharma-jñāna-kṣānti
    acceptance of Dharma-knowledge concerning suffering
```

VAK 6.25 announces the arising of this first uncontaminated moment; VAK 6.26 begins its detailed sequence.

## Doctrinal Determination

VAK 6.25 establishes two successive levels.

```text
Liberation-conducive root {
    constitution:
        hearing and reflection

    modes:
        bodily action
        vocal action
        mental action

    principal mode:
        mental action

    support:
        human existence

    status:
        mundane
}

DharmaAcceptance {
    immediate predecessor:
        Highest Mundane Dharmas

    status:
        uncontaminated

    path:
        Path of Seeing
}
```

## Logical Determination

The verse marks a boundary rather than merely another preparatory grade:

```text
orientation toward liberation
    ↓
preparation for penetration
    ↓
Highest Mundane Dharmas
    ↓
Dharma-Acceptance
```

The first three remain within mundane formation. Dharma-Acceptance is the first path-moment whose determination is **anāsrava**.

The transition is therefore:

```text
mundane preparation
    → uncontaminated seeing
```

At the kārikā-pass level, this is the essential structural determination. The detailed logic of the transition belongs to the later Bhāṣya pass.

```text

    is formed through hearing and reflection

    is expressed through bodily,
    vocal, and mental action

    is principally mental action

    is planted in human existence

From the Highest Mundane Dharmas:

    there immediately arises
    uncontaminated Dharma-Acceptance

This is the transition:

    mundane preparation
        → first uncontaminated path-moment
```

VAK 6.25 opens the second block by crossing from the preparatory architecture into the Path of Seeing.
