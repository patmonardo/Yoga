# VAK_6.78

## Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

> असंस्कृतैव धात्वाख्या विरागो रागसंक्षयः ।  
> प्रहाणधातुरन्येषां निरोधाख्यस्तु वस्तुनः ॥

## IAST

> **asaṃskṛtaiva dhātvākhyā virāgo rāga-saṃkṣayaḥ |**  
> **prahāṇa-dhātur anyeṣāṃ nirodhākhyas tu vastunaḥ ||**

## Padaccheda

```text
asaṃskṛtā eva
dhātu-ākhyā

virāgaḥ
rāga-saṃkṣayaḥ

prahāṇa-dhātuḥ
anyeṣām

nirodha-ākhyaḥ
tu
vastunaḥ
```

## Literal translation

> **What is called the elements is exclusively unconditioned. Dispassion is the complete destruction of attachment; the abandonment-element is that of the other afflictions, while what is called cessation concerns the real thing.**

The final **vastunaḥ** is highly compressed. Its exact extension should be checked against the Bhāṣya, but the threefold division is clear.

## Three dhātus of liberation

The verse introduces three:

```text
virāga-dhātu
    element of dispassion

prahāṇa-dhātu
    element of abandonment

nirodha-dhātu
    element of cessation
```

And immediately determines them as:

```text
asaṃskṛta eva
    exclusively unconditioned
```

Here **dhātu** is best preserved as **domain**, **basis**, or **elemental determination**—not a material element.

## Virāga-dhātu

```text
virāgo rāga-saṃkṣayaḥ
```

> **Dispassion is the complete destruction of rāga.**

This is more exact than translating *virāga* merely as emotional detachment.

```text
rāga
    attachment
    passion
    appropriating adhesion

virāga
    its complete exhaustion
```

Thus the element of dispassion is the unconditioned cessation corresponding specifically to the destruction of attachment.

## Prahāṇa-dhātu

```text
prahāṇa-dhātur anyeṣām
```

> **The abandonment-element is the destruction of the others.**

“Others” most naturally means the other afflictions besides rāga.

```text
rāga
    → virāga-dhātu

other kleśas
    → prahāṇa-dhātu
```

This is not necessarily a division into two different unconditioned substances. It is a difference of designation according to the obstruction abandoned.

## Nirodha-dhātu

```text
nirodhākhyas tu vastunaḥ
```

The third is “called cessation” in relation to **vastu**.

Here **vastu** should not be weakened to an abstract “thing.” It indicates the real conditioned basis or actual occurrence whose continuation is stopped.

A cautious first-pass rendering is:

> **The cessation-element is so called with reference to the cessation of the conditioned actuality itself.**

The contrast appears to be:

```text
virāga
    cessation considered relative to rāga

prahāṇa
    cessation considered relative to other afflictions

nirodha
    cessation considered relative to the conditioned vastu
```

Thus the same unconditioned freedom can be named according to:

```text
the passion ended
the affliction abandoned
the conditioned occurrence ceased
```

## Relation to the Aśaikṣa mind

VAK_6.77 gave the dynamic event:

```text
Path ceases
    abandons obstruction

Aśaikṣa mind arises
    liberated
```

VAK_6.78 now names the unconditioned domains disclosed by that abandonment:

```text
virāga-dhātu
prahāṇa-dhātu
nirodha-dhātu
```

So the Aśaikṣa mind is not merely a superior mental state. It arises in relation to an objective structure of accomplished cessation:

```text
conditioned side
    Aśaikṣa-citta

unconditioned side
    liberation-elements
```

The Kośa does not reduce liberation to psychology.

## Doctrinal determination

```text
virāga-dhātu
    destruction of rāga

prahāṇa-dhātu
    destruction of the other afflictions

nirodha-dhātu
    cessation considered relative
    to the conditioned vastu

all three
    asaṃskṛta
```

The Path therefore culminates in both:

```text
a liberated conditioned mind
and
an unconditioned cessation
```

## Logical determination

The same achieved freedom is determined through three relations:

```text
relation to attachment
    virāga

relation to the remaining afflictions
    prahāṇa

relation to conditioned actuality
    nirodha
```

This is not yet a new metaphysical trinity of substances. It is a differentiated articulation of unconditioned freedom.

## Determinate summary

```text
VAK 6.78 establishes:

1. The three liberation-elements
   are exclusively unconditioned.

2. Virāga is the complete exhaustion
   of attachment.

3. Prahāṇa is the abandonment
   of the other afflictions.

4. Nirodha names cessation
   in relation to conditioned actuality.

5. The Aśaikṣa mind is therefore
   the conditioned liberated mind
   corresponding to an accomplished
   unconditioned cessation.
```
