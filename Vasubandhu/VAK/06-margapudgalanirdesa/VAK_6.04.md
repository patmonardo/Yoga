# VAK 6.04

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

यत्र भिन्नेन तद्बुद्धिरन्यापोहे धिया च तत् ।
घटार्थवत्संवृतिसत्परमार्थसदन्यथा ॥ ६.४ ॥

### IAST

yatra bhinnena tadbuddhir anyāpohe dhiyā ca tat |
ghaṭārthavat saṃvṛtisat paramārthasad anyathā || 6.4 ||

## Padaccheda

| Pada | Form | Determination |
|---|---|---|
| yatra | relative adverb | where; in the case in which |
| bhinnena | instrumental singular of bhinna | when broken; through division or destruction |
| tad-buddhiḥ | nominative singular feminine | the cognition of that |
| anya-apohe | locative singular | when other components are removed |
| dhiyā | instrumental singular feminine | by cognition; through discriminative thought |
| ca | conjunction | and |
| tat | nominative/accusative singular neuter | that |
| ghaṭa-artha-vat | comparative compound | like the meaning or object “pot” |
| saṃvṛti-sat | nominative singular neuter | conventionally real |
| paramārtha-sat | nominative singular neuter | ultimately real |
| anyathā | adverb | otherwise |

## Grammar

The verse defines two kinds of reality through a cognitive test.

```text
yatra
    where

bhinnena
    when the thing is broken

tad-buddhiḥ
    the cognition of that thing
```

The implied sense is that the corresponding cognition no longer occurs.

The second test is:

```text
anya-apohe
    when other constituents are removed

dhiyā
    by cognition or analysis
```

Thus, where the cognition of a thing disappears when the thing is physically divided, or when its constituents are analytically removed, that is conventionally real.

The example is:

```text
ghaṭa-artha-vat
    like the meaning or object “pot”
```

The concluding contrast is compressed:

```text
paramārtha-sat anyathā
    ultimate reality is otherwise
```

That is, where cognition does not disappear under physical division or analytical removal, the object is ultimately real.

## Literal Translation

Where the cognition of that ceases when the thing is broken, and when other constituents are removed by cognition, that is conventionally real, like the meaning “pot.” Ultimate reality is otherwise.

## Philosophical Translation

A thing is conventionally real when the cognition corresponding to it disappears either through the physical dissolution of the composite or through the analytical exclusion of its constituents—as with a pot. What withstands such dissolution and analysis is ultimately real.

## Technical Vocabulary

### saṃvṛti-sat

```text
saṃvṛti-sat
    conventionally real
```

This does not mean sheer illusion or complete non-being. A pot functions, can be used, named, recognized, bought, carried, and broken. Its unity, however, does not remain when the supporting composite is destroyed.

```text
pot intact
    → pot-cognition

pot shattered into fragments
    → pot-cognition disappears
```

Conventional reality is therefore functional and cognizable unity dependent upon composition.

### paramārtha-sat

```text
paramārtha-sat
    ultimately real
```

Ultimate reality is defined here through analytical endurance. It is what remains an object of cognition when composite unity is removed.

```text
paramārtha
    is determined through analysis

not:
    placed beyond analysis
```

Ultimate reality is connected to the dharma as the final object reached by discriminative analysis.

### tad-buddhi

```text
tad-buddhi
    cognition of that
```

The verse asks whether the same cognition continues to apply. Before breakage there is the cognition “pot”; after breakage there are cognitions of shards, fragments, color, and resistance. The cognition “pot” no longer has an object.

### anya-apoha

```text
anya
    other

apoha
    exclusion
    removal
```

The expression refers here to analytical subtraction or exclusion. One may physically break the pot or cognitively remove color, shape, hardness, spatial configuration, and parts. No independent pot remains behind the constituents.

### artha

In ghaṭārthavat, artha is meaning or intended object: the object meant as “pot.”

## Analysis

VAK 6.02 named the Four Truths. VAK 6.03 identified the whole contaminated field as the truth of suffering. VAK 6.04 now asks in what sense the things within this truth-field are real.

The distinction is generated by analysis:

```text
composite object
    ↓ physical division
or
    ↓ analytical exclusion

does the corresponding cognition remain?
```

If not, the object is saṃvṛti-sat. If so, it is paramārtha-sat.

## Conventional reality is not unreality

It would be incorrect to infer:

```text
composite
    therefore nonexistent
```

The pot is conventionally real because it is causally effective, functionally usable, socially designated, and cognitively apprehensible. Its unity is not analytically final.

```text
saṃvṛti-sat
    dependent reality
    constituted unity
    functional appearance
```

## Ultimate reality is not Brahman

Paramārtha-sat is not initially one all-encompassing Absolute. It is the irreducible object yielded through analysis.

```text
composite whole
    analyzed
        ↓
final dharma determinations
```

The Kośa does not say that only one thing is ultimately real. It says that what survives discriminative decomposition is ultimately real.

## Relation to Pure Prajñā

Ultimate reality is disclosed through the discriminative movement:

```text
composite cognition
    ↓
analysis and exclusion
    ↓
irreducible determination
```

Pure Prajñā is the discriminative principle through which constructed unity and irreducible determination become explicit.

```text
paramārtha-sat
    is reality as determined
    under complete discrimination
```

## Logical Determination

The verse distinguishes two forms of unity.

```text
unity through aggregation
    versus
unity through self-determination
```

The conventional object is true within its functional configuration. It fails only when claimed as analytically ultimate.

The distinction is therefore stronger than false versus true:

```text
composite appearance
    saṃvṛti

analytically persistent determination
    paramārtha
```

## Path-of-Knowing Interpretation

The ordinary person apprehends wholes such as person, pot, house, forest, army, and self. The Path asks what remains when the constructed whole is analyzed.

The same procedure that analyzes a pot prepares the analysis of the person:

```text
“self”
    analyzed into
aggregates
spheres
domains
conditioned processes
```

If the cognition of an independent self disappears under analysis, self is conventionally real at most, not ultimately real.

Yet conventional personhood is not nothing. It is the living configuration in which the Path becomes actual.

## Interpretive Note

The verse refuses both naive realism and nihilism:

```text
naive realism:
    the pot exists as an ultimate unit

nihilism:
    the pot does not exist at all
```

Instead:

```text
the pot is conventionally real
because its functional unity is effective

the pot is not ultimately real
because that unity does not survive analysis
```

Saṃvṛti and paramārtha are not two substances. They are two determinations of reality disclosed by analysis.
