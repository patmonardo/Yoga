# VAK 6.07

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

विपर्यासात्तद्विपक्षौ त्रिधात्वाप्तावमलौ च तौ ।
अलोभ आर्यवंशाश्च तेषां तुष्ट्यात्मकास्त्रयः ॥ ६.७ ॥

### IAST

viparyāsāt tad-vipakṣau tri-dhātv-āptāv amalau ca tau |
alobha āryavaṃśāś ca teṣāṃ tuṣṭy-ātmakās trayaḥ || 6.7 ||

## Grammar

The opening refers back to the two defects of VAK 6.06:

```text
atuṣṭi
    discontent

mahecchatā
    great or extensive desire
```

Their opponents are established through reversal:

```text
viparyāsāt tad-vipakṣau
    their two opponents
    are determined by inversion
```

Thus:

```text
atuṣṭi ↔ santuṣṭi
mahecchatā ↔ alpecchatā
```

The dual forms āptau, amalau, and tau all refer to these two qualities. The verse further states that they belong throughout the three domains, may also be uncontaminated, and possess alobha as their intrinsic nature.

The final half introduces the āryavaṃśas, three of which have contentment as their nature. The fourth is completed in VAK 6.08.

## Literal Translation

By inversion, their two opponents belong to the three domains and may also be uncontaminated. They are non-greed. There are also the lineages of the noble ones; among these, three have contentment as their nature.

## Philosophical Translation

The opposed virtues are obtained by reversing discontent and expansive desire: contentment and fewness of desires. Their scope extends throughout the three domains and even into the uncontaminated Path, and their intrinsic determination is non-appropriation. These virtues are further articulated as the noble lineages, three of which consist in contentment.

## Technical Vocabulary

### viparyāsa

Here viparyāsa means reversal or inversion. It does not denote the standard cognitive inversions. The verse derives the counter-determinations by reversing the structures defined in VAK 6.06.

### vipakṣa

Vipakṣa is opponent or counterforce. Santuṣṭi counteracts atuṣṭi; alpecchatā counteracts mahecchatā.

### santuṣṭi

Contentment concerns what has already been obtained. It means that possession does not initiate further quantitative demand.

### alpecchatā

Fewness of desires concerns what has not been obtained. It is not merely owning little but the restriction of acquisitive projection toward the unpossessed field.

### alobha

Alobha is non-greed or non-appropriation. Contentment and fewness of desires are distinct functions of this one wholesome root.

### tri-dhātv-āpta

The two virtues may belong to the desire, form, and formless domains.

### amala

The two may also be uncontaminated and integrated into the supramundane Path.

### āryavaṃśa

The noble lineages are not biological lineages but transmitted modes of noble practice. Three concern contentment with robes, food, and lodging; the fourth concerns the Path activity completed in VAK 6.08.

## Analysis

VAK 6.06 gave the negative preparation: freedom from discontent and great desire. VAK 6.07 supplies their positive determinations:

```text
contentment
fewness of desires
```

At the functional level they differ:

```text
santuṣṭi
    concerns what is obtained

alpecchatā
    concerns what is not obtained
```

At the level of intrinsic nature they are one:

```text
alobha
```

The same wholesome root is specified through two object-relations:

```text
alobha toward the obtained
    appears as contentment

alobha toward the unpossessed
    appears as fewness of desires
```

VAK 6.06 established the cycle of acquisition and increase. VAK 6.07 breaks it at both points:

```text
alpecchatā
    interrupts desire before acquisition

santuṣṭi
    interrupts desire after acquisition
```

## Logical Determination

VAK 6.06 gave two negative infinities:

```text
atuṣṭi
    endless quantitative increase

mahecchatā
    endless extensive acquisition
```

VAK 6.07 supplies their limits:

```text
santuṣṭi
    limit placed upon quantity

alpecchatā
    limit placed upon extension
```

Both are determinations of alobha, freedom from appropriative infinity.

## Interpretive Note

The solution to endless desire is not the correct quantity of possession. It is transformation of the appropriative relation itself.

```text
use
    without
appropriative accumulation
```

This is the empirical simplicity preserved by the noble lineage.

## Determinate Summary

The opposites of discontent and great desire are contentment and fewness of desires. They belong throughout the three domains, may be worldly or uncontaminated, and have non-greed as their intrinsic nature. Three noble lineages have contentment as their nature.
