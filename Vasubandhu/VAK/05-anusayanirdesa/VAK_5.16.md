# VAK_5.16 — Why the Uncontaminated Is Not an Object of Attachment, Hatred, Conceit, or Overvaluation

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

```sanskrit
न रागस्तस्य वर्ज्यत्वान्न द्वेषोऽनपकारतः ।
न मानो न परामर्शौ शान्तशुद्ध्यग्रभावतः ॥ ५.१६ ॥
```

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

```text
na rāgas tasya varjyatvān na dveṣo 'napakārataḥ |
na māno na parāmarśau śānta-śuddhy-agrabhāvataḥ || 5.16 ||
```

The local source gives:

```text
na rāgastasya varjyatvāt na dveṣo 'napakārataḥ /
na māno na parāmarśau śāntaśuddhyagrabhāvataḥ // VAkK_5.16 //
```

The verse continues the argument of VAK_5.14–5.15. Although cessation and path can be objects of certain afflicted cognitions, they cannot serve as objects for every anuśaya.

## 4. Grammar

| Term | Form | Working meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `na` | negative particle | not |
| `rāgaḥ` | nominative masculine singular | attachment, passion |
| `tasya` | genitive masculine/neuter singular | toward it, of it |
| `varjyatvāt` | ablative singular abstract noun | because it is to be abandoned or avoided |
| `dveṣaḥ` | nominative masculine singular | hatred, hostility |
| `anapakārataḥ` | ablative singular | because it does no harm |
| `mānaḥ` | nominative masculine singular | conceit, comparative self-measurement |
| `parāmarśau` | nominative masculine dual | the two overvaluations or adherences |
| `śānta-śuddhi-agrabhāvataḥ` | ablative compound | because of being peaceful, pure, and supreme |

The repeated ablatives give four reasons for four exclusions.

---

## 5. Literal translation

> Attachment does not take it as object, because it is to be abandoned; hatred does not, because it causes no harm. Conceit does not, nor do the two overvaluations, because it is peaceful, pure, and supreme.

---

## 6. Philosophical translation

> Although cessation and the path can become objects of false view, doubt, and ignorance, they cannot become objects of every affliction. Attachment does not properly arise toward them because attachment itself is precisely what is to be relinquished. Hatred cannot arise toward them because they inflict no injury. Conceit cannot measure itself through them, and the two forms of overvaluation cannot mistake them as inferior yet supreme, because cessation and path are genuinely peaceful, pure, and unsurpassed within the liberative order.

The verse therefore narrows the field established in VAK_5.14:

```text
uncontaminated object
    can be misconceived

but
    cannot support every mode of affliction
```

# 7. The asymmetry of the six anuśayas

VAK_5.14 identified six anuśayas directed toward uncontaminated objects:

```text
cessation:
    mithyā-dṛṣṭi
    vicikitsā
    avidyā

path:
    mithyā-dṛṣṭi
    vicikitsā
    avidyā
```

VAK_5.16 explains why the others are excluded:

```text
rāga
    excluded

dveṣa
    excluded

māna
    excluded

dṛṣṭi-parāmarśa
    excluded

śīlavrata-parāmarśa
    excluded
```

The chapter is thus constructing not merely a list of possible object-relations, but a compatibility matrix:

```text
anuśaya type
    × object type
    × structural possibility
```

---

# 8. `Na rāgaḥ`

```text
rāga
    attachment
    appropriation
    affective adhesion
```

The verse gives:

```text
varjyatvāt
    because it is to be abandoned
```

The exact referent and argumentative force should be checked in the Bhāṣya. The secure first-pass point is that attachment and the uncontaminated stand in a relation of exclusion.

The liberative object is not an object to be possessed in the same mode as conditioned goods.

```text
appropriative relation
    contradicts
liberative function
```

Thus even attachment to liberation, if constituted as grasping, would reproduce the structure that liberation removes.

---

# 9. `Na dveṣaḥ`

```text
dveṣa
    hatred
    hostility
    aversion
```

The reason is:

```text
anapakārataḥ
    because it does no injury
```

Hatred presupposes an object represented as harmful, injurious, or obstructive.

But cessation and path are not injurious:

```text
object does not harm
    therefore
hatred lacks its adequate basis
```

This is an exact object-affliction dependency.

The verse does not treat hatred as a free-floating force. It requires a determinate representation of injury.

---

# 10. `Na mānaḥ`

```text
māna
    conceit
    comparative self-measurement
```

Conceit requires a field in which the appropriated self can measure itself as:

```text
superior
equal
inferior
```

But cessation and path do not function as ordinary comparative possessions.

They cannot coherently become grounds for self-exaltation in the same way as status, knowledge, discipline, or attainment conceived as mine.

The exact Abhidharma rationale remains for the Bhāṣya, but the structural point is clear:

```text
what dissolves appropriation
    cannot consistently ground
appropriative comparison
```

---

# 11. `Na parāmarśau`

The two `parāmarśas` are:

```text
dṛṣṭi-parāmarśa
    overvaluation of views

śīlavrata-parāmarśa
    overvaluation of disciplines and observances
```

Their exclusion is explained by:

```text
śānta
    peaceful

śuddhi
    pure

agra
    supreme
```

The two overvaluations arise by falsely taking something deficient as highest, pure, or liberating.

But cessation and path are not falsely elevated.

They actually possess the qualities that the overvaluations wrongly project onto inferior objects.

This is the decisive contrast:

```text
parāmarśa
    inferior object
        falsely posited as supreme

cessation and path
    genuinely peaceful, pure, and supreme
```

Therefore the error-form cannot arise in the same manner.

---

# 12. A positive dialectic of exclusion

The verse does not merely say that four afflictions are absent.

It gives a positive reason for each absence:

```text
rāga
    excluded by abandonability

dveṣa
    excluded by non-injury

māna
    excluded by non-comparability

parāmarśa
    excluded by genuine peace, purity, and supremacy
```

This is a positive dialectic because each negation is grounded in a determinate contradiction between:

```text
affliction-form
and
object-character
```

The exclusion is therefore intelligible, not merely enumerative.

---

# 13. Valid object, invalid relation

VAK_5.14 established:

```text
valid liberative object
    can still be taken
    through false cognition
```

VAK_5.16 now adds:

```text
not every false relation
    is structurally possible
    toward that object
```

Thus the Kośa distinguishes:

```text
object validity
relation validity
relation compatibility
```

This is a powerful scientific refinement.

A true object does not automatically guarantee true cognition, but its own character still limits the forms of error that can coherently arise around it.

# 16. Kārikā–Bhāṣya opening

The kārikā securely establishes:

```text
rāga is excluded
dveṣa is excluded
māna is excluded
the two parāmarśas are excluded
```

and supplies reasons:

```text
varjyatva
anapakāra
śāntatva
śuddhatva
agrabhāva
```

The Bhāṣya should clarify:

```text
1. the exact referent of tasya
2. the precise force of varjyatvāt
3. why conceit is excluded under the final causal phrase
4. how the two parāmarśas relate specifically to genuine peace, purity, and supremacy
5. whether cessation and path are treated identically throughout the explanation
```

The independent first-pass result remains clear:

```text
an uncontaminated object
    can be misconceived

but its determinate character
    excludes several forms of afflictive relation
```
