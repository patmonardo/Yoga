# VAK 5.50

## Sanskrit

प्रदाश उपनाहश्च विहिंसा चेति रागजौ ।
मायामदौ प्रतिघजे उपनाहविहिंसने ॥ ५.५० ॥

## IAST

pradāśa upanāhaś ca vihiṃsā ceti rāgajau /
māyā-madau pratighaje upanāha-vihiṃsane // 5.50 //

## Padaccheda

pradāśaḥ | upanāhaḥ | ca | vihiṃsā | ca | iti | rāga-jau | māyā-madau | pratigha-je | upanāha-vihiṃsane

## Grammar

- **pradāśaḥ** — nominative singular masculine, “spite,” “malicious obstinacy,” or “the will to expose and attack another’s fault.”
- **upanāhaḥ** — nominative singular masculine, “rancor,” “harbored hostility,” literally a binding-on or fastening of resentment.
- **vihiṃsā** — nominative singular feminine, “harmfulness,” “cruelty,” the disposition to injure.
- **iti** — closes the enumeration of the six **kleśa-malas** begun in VAK 5.49.
- **rāga-jau** — nominative dual masculine, “the two born of attachment.”
- **māyā-madau** — nominative dual masculine: **māyā** and **mada**.
- **pratigha-je** — nominative dual feminine, “the two born of hostility/resistance.”
- **upanāha-vihiṃsane** — nominative dual feminine: rancor and harmfulness.

The syntactic structure is:

```text
rāgajau māyāmadau
pratighaje upanāhavihiṃsane
```

That is:

```text
māyā and mada are born of rāga;
upanāha and vihiṃsā are born of pratigha.
```

The roots of **pradāśa** and **śāṭhya** are deferred to VAK 5.51.

## Literal Translation

Spite, rancor, and harmfulness—thus [the six stains are completed]. Deception and intoxicated pride are the two born of attachment; rancor and harmfulness are the two born of hostility.

## Philosophical Translation

The six afflictive stains are completed by spite, harbored resentment, and harmfulness. Of these stains, deceptive appearance and intoxicated self-elevation arise from appropriation, whereas rancor and the will to injure arise from resistance and hostility.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.49 introduced six **kleśa-malas**:

```text
māyā
śāṭhya
mada
pradāśa
upanāha
vihiṃsā
```

VAK 5.50 completes the list and begins their derivation from root afflictions.

The first division is:

```text
rāga
    → māyā
    → mada

pratigha
    → upanāha
    → vihiṃsā
```

The verse therefore distinguishes two ways in which a root affliction stains practical appearance.

From **rāga**, the subject distorts reality so as to preserve appropriation:

```text
māyā
    falsifies appearance

mada
    falsifies self-measure
```

From **pratigha**, the subject stabilizes negation against another:

```text
upanāha
    retains hostility

vihiṃsā
    actualizes hostility as injury
```

Thus the stains are not random defects. Each is a determinate practical form of a more universal root relation.

## Technical Vocabulary

### Kleśa-mala

A **mala** is a stain, contamination, or defilement.

The term marks the point at which affliction no longer remains merely latent or internally affective. It colors the way the subject appears, acts, and relates.

```text
kleśa
    root contradiction

mala
    the coloring of manifestation by that contradiction
```

### Māyā

Here **māyā** means deception or false display.

It does not mean cosmic appearance in the later Vedāntic sense. It is the production of an appearance designed to conceal the actual determination of the subject.

Working translation:

```text
māyā
    = deceptive presentation
```

### Mada

From √mad, “to be exhilarated, intoxicated, proud.”

**Mada** is an intoxication with one’s own advantages, qualities, status, vitality, or attainment.

It is generated by attachment because the subject appropriates a determination as “mine” and then becomes inflated by possession of it.

```text
appropriation
    ↓
self-elevation through what is appropriated
```

### Upanāha

From **upa-ā-√nah**, “to bind on,” “to fasten upon.”

Upanāha is not the first arising of anger. It is hostility retained and bound to the continuum.

Working translation:

```text
upanāha
    = rancor / harbored hostility
```

### Vihiṃsā

From **vi-√hiṃs**, “to injure, harm, violate.”

Vihiṃsā is the disposition toward injury. It is hostility passing from retained negation into an active destructive orientation.

Working translation:

```text
vihiṃsā
    = harmfulness / cruelty
```

### Pradāśa

The precise nuance should be checked closely against the Bhāṣya.

The term is commonly associated with spite, vindictiveness, or malicious insistence upon another’s faults.

Unlike upanāha, which retains hostility, pradāśa appears to involve the active projection or exposure of blame.

## Doctrinal Determination

The verse establishes that derivative stains are classified by their root source.

This yields a doctrinal principle:

```text
same manifest class
    may contain
members generated by different roots
```

The six stains form one class because they stain conduct and appearance. Yet their causal derivation differs.

The two attachment-born stains preserve an appropriated self-world relation:

```text
māyā
    protects acquisition through false appearance

mada
    enjoys acquisition as elevated selfhood
```

The two hostility-born stains stabilize and externalize negation:

```text
upanāha
    preserves the hostile determination through time

vihiṃsā
    directs that hostility toward injury
```

This is crucial for the Path. Similar outward conduct may require different discriminations because the manifest stain is not eliminated merely by suppressing its appearance. Its root relation must be known.

## Logical Determination

The verse distinguishes the universal root from its practical specifications.

```text
root relation
    ↓ specification
stained mode of appearance or action
```

For rāga:

```text
appropriation
    → concealment of truth
    → inflation of self
```

For pratigha:

```text
negation of the other
    → retention of hostility
    → destructive action
```

The two pairs display different internal movements.

Attachment moves inward toward possession and self-enjoyment:

```text
object appropriated
    ↓
appearance manipulated
    ↓
self magnified
```

Hostility moves outward toward fixation and injury:

```text
other negated
    ↓
negation retained
    ↓
negation enacted
```

Thus the stains disclose the practical direction of their roots.

## Path-Rationale

This verse contributes directly to the rationale of the Path.

The practitioner must discriminate not only:

```text
what state is manifest?
```

but also:

```text
what root relation is expressing itself here?
```

A deceptive presentation may look like a problem of speech or social conduct, yet its root may be attachment to gain, status, or self-image.

Rancor may look like mere recollection, yet it is hostility preserved as identity through time.

The diagnostic movement is therefore:

```text
manifest stain
    ↓
functional analysis
    ↓
root affliction
    ↓
corresponding path-counteraction
```

This is why the classification reveals the Path: it converts moral description into discriminative causal knowledge.

## Organon Note

VAK 5.50 supplies a useful generative schema:

```text
RootAffliction
    generates
DerivativeStain
```

But the generation is typed by direction:

```text
Rāga
    = appropriative orientation

Pratigha
    = resistant-hostile orientation
```

Their products preserve that direction:

```text
Māyā and Mada
    preserve appropriation

Upanāha and Vihiṃsā
    preserve negation
```

For the Platform, a derivative determination should therefore encode both:

```text
its manifest function
its generating root
```

The same surface category—“stain”—does not erase causal difference.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. How precisely does the Bhāṣya define **pradāśa**, and how does it distinguish it from **upanāha**?
2. Why are **māyā** and **mada** both generated from rāga rather than from ignorance or conceit respectively?
3. Does **māyā** protect gain, reputation, pleasure, or all appropriated determinations generally?
4. Is **upanāha** defined primarily by duration, recollection, or renewed activation of hostility?
5. Does **vihiṃsā** mean an intention to harm, delight in harm, or the absence of compassion?
6. Why are the causal derivations of **śāṭhya** and **pradāśa** postponed to the following verse?
7. Does Vasubandhu accept these derivations as intrinsic causal relations or as Vaibhāṣika classificatory conventions?
