# VAK 5.56

## Sanskrit

दौर्मनस्येन काङ्क्षा अन्ये सौमनस्येन कामजाः ।
सर्वेऽप्युपेक्षया स्वैः स्वैर्यथाभूम्यूर्ध्वभूमिकाः ॥ ५.५६ ॥

## IAST

daurmanasyena kāṅkṣā anye saumanasyena kāmajāḥ /
sarve 'py upekṣayā svaiḥ svair yathā-bhūmy ūrdhva-bhūmikāḥ // 5.56 //

## Padaccheda

daurmanasyena | kāṅkṣā | anye | saumanasyena | kāma-jāḥ | sarve | api | upekṣayā | svaiḥ | svaiḥ | yathā-bhūmi | ūrdhva-bhūmikāḥ

## Grammar

- **daurmanasyena** — instrumental singular neuter, “with mental distress,” “with dejection.”
- **kāṅkṣā** — nominative singular feminine, “doubt,” especially afflictive uncertainty concerning what is true, effective, or to be practiced.
- **anye** — nominative plural masculine, “the others,” referring to the remaining afflictions relevant to the present distribution.
- **saumanasyena** — instrumental singular neuter, “with mental gladness.”
- **kāmajāḥ** — nominative plural masculine, “arising in the desire-domain,” “born of kāma.”
- **sarve api** — “all also,” extending the compatibility rule to every affliction under consideration.
- **upekṣayā** — instrumental singular feminine, “with equanimous or neutral feeling.”
- **svaiḥ svaiḥ** — distributive instrumental plural, “with their own respective [feelings].”
- **yathā-bhūmi** — indeclinable adverbial compound, “according to the domain,” “as appropriate to each level.”
- **ūrdhva-bhūmikāḥ** — nominative plural masculine, “those belonging to the higher domains.”

## Literal Translation

Doubt is associated with mental distress; the other afflictions arising in the desire-domain are associated with mental gladness. All are also associated with equanimity. Those belonging to the higher domains are associated, respectively, with the feelings appropriate to their own levels.

## Philosophical Translation

Afflictive doubt arises together with mental distress, while the remaining desire-domain afflictions may arise with mental gladness. Yet every affliction can also inhabit an equanimous affective field. In the higher domains, afflictions combine only with the feeling-modes available at their respective levels.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.55 began the compatibility map between afflictions and feelings:

```text
rāga
    → pleasure and gladness

dveṣa
    → pain and distress

moha
    → all five feelings

false view
    → gladness and distress
```

VAK 5.56 completes that map:

```text
kāṅkṣā
    → mental distress

other desire-domain afflictions
    → mental gladness

all afflictions
    → equanimity

higher-domain afflictions
    → feelings available in their own domains
```

The decisive addition is:

```text
equanimity
    does not entail
absence of affliction
```

The affective middle can still be occupied by attachment, ignorance, view, doubt, conceit, or other afflicted determinations.

## Technical Vocabulary

### Kāṅkṣā

**Kāṅkṣā** is afflictive doubt or uncertainty.

It is not free inquiry. It is a suspended practical-cognitive state in which the continuum cannot determine itself regarding truth, path, or action.

```text
alternative possibilities
    remain unresolved
        ↓
practical determination is inhibited
```

Its conjunction with **daurmanasya** is intelligible because unresolved doubt produces mental unease rather than stable gladness.

### Daurmanasya

Mental distress, dejection, or disagreeable mental feeling.

It is distinct from doubt itself:

```text
kāṅkṣā
    = indeterminate cognitive orientation

daurmanasya
    = painful mental tone
```

The two arise together but must not be identified.

### Kāmaja

“Born in the desire-domain.”

This is a domain predicate, not a biological or merely sensual description.

```text
kāmaja
    = capable of arising within the desire-domain field
```

The term identifies the structural range within which the affliction and its compatible feelings occur.

### Upekṣā

Equanimous or neutral feeling.

Here **upekṣā** is one of the five feeling-faculties. It does not yet mean perfected spiritual equanimity.

```text
affective neutrality
    ≠
liberated equipoise
```

This distinction is essential. Neutral feeling may be accompanied by non-discrimination, latent appropriation, conceit, or false view.

### Yathābhūmi

“According to the domain.”

The possible feeling-affliction conjunctions are constrained by the constitution of each domain.

```text
available feelings of a domain
    constrain
possible afflictive conjunctions
```

### Ūrdhvabhūmika

An affliction belonging to a higher level or domain.

Higher does not mean liberated. It means that the affliction has a subtler range of occurrence.

## Doctrinal Determination

VAK 5.56 blocks a common error:

```text
absence of pleasure and pain
    mistaken for
absence of affliction
```

All afflictions can be associated with **upekṣā**. Therefore an affectively calm state may remain cognitively or volitionally afflicted.

This gives three distinct possibilities:

```text
pleasant feeling + affliction
painful feeling + affliction
neutral feeling + affliction
```

The Path cannot determine purity from affective tone alone.

### Doubt and distress

Doubt is associated with mental distress because its operation is suspension:

```text
not affirmed
not denied
not resolved
    ↓
mental unease
```

Doubt therefore obstructs movement not by choosing the wrong path but by preventing determinate commitment.

### Gladness and desire-domain affliction

The remaining desire-domain afflictions may accompany mental gladness. This is doctrinally significant because affliction may present itself as agreeable, successful, or satisfying.

```text
pleased with possession
pleased with status
pleased with victory
pleased with a doctrine
pleased with concealment
```

Gladness does not certify truth or wholesomeness.

### Equanimity and universal compatibility

The phrase **sarve 'py upekṣayā** universalizes neutral conjunction.

```text
all afflictions
    may occur with
neutral feeling
```

This means that some afflictions persist below the threshold of vivid emotional disturbance.

They may appear as:

```text
quiet assumption
stable bias
unexamined certainty
subtle attachment
neutralized conceit
```

### Higher domains

Afflictions of higher domains combine with the feelings structurally available at those domains.

The verse therefore maintains two truths at once:

```text
higher domains refine affect

but

higher domains do not abolish affliction
```

Elevation changes the mode of conjunction, not necessarily the root orientation.

## Logical Determination

VAK 5.56 completes a compatibility matrix constrained by domain.

```text
AfflictionType
    × FeelingType
    × Domain
        → permitted conjunction
```

The new rule is not merely:

```text
affliction A accompanies feeling B
```

but:

```text
affliction A may accompany feeling B
only where domain D makes B available
```

Thus the compatibility relation is domain-indexed.

### Neutrality as logical middle

Equanimity is the middle between pleasant and painful feeling:

```text
pleasant
    ↔ neutral ↔
painful
```

But the middle is not a synthesis that has overcome the extremes. It is immediate neutrality.

Therefore:

```text
upekṣā
    may suspend affective polarity
without sublating afflictive orientation
```

This is a crucial distinction between:

```text
neutralization
    and
liberation
```

### Domain-relative possibility

The higher-domain rule expresses modal restriction:

```text
what can arise
    depends upon
what the domain can support
```

The domain is therefore not an external location. It is a structured field of possible conjunctions.

## Path Determination

The practical lesson is exact:

```text
calmness
    is evidence of calmness
not yet evidence of knowledge
```

A practitioner must inspect not only intense pleasure or distress but also apparently neutral states.

The Path-map therefore asks:

```text
What is the feeling-tone?

What afflictive orientation is compatible with it?

In which domain or level is this event occurring?
```

Possible markers are:

```text
distress + unresolved alternatives
    → inspect doubt

gladness + appropriation or self-confirmation
    → inspect desire-domain affliction

neutrality + non-discrimination
    → inspect ignorance

neutrality + subtle retention
    → inspect attachment

neutrality + elevated self-measure
    → inspect conceit
```

This is why the Path must remain centered on the Truth of Origin. A calm or elevated manifestation does not reveal its own root transparently.

```text
manifest feeling
    ↓ discrimination
compatible affliction
    ↓ tracing
root and domain
    ↓
mode of abandonment
```

## Organon Note

VAK 5.56 requires a three-coordinate state model:

```text
CognitiveEvent
    hasFeeling
    hasAfflictiveOrientation
    occursInDomain
```

No one coordinate determines the other two.

```text
same feeling
    may support different afflictions

same affliction
    may support different feelings

same affliction
    changes its possible conjunctions by domain
```

Suggested constraint pattern:

```text
PermittedConjunction
    hasAfflictionType
    hasFeelingType
    hasDomain
```

The phrase **yathābhūmi** should be represented as a domain-relative validation rule rather than a universal property of the affliction.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. Which precise afflictions are included under **anye kāmajāḥ**?
2. Is **kāṅkṣā** here fully equivalent to **vicikitsā**, or does the Bhāṣya mark a nuance?
3. Why is doubt restricted to **daurmanasya** rather than also associated with equanimity in the first half, given the universal rule in the second half?
4. Does **sarve 'pi upekṣayā** include every root and derivative affliction without exception?
5. Which feelings are available at each higher domain under **svaiḥ svair yathābhūmi**?
6. Does the Bhāṣya distinguish ordinary neutral feeling from cultivated equanimity at this point?
7. How does this verse coordinate with the earlier doctrine that higher-domain afflictions are ethically indeterminate?
8. Does the domain-relative rule concern only feeling compatibility, or also the strength and mode of the affliction itself?
