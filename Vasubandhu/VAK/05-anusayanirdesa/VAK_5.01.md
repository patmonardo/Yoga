# VAK_5.01 — The Anuśayas as the Root of Existence

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

```sanskrit
मूलं भवस्यानुशयाः षड् रागः प्रतिघस्तथा ।
मानोऽविद्या च दृष्टिश्च विचिकित्सा च ते पुनः ॥ ५.१ ॥
```

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

```text
mūlaṃ bhavasyānuśayāḥ ṣaḍ rāgaḥ pratighas tathā |
māno 'vidyā ca dṛṣṭiś ca vicikitsā ca te punaḥ || 5.1 ||
```

Local source:

```text
mūlaṃ bhavasyānuśayāḥ ṣaḍrāgaḥ pratighastathā /
māno 'vidyā ca dṛṣṭiśca vicikitsā ca te punaḥ // VAkK_5.1 //
```

The final words:

```text
te punaḥ
    these, again
```

carry the enumeration forward into VAK_5.02, where the six are counted as seven through the division of `rāga`.

## 4. Grammar

| Term | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `mūlam` | nominative neuter singular | root, basis, originating ground |
| `bhavasya` | genitive masculine singular | of existence, of becoming |
| `anuśayāḥ` | nominative masculine plural | anuśayas, underlying afflictive dispositions |
| `ṣaṭ` | indeclinable numeral | six |
| `rāgaḥ` | nominative masculine singular | attachment, passion |
| `pratighaḥ` | nominative masculine singular | resistance, hostility, aversion |
| `tathā` | adverb | likewise, and also |
| `mānaḥ` | nominative masculine singular | conceit, comparative self-estimation |
| `avidyā` | nominative feminine singular | non-knowing, ignorance |
| `ca` | conjunction | and |
| `dṛṣṭiḥ` | nominative feminine singular | view |
| `vicikitsā` | nominative feminine singular | doubt, irresolution |
| `te` | nominative masculine plural | they, those |
| `punaḥ` | adverb | again, further, in turn |

The opening is an equational judgment:

```text
anuśayāḥ = bhavasya mūlam
```

Although `mūlam` is singular and `anuśayāḥ` plural, Sanskrit permits the predicate noun to state the single collective function of the many anuśayas:

```text
the anuśayas, taken together,
    are the root of existence
```

The verse then enumerates six singular names under the plural class.

---

## 5. Literal Translation

> The anuśayas are the root of existence. They are six: attachment, resistance, conceit, non-knowing, view, and doubt. These, again—

The suspended ending is intentional. Its completion belongs to VAK_5.02.

---

## 6. Philosophical Translation

> The underlying afflictive dispositions constitute the root from which conditioned existence is perpetuated. Six fundamental forms are initially distinguished: attachment, hostile resistance, conceit, non-knowing, view, and doubt. These six must, however, be differentiated further.

---

## 7. Technical Vocabulary

### `Bhava`

```text
bhava
    existence
    becoming
    conditioned continuity of existence
```

Here `bhava` is not bare Being. It is the conditioned continuation of existence whose root is being sought.

The verse therefore asks for the internal ground of continued becoming:

```text
What sustains bhava?

anuśaya
```

### `Mūla`

```text
mūla
    root
    originating basis
    sustaining ground
```

The root is not merely the first event in a temporal chain. It names that from which the system repeatedly grows.

```text
root
    → renewed manifestation
    → renewed activity
    → renewed existence
```

### `Anuśaya`

For the first kārikā pass, `anuśaya` should remain visible as a technical term.

Provisional range:

```text
anuśaya
    underlying disposition
    latent afflictive tendency
    persistent afflictive adherence
```

The term must not yet be reduced simply to:

```text
kleśa
```

The chapter title is `Anuśayanirdeśa`, and the Bhāṣya pass must determine precisely how Vasubandhu relates:

```text
anuśaya
kleśa
paryavasthāna
upakleśa
```

The present verse establishes only the primary thesis:

```text
anuśayāḥ
    are the root of bhava
```

---

## 8. The Six Anuśayas

### 1. `Rāga`

```text
rāga
    attachment
    passion
    affective appropriation
```

The disposition toward an object as something to be acquired, retained, enjoyed, or made one's own.

### 2. `Pratigha`

```text
pratigha
    resistance
    aversion
    hostility
```

Literally suggestive of striking against or opposing. It is the negative orientation toward what is encountered as obstructive, injurious, or unwanted.

### 3. `Māna`

```text
māna
    conceit
    self-measurement
    comparative estimation
```

`Māna` is not exhausted by vanity. It is the activity of measuring self against other under the presupposition of a substantial self-position.

### 4. `Avidyā`

```text
avidyā
    non-knowing
    ignorance
```

The privative ground through which the field is not known according to its actual determinations.

### 5. `Dṛṣṭi`

```text
dṛṣṭi
    view
    doctrinal or interpretive position
```

Here `dṛṣṭi` is not vision in general. It names determinate views capable of binding cognition to a false articulation of the field.

### 6. `Vicikitsā`

```text
vicikitsā
    doubt
    irresolution
    wavering inquiry
```

Not inquiry as such, but unresolved oscillation that obstructs determinate recognition and commitment to the path.

---

## 9. Doctrinal Determination

The opening of Chapter 5 changes the level of explanation.

Chapter 4 established:

```text
karma
    activity
    its modes
    its paths
    its maturation
```

Chapter 5 asks what repeatedly generates and directs karmic activity within conditioned existence:

```text
anuśaya
    → kleśa-manifestation
    → karma
    → bhava
```

The precise relations will require the Bhāṣya, but the chapter begins by locating the root beneath overt activity.

The sixfold list is not yet final. VAK_5.02 will divide `rāga` according to its domains and thereby produce the conventional count of seven anuśayas.

---

## 10. Logical Determination

The verse has three movements:

```text
1. Ground judgment
    anuśayāḥ = bhavasya mūlam

2. Initial classification
    ṣaṭ

3. Enumeration
    rāga
    pratigha
    māna
    avidyā
    dṛṣṭi
    vicikitsā
```

The final `te punaḥ` prevents the sixfold list from being mistaken for an absolutely finished classification.

```text
unity of ground
    ↓
plurality of fundamental determinations
    ↓
further internal differentiation
```

The opening scientific act is therefore not merely naming afflictions. It derives a classified plurality from a single functional principle:

```text
that which roots bhava
```

---

## 11. Interpretive Note

This chapter begins with a powerful reversal of perspective.

In the Karma chapter, activity appeared as the producer of the differentiated world:

```text
karmajaṃ lokavaicitryam
    the diversity of the world is born from karma
```

Now the inquiry moves behind activity:

```text
mūlaṃ bhavasyānuśayāḥ
    the anuśayas are the root of existence
```

The new question is not only:

```text
What does activity produce?
```

but:

```text
What produces the recurrent orientation of activity itself?
```

For the Organon, this is immediately relevant to path-discrimination, but the first-pass determination should remain restrained:

```text
anuśaya
    persistent conditioning of orientation

mārga
    systematic counter-determination and removal
```

The full reflective account belongs to VAKB.
