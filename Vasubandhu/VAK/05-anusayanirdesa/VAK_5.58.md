# VAK 5.58

## Sanskrit

माया शाठ्यमथो म्रक्षो मिद्धं चोभयथा मदः ।
सुखाभ्यां सर्वगोपेक्षा चत्वार्यन्यानि पञ्चभिः ॥ ५.५८ ॥

## IAST

māyā śāṭhyam atho mrakṣo middhaṃ cobhayathā madaḥ /
sukhābhyāṃ sarvagopekṣā catvāry anyāni pañcabhiḥ // 5.58 //

## Padaccheda

māyā | śāṭhyam | atho | mrakṣaḥ | middham | ca | ubhayathā | madaḥ | sukhābhyām | sarvagā | upekṣā | catvāri | anyāni | pañcabhiḥ

## Grammar

- **māyā** — nominative singular feminine, “deception,” falsification of outward appearance.
- **śāṭhyam** — nominative singular neuter, “crookedness,” falsification of intention.
- **atho** — connective particle, “and further,” “then also.”
- **mrakṣaḥ** — nominative singular masculine, “concealment,” especially concealment of one’s fault.
- **middham** — nominative singular neuter, “sleep,” a derivative affliction that withdraws cognitive availability.
- **ca** — “and.”
- **ubhayathā** — indeclinable, “in both ways,” here with both mental gladness and mental distress.
- **madaḥ** — nominative singular masculine, “intoxication,” “conceitful exhilaration.”
- **sukhābhyām** — instrumental dual, “with the two pleasant feelings,” bodily pleasure and mental gladness.
- **sarvagā** — nominative singular feminine, “going everywhere,” “universally accompanying.” It qualifies **upekṣā**.
- **upekṣā** — nominative singular feminine, equanimous or neutral feeling.
- **catvāri anyāni** — “the four others,” namely āhrīkya, anapatrāpya, styāna, and auddhatya.
- **pañcabhiḥ** — instrumental plural, “with the five,” referring to all five feeling-faculties.

## Literal Translation

Deception, crookedness, concealment, and sleep occur in both ways. Intoxication is associated with the two pleasant feelings. Equanimity accompanies all. The other four are associated with all five.

## Philosophical Translation

Deception, crooked intention, concealment of fault, and cognitive sleep may arise either with mental gladness or with mental distress. Intoxicated self-estimation is conjoined with bodily pleasure or mental gladness. Equanimity is universally compatible with the afflictive field. The four remaining pervasive derivative afflictions—shamelessness, lack of moral concern, heaviness, and restlessness—can accompany every form of feeling.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.55–5.57 distributed the root and derivative afflictions according to feeling. VAK 5.58 completes the matrix.

```text
māyā
śāṭhya
mrakṣa
middha
    → saumanasya or daurmanasya

mada
    → sukha or saumanasya

upekṣā
    → compatible with all afflictions

āhrīkya
anapatrāpya
styāna
auddhatya
    → all five feelings
```

The verse therefore distinguishes four kinds of affective range:

```text
bipolar mental range
pleasant range
universal neutral compatibility
complete five-feeling range
```

The Path-map is now not merely a list of names. It is a constraint system governing which affective forms can coexist with which afflictive determinations.

## Technical Vocabulary

### Ubhayathā

**Ubhayathā**, “in both ways,” refers here to mental gladness and mental distress.

The Bhāṣya explains that one may deceive, act crookedly, conceal a fault, or fall asleep either with a glad mind or with a distressed mind.

```text
the same afflictive function
    can be enacted
under opposite mental tones
```

Thus feeling-tone alone does not identify the function.

### Mada

**Mada** is intoxication through one’s own qualities, status, vitality, possessions, or attainments.

Its affinity with pleasure is structurally intelligible:

```text
experienced excellence
    ↓
pleasant self-relation
    ↓
inflated self-measure
```

Mada does not merely enjoy pleasure. It converts agreeable self-experience into excessive valuation.

### Sarvagā Upekṣā

The compound resolves as:

```text
sarvagā upekṣā
    = equanimity that goes with all
```

Equanimity is not itself affliction, but no affliction is excluded from conjunction with it.

This is doctrinally decisive:

```text
lack of affective intensity
    ≠
lack of affliction
```

### The Four Others

The “four others” are:

```text
āhrīkya
anapatrāpya
styāna
auddhatya
```

They accompany all five feeling-faculties because the first two belong to the great unwholesome ground and the latter two to the great afflictive ground.

Their function is pervasive rather than tied to one affective polarity.

## Doctrinal Determination

### Deceptive functions are affectively reversible

Māyā, śāṭhya, mrakṣa, and middha can occur under either gladness or distress.

```text
glad deception
    and
distressed deception

pleasant concealment
    and
painful concealment
```

The invariant is not the feeling but the practical function.

This is important for seeing. The practitioner must identify what the state is doing, not merely how it feels.

### Conceit appropriates agreeable self-experience

Mada is restricted to the two pleasant feelings because it is an affirmative inflation of the self-relation.

```text
pleasure
    becomes
self-intoxication
```

Yet pleasure remains distinct from mada. The affliction lies in the distortion of measure.

### Equanimity does not certify purity

The statement **sarvagā upekṣā** blocks a common practical error: taking neutrality for liberation.

```text
quiet feeling
    may still contain
attachment
view
conceit
ignorance
or derivative affliction
```

Equanimity is therefore a site of examination, not an automatic conclusion.

### Pervasive derivatives

Āhrīkya, anapatrāpya, styāna, and auddhatya can accompany all five feelings because they affect the general ethical or dynamic condition of the event.

```text
āhrīkya
    removes inner restraint

anapatrāpya
    removes concern for consequences

styāna
    contracts functional capacity

auddhatya
    disperses functional capacity
```

None of these operations depends upon one determinate affective tone.

## Logical Determination

VAK 5.58 completes a compatibility relation:

```text
Upakleśa × FeelingFaculty
    → permitted conjunction
```

The verse demonstrates that afflictive classification cannot be reduced to affective polarity.

```text
same feeling
    may support different afflictions

same affliction
    may support opposite feelings
```

The identity of the affliction therefore lies in its function and ground, not in its phenomenal tone.

### Function versus appearance

The crucial distinction is:

```text
how the state appears
    ≠
what relation the state performs
```

Māyā is especially revealing because its content is itself a falsification of appearance. It may feel good or bad while performing the same deceptive function.

### Universal compatibility

Upekṣā supplies a universal compatibility relation:

```text
for every affliction K,
Compatible(K, upekṣā)
```

This does not mean every occurrence of equanimity is afflicted. It means equanimity does not logically exclude affliction.

### Complete affective range

For the four pervasive derivatives:

```text
FeelingRange(x)
    = {sukha, duḥkha, saumanasya, daurmanasya, upekṣā}
```

Their diagnostic marker must therefore be functional rather than affective.

## Path Determination

The practical lesson is a refinement of seeing.

```text
Do not ask only:
How do I feel?

Ask also:
What operation is occurring through this feeling?
```

The verse supplies several markers:

```text
gladness or distress
    + falsified presentation
    → inspect māyā or śāṭhya

gladness or distress
    + concealment of fault
    → inspect mrakṣa

pleasant feeling
    + inflated self-measure
    → inspect mada

any feeling
    + collapse of restraint
    → inspect āhrīkya or anapatrāpya

any feeling
    + contraction or dispersion
    → inspect styāna or auddhatya

neutral feeling
    → do not infer purity
```

This is exactly how the Path places markers in the middle of experience. The marker is not the Truth of Origin itself; it is the determinate sign through which one traces the manifestation back to its generating root.

```text
feeling-tone
    ↓
manifest function
    ↓
root affliction
    ↓
mode of abandonment
```

## Organon Note

VAK 5.58 requires a distinction between phenomenal polarity and functional determination.

```text
CognitiveEvent
    hasFeeling
    hasAfflictiveFunction
```

The two fields must not be collapsed.

Suggested constraints:

```text
Māyā
    compatibleWith {saumanasya, daurmanasya, upekṣā}

Mada
    compatibleWith {sukha, saumanasya, upekṣā}

Āhrīkya
    compatibleWith all FeelingFaculty
```

Since upekṣā is universally compatible, a rule engine must never infer `NonAfflictedEvent` solely from neutral feeling.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. Does **ubhayathā** include only saumanasya and daurmanasya, or is any broader reading entertained by Yaśomitra?
2. Why is **middha** grouped with the three forms of practical concealment rather than with styāna in this feeling-distribution?
3. How precisely does the Bhāṣya justify mada’s conjunction with bodily pleasure in the third dhyāna and mental gladness below it?
4. Is **sarvagā upekṣā** stated of all anuśayas only, or extended without qualification to all upakleśas?
5. Are the four fully pervasive derivatives universal because of their mahābhūmika status alone, or because of an additional functional reason?
6. How does this feeling-matrix prepare the transition to the five nivaraṇas in VAK 5.59?
