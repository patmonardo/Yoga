# VAK 5.57

## Sanskrit

दौर्मनस्येन कौकृत्यमीर्ष्या क्रोधो विहिंसनम् ।
उपनाहः प्रदाशश्च मात्सर्यं तु विपर्ययात् ॥ ५.५७ ॥

## IAST

daurmanasyena kaukṛtyam īrṣyā krodho vihiṃsanam /
upanāhaḥ pradāśaś ca mātsaryaṃ tu viparyayāt // 5.57 //

## Padaccheda

daurmanasyena | kaukṛtyam | īrṣyā | krodhaḥ | vihiṃsanam | upanāhaḥ | pradāśaḥ | ca | mātsaryam | tu | viparyayāt

## Grammar

- **daurmanasyena** — instrumental singular neuter, “with mental distress,” “with dissatisfaction.”
- **kaukṛtyam** — nominative singular neuter, “regret,” “remorse,” literally the state of regarding something as badly done.
- **īrṣyā** — nominative singular feminine, “envy,” “jealousy.”
- **krodhaḥ** — nominative singular masculine, “anger.”
- **vihiṃsanam** — nominative singular neuter, “harmfulness,” “injurious intent.”
- **upanāhaḥ** — nominative singular masculine, “enmity retained,” “grudge,” “hostile fixation.”
- **pradāśaḥ** — nominative singular masculine, “contentious indignation,” “hostile insistence,” “esteeming or defending what is bad.”
- **ca** — “and.”
- **mātsaryam** — nominative singular neuter, “avarice,” “miserly withholding.”
- **tu** — contrastive particle, “but.”
- **viparyayāt** — ablative singular, “by reversal,” “with the contrary,” referring here to the feeling opposite to mental distress, namely mental gladness.

## Literal Translation

Regret, envy, anger, harmfulness, retained enmity, and contentious indignation are associated with mental distress; avarice, however, with the contrary.

## Philosophical Translation

The derivative afflictions that negate, resist, injure, or brood over a disturbed relation arise together with mental distress. Miserly withholding, however, is conjoined with mental gladness, because it takes satisfaction in possession and preservation rather than in rejection.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.55–5.56 mapped the root afflictions across the five feeling-faculties.

VAK 5.57 now descends to determinate upakleśas:

```text
root affective compatibility
    ↓
particular derivative afflictions
```

The verse divides them according to two opposite affective orientations.

### Distress-conjoined afflictions

```text
kaukṛtya
īrṣyā
krodha
vihiṃsā
upanāha
pradāśa
    → daurmanasya
```

These all contain a negative relation to what has occurred, what another possesses, what resists the subject, or what the subject continues to oppose.

### Gladness-conjoined affliction

```text
mātsarya
    → saumanasya
```

Avarice is structurally different. It does not primarily suffer an unwanted object. It takes satisfaction in retaining and withholding what is possessed.

Thus the contrast is:

```text
aversive deformation
    → mental distress

appropriative retention
    → mental gladness
```

## Technical Vocabulary

### Kaukṛtya

**Kaukṛtya** is regret or remorse regarding an action taken or omitted.

Its logical form is retrospective negation:

```text
what occurred
    is judged
as what should not have occurred
```

This explains its conjunction with **daurmanasya**. The mind is divided against its own completed action.

### Īrṣyā

**Īrṣyā** is distress at another’s excellence, success, possession, or pleasure.

```text
another has the good
    ↓
that good appears as my deprivation
    ↓
mental distress
```

The object is not merely another person. It is the relation between another’s flourishing and one’s own comparative self-position.

### Krodha

**Krodha** is acute anger, the immediate intensification of hostility.

```text
opposition encountered
    ↓
rejection intensified
    ↓
anger
```

### Vihiṃsā

**Vihiṃsā** is harmfulness or the intention to injure.

It translates hostility into a practical direction:

```text
aversive judgment
    ↓
will to damage or diminish
```

### Upanāha

**Upanāha** is retained hostility: a grudge or fixation that preserves anger after the immediate occasion has passed.

```text
anger
    becomes
stored negation
```

This is a particularly clear case of an upakleśa as immediate reflection. The original hostile event is no longer present, yet its determination persists within the continuum.

### Pradāśa

**Pradāśa** is a contentious or hostile insistence by which one clings to a defective position, action, or valuation.

Its exact nuance should be checked against the Bhāṣya, but the structural point is secure:

```text
correction is encountered
    ↓
the defective position is defended
    ↓
mental distress and hostility intensify
```

### Mātsarya

**Mātsarya** is miserly withholding.

Unlike envy, which suffers another’s possession, avarice enjoys one’s own exclusive possession:

```text
this is mine
    ↓
I take pleasure in retaining it
    ↓
I resist sharing or release
```

Hence the verse assigns it not to distress but to the contrary feeling, **saumanasya**.

## Doctrinal Determination

The verse demonstrates that the same broad root can generate affectively distinct manifestations.

Both envy and avarice concern possession, but their orientations differ:

```text
īrṣyā
    distress at another’s possession

mātsarya
    glad retention of one’s own possession
```

Therefore doctrinal classification cannot stop at object-content.

```text
same thematic field
    ≠
same afflictive function
```

The Path must determine:

```text
what relation is enacted?
what feeling accompanies it?
what practical direction follows?
```

The six distress-conjoined afflictions form a sequence from inward disturbance to outward injury:

```text
regret
    → comparative distress
    → anger
    → harmful intent
    → retained hostility
    → defense of the defective position
```

This is not necessarily a temporal series in every case. It is a structural progression showing how negation can deepen and stabilize.

## Logical Determination

VAK 5.57 adds polarity within the derivative field.

```text
negative relation
    +
mental distress
```

versus:

```text
positive appropriation
    +
mental gladness
```

This is important because “positive feeling” is not equivalent to wholesome determination.

```text
saumanasya
    can accompany
mātsarya
```

The pleasant tone belongs to the success of retention, not to freedom from affliction.

The verse therefore blocks a false inference:

```text
I feel pleased
    therefore
this relation is wholesome
```

Instead:

```text
feeling-tone
    must be interpreted through
relational orientation
```

## Path Determination

The practical marker system now becomes more precise.

```text
mental distress + retrospective self-division
    → inspect kaukṛtya

mental distress + another’s good
    → inspect īrṣyā

mental distress + acute opposition
    → inspect krodha

mental distress + wish to injure
    → inspect vihiṃsā

mental distress + preserved grievance
    → inspect upanāha

mental distress + defensive insistence
    → inspect pradāśa

mental gladness + exclusive retention
    → inspect mātsarya
```

This is exactly how the Path “places markers.” It does not merely prohibit behavior after the fact. It discriminates the affective and relational structure while it is arising.

The rationale is:

```text
feeling appears
    ↓
relation is identified
    ↓
upakleśa is named
    ↓
root and counteraction become available
```

The Truth of Origin remains central because the feeling itself is only the immediate sign. The Path must still trace the manifestation back to its generating root.

## Organon Note

VAK 5.57 requires a distinction between thematic object and relational function.

```text
PossessionField
    may generate
Envy
or
Avarice
```

Suggested model:

```text
AfflictiveEvent
    hasFeeling
    hasRelationalOrientation
    hasTemporalMode
    hasPracticalDirection
```

Examples:

```text
īrṣyā
    hasFeeling daurmanasya
    hasOrientation comparative-negation

upanāha
    hasFeeling daurmanasya
    hasTemporalMode retained

mātsarya
    hasFeeling saumanasya
    hasOrientation exclusive-retention
```

The same apparent topic must not collapse distinct logical forms.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. How precisely does the Bhāṣya define **pradāśa** in this context?
2. Does **viparyayāt** refer exclusively to **saumanasya**, or more generally to the pleasant mental polarity?
3. Why is **mātsarya** associated with gladness while **īrṣyā** is associated with distress, despite their shared relation to possession?
4. Does the Bhāṣya distinguish **krodha**, **upanāha**, and **vihiṃsā** primarily by temporal persistence, intensity, or practical direction?
5. Is **kaukṛtya** always afflictive here, or does the broader system permit a non-afflictive form of regret?
6. How does the feeling-association of these upakleśas affect their abandonment through cultivation?
