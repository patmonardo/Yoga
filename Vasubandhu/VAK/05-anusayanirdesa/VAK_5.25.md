# VAK 5.25

## Sanskrit

सर्वकालास्तिता उक्तत्वाद् द्वयात् सद्विषयात् फलात् ।
तदस्तिवादात् सर्वास्तिवादा इष्टाश्चतुर्विधाः ॥ ५.२५ ॥

## IAST

sarvakālāstitā uktatvāt dvayāt sadviṣayāt phalāt |
tadastivādāt sarvāstivādā iṣṭāś caturvidhāḥ || 5.25 ||

## Source note

The kārikā source gives the sequence:

```text
sarvakālāstitā uktatvāt dvayāt sadviṣayāt phalāt
```

The compact syntax carries several reasons in compressed form. Pass 1 therefore preserves the received wording and identifies the argumentative structure without yet resolving every Bhāṣya-level expansion.

## Grammar

- `sarvakālāstitā` — existence in all times; the thesis that dharmas exist across past, present, and future.
- `uktatvāt` — ablative of cause: because it has been stated or declared.
- `dvayāt` — because of two; a compressed reference to a twofold ground whose precise expansion belongs to the Bhāṣya.
- `sadviṣayāt` — because the object exists; from `sat` plus `viṣaya`.
- `phalāt` — because of the result or fruit.
- `tadastivādāt` — because of asserting that it exists; from the claim `tat asti`.
- `sarvāstivādāḥ` — proponents of universal existence; those who maintain that all dharmas exist across the three times.
- `iṣṭāḥ` — are accepted, admitted, or recognized.
- `caturvidhāḥ` — fourfold; of four kinds.

## Literal translation

Because existence in all times has been stated, because of the twofold ground, because the object exists, and because of the result, there is the assertion that it exists. From that doctrine of existence, the Sarvāstivādins are recognized as fourfold.

## Philosophical translation

The thesis that dharmas exist throughout the three times is supported by a compressed set of considerations: the declared temporal existence of dharmas, a twofold ground, the existence of an object for cognition, and the efficacy of a result. On the basis of differing accounts of what this temporal existence means, four forms of Sarvāstivāda are admitted.

## Analysis

Verses 5.23–5.24 required past and future afflictions to possess real binding efficacy. Verse 5.25 now makes explicit the ontological thesis presupposed by that machinery:

```text
past dharma can bind
future dharma can bind

therefore
past and future cannot be sheer nonbeing
```

The verse names four compressed considerations:

```text
sarvakālāstitā
    existence across all times

dvaya
    a twofold ground or argument

sadviṣaya
    cognition has an existent object

phala
    causal action issues in a result
```

The movement is from operational facts to ontological commitment:

```text
binding across time
object-directed cognition
causal fruition
    → temporal existence of dharmas
```

The second half then distinguishes the general thesis from its internal interpretations. `Sarvāstivāda` is not a single undifferentiated doctrine. Four accounts are accepted concerning how a dharma can be said to exist through the three times.

Verse 5.26 will enumerate these four positions. Verse 5.25 establishes their common problem-space:

```text
what remains identical?
what changes?
what makes a dharma past, present, or future?
```

## Doctrinal determination

The doctrine is not introduced as idle metaphysics. It answers practical and epistemic requirements already generated by the anuśaya analysis.

If a past affliction can still bind, its pastness cannot mean absolute nonexistence. If cognition can take a past or future dharma as object, its object cannot be entirely unreal. If an action produces a later result, the causal relation requires more than disconnected instants.

Thus:

```text
temporal existence
    grounds binding
    grounds object-reference
    grounds fruition
```

But the assertion `all exists` immediately produces a further problem. If past, present, and future dharmas all exist, what distinguishes the three times?

That question generates the four Sarvāstivāda positions announced here and developed next.

## Logical determination

Verse 5.25 moves from determinate operations to their presupposed ground:

```text
5.23–5.24
    temporal binding relations

5.25
    ontological condition of temporal binding
```

The argument has the form:

```text
an operation is real
    → its relata must possess sufficient being
```

More specifically:

```text
cognition requires an object
causality requires a result
bondage requires an efficacious affliction

therefore
past and future dharmas cannot be reduced to nothing
```

This is not yet proof of one unique theory of temporal existence. It is the establishment of a problem whose solution admits four determinations.

The crucial distinction is:

```text
common thesis
    all dharmas exist across the three times

competing principles
    how temporal difference is constituted
```

## Organon note

A knowledge architecture cannot merely store temporal labels:

```text

future
```

It must determine what kind of persistence allows a past state to remain causally or normatively operative, and what kind of anticipatory structure allows a future state to serve as an object or constraint.

For the Platform:

```text

    ≠ deletion or creation of an unrelated entity

    = mode under which a persisting determination is operative
```

The fourfold Sarvāstivāda debate is therefore immediately relevant to event sourcing, latent state, deferred effects, prediction, and the persistence of constraints.
