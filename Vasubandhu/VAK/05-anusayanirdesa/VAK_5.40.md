# VAK 5.40

## Sanskrit

आसयन्त्यास्रवन्त्येते हरन्ति श्लेषयन्त्यथ ।
उपगृह्णन्ति चेत्येषामास्रवादिनिरुक्तयः ॥ ५.४० ॥

## IAST

āsayanty āsravanty ete haranti śleṣayanty atha /
upagṛhṇanti cety eṣām āsravādi-niruktayaḥ // 5.40 //

## Padaccheda

āsayanti | āsravanti | ete | haranti | śleṣayanti | atha | upagṛhṇanti | ca | iti | eṣām | āsrava-ādi-niruktayaḥ

## Grammar

- **āsayanti** — 3rd person plural, present, causative-like verbal form from √śī / ā-√śī in the sense “cause to lie, lodge, settle”; here: they establish or settle in the stream.
- **āsravanti** — 3rd person plural, present, from ā-√sru, “flow, stream, leak, discharge.”
- **ete** — nominative plural masculine, “these,” referring to the afflictive dharmas under discussion.
- **haranti** — 3rd person plural, present, from √hṛ, “carry away, remove, sweep off.”
- **śleṣayanti** — 3rd person plural, present causative from √śliṣ, “cause to adhere, fasten, bind closely.”
- **atha** — “and then / moreover.”
- **upagṛhṇanti** — 3rd person plural, present, from upa-√grah, “take up, seize, appropriate, grasp.”
- **ca iti** — “and thus.”
- **eṣām** — genitive plural, “of these.”
- **āsrava-ādi-niruktayaḥ** — nominative plural feminine, “the etymological explanations beginning with āsrava.”

## Literal Translation

These cause [themselves] to settle, they flow, they carry away, they cause adhesion, and they appropriate; thus are the derivations of the terms beginning with “influx.”

## Philosophical Translation

The afflictive powers are named according to what they do within the stream: they lodge in it, flow through it, sweep it away, fasten it, and seize it as their own. The terms beginning with **āsrava** are therefore functional names of distinct operations.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.35–5.39 progressively classified the same afflictive field as:

1. **āsrava** — influx or outflow,
2. **ogha** — flood,
3. **yoga** — yoke or fastening,
4. **upādāna** — appropriation,
5. **anuśaya** — subtle following and continuing tendency.

VAK 5.40 now gives the governing semantic principle: these names arise from characteristic operations.

| Term | Verbal power | Functional determination |
|---|---|---|
| **anuśaya** | **āsayanti** | they lodge, settle, or lie within the stream |
| **āsrava** | **āsravanti** | they flow through and sustain the stream |
| **ogha** | **haranti** | they carry or sweep beings away |
| **yoga** | **śleṣayanti** | they fasten and make the stream adhere |
| **upādāna** | **upagṛhṇanti** | they seize and appropriate |

The verse should therefore not be read as mere wordplay. It identifies a family of distinct causal powers operating upon one continuum.

## Technical Vocabulary

### Āsayanti

“To cause to lie or settle.” The affliction is not necessarily continuously manifest; it can lodge within the continuum as a retained disposition.

### Āsravanti

“To flow.” The afflictive field has continuity and direction. It is not merely a set of isolated mental events.

### Haranti

“To carry away.” The stream exerts force upon the practitioner, drawing cognition and conduct along with it.

### Śleṣayanti

“To cause adhesion.” The yoke binds components together and makes disengagement difficult.

### Upagṛhṇanti

“To take up or seize.” Appropriation converts relation into possession: “this is mine,” “this is myself,” or “this is what I stand upon.”

### Nirukti

An etymological explanation that reveals operative meaning. Here language is used to disclose causal function rather than merely lexical origin.

## Doctrinal Determination

The same afflictive dharmas can receive different names because they exercise different powers.

Therefore:

```text
same dharma-field
    ≠
one undifferentiated function
```

A determination may:

```text
lodge
flow
carry
bind
appropriate
```

without thereby becoming five independent substances.

The doctrine is thus power-indexed. Classification follows **kāritra**, not merely item identity.

This also clarifies why the Kośa repeatedly reclassifies the same afflictions. Each classification answers a different operational question:

```text
How does it persist?
How does it move?
How does it overpower?
How does it bind?
How does it become mine?
```

## Logical Determination

The verse establishes a general rule:

```text
one determination
    may support
multiple non-equivalent predicates
    according to distinct powers
```

Hence:

```text
identity of bearer
    does not collapse
plurality of operations
```

The predications are not interchangeable:

```text
flowsThrough(x, stream)
    ≠
carriesAway(x, agent)

binds(x, agent)
    ≠
appropriates(x, object)
```

The Dialectical Copula must therefore be operation-sensitive. It must not flatten all relations into generic association.

## Organon Note

For the Platform, VAK 5.40 supplies a compact ontology of event-stream powers:

```text
SettlingPower
FlowPower
TransportPower
BindingPower
AppropriationPower
```

A single determination may instantiate several powers while each power retains its own conditions, effects, and intervention points.

This is directly relevant to our maxim:

> **Faith in Actuality: make the analysis actual.**

The analysis becomes actual when terms are implemented as executable distinctions rather than as decorative labels.

A practical schema might distinguish:

```text
persistsAsDisposition
propagatesThroughStream
redirectsAgentTrajectory
stabilizesAttachment
convertsObjectToPossession
```

The Copula situated in the Heart is therefore generative through differentiated Spanda. One pulse may lodge, another transmit, another seize. The unity lies in the living field; the difference lies in the operation.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. Does the Bhāṣya map **āsayanti** explicitly to **anuśaya**, or does it give a broader derivation?
2. Is **haranti** interpreted primarily as carrying beings across the stream, carrying them toward lower destinies, or overwhelming their control?
3. Does **śleṣayanti** emphasize adhesion to existence, mutual fastening of affliction and continuum, or bondage in a broader sense?
4. How strictly does Vasubandhu treat these as genuine etymologies rather than pedagogical semantic explanations?
5. Does the commentary preserve a one-to-one mapping between the five verbs and five categories?
6. Can one and the same affliction exercise all five powers in one event, or are these powers distributed according to conditions and standpoint?
