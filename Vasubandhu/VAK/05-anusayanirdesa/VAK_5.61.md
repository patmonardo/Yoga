# VAK 5.61

## Sanskrit

**Devanāgarī**

प्रहाणाधारभूतत्त्वदूषणाख्यश् चतुर्विधः ।  
प्रतिपक्षः प्रहातव्यः क्लेश आलम्बनान् मतः ॥ ५.६१ ॥

**IAST**

prahāṇādhārabhūtatva-dūṣaṇākhyaś caturvidhaḥ |  
pratipakṣaḥ prahātavyaḥ kleśa ālambanān mataḥ || 5.61 ||

> **Textual note.** The GRETIL line is compressed and appears to omit or conflate one member of the standard fourfold list. The kārikā nevertheless explicitly says that the counteragent is fourfold. The exact four names and their order should be verified in the Bhāṣya and critical edition.

## Translation

The counteragent is fourfold, designated in terms of abandonment, support, removal, and discrediting. An affliction to be abandoned is held to be abandoned through separation from its object.

## Analysis

Verse 5.60 explained the destruction of affliction through four causal conditions. Verse 5.61 now turns from the causes of destruction to the **forms of counteraction** themselves.

The crucial term is:

> **pratipakṣa** — counteragent, opponent, antidote

This is not a single undifferentiated negation. Counteraction has a structure.

### 1. Fourfold counteraction

The verse explicitly states:

> **caturvidhaḥ pratipakṣaḥ** — the counteragent is fourfold

The doctrinal list is compressed in the transmitted kārikā. At minimum, the verse distinguishes functions such as:

1. **prahāṇa** — actual abandonment;
2. **ādhārabhūtatva** — establishing or maintaining the basis of freedom;
3. **dūrībhāva / removal** — increasing distance from the abandoned affliction;
4. **dūṣaṇa / vidūṣaṇa** — exposing or discrediting the afflicted object and relation.

These are not four unrelated practices. They are four determinations of one liberative process:

```text
exposure of defect
    → active opposition
    → abandonment
    → stabilized distance
```

The exact allocation of names must remain open until the Bhāṣya pass, but the conceptual structure is secure: liberation requires more than a single instant of rejection.

### 2. Counteraction is positive determination

The verse does not define freedom as mere absence of kleśa. A counteragent must arise with a determinate function.

```text
kleśa
    is not removed by blank negation

it is removed by
    a positively specified contrary operation
```

This continues the principle already visible in 5.60:

```text
knowledge
support-severance
counteragent
stabilized non-return
```

Thus `pratipakṣa` is the positive side of abandonment.

### 3. Abandonment through the object-relation

The second half states that the affliction is abandoned:

> **ālambanāt** — through, or with respect to, its object

This is a highly important relation. An affliction does not exist as an isolated substance. It is an afflicted mode of taking an object.

```text
kleśa
    = distorted relation to an object
```

Therefore abandonment must transform that relation.

The object may be:

- known as it is;
- deprived of its power to sustain the affliction;
- relinquished as an appropriated support;
- or seen under a contrary determination.

The affliction is then no longer reproducible through the same objective relation.

### 4. The dialectical sequence

The verse yields a precise movement:

```text
object under afflicted determination
    → defect exposed
    → counteragent established
    → afflicted relation abandoned
    → distance stabilized
```

This is stronger than a psychology of suppression. The relation itself is reconstructed.

### 5. Scientific significance

The Kośa is distinguishing at least three levels:

```text
cause of destruction
mode of counteraction
resulting abandonment
```

So:

```text
kṣaya
    is the result

pratipakṣa
    is the active contrary determination

prahāṇa
    is the achieved severance
```

These cannot be collapsed into one vague idea of cessation.

## Organon determination

A useful restrained formulation is:

```text
negative relation
    is not overcome by mere absence

it is overcome by
    a determinate contrary relation
    that severs its objective support
```

The positive dialectic is therefore:

```text
critique
    → counteraction
    → abandonment
    → stabilized freedom
```

## Questions for the Bhāṣya pass

1. What is the exact critical Sanskrit reading of the fourfold list?
2. Are the four members `vidūṣaṇa`, `prahāṇa`, `ādhāra`, and `dūrībhāva`, and in what order?
3. Which path-moments correspond to each counteragent?
4. How exactly should `ālambanāt` be construed grammatically and doctrinally?
5. Does abandonment occur through separation from the object, from the affliction taking that object, or from both?
