# VAK 5.64

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

परिज्ञा नव कामाद्यप्रकारद्वयसंक्षयः ।
एका द्वयोः क्षये द्वे ते तथोर्ध्वं तिस्र एव ताः ॥ ५.६४ ॥

## Grammar

The opening statement is elliptical:

```text
parijñāḥ nava
    nine complete discriminations
```

The remainder begins their enumeration.

**kāmādya-prakāra-dvaya-saṃkṣayaḥ ekā** means:

```text
the complete destruction,
in the desire-domain,
of the first pair of classes
    is one parijñā
```

The Bhāṣya fixes the first pair as the afflictions abandoned through seeing the truths of:

```text
duḥkha
samudaya
```

Thus **prakāra-dvaya** does not mean two arbitrary species. It refers to two truth-indexed classes of affliction:

```text
duḥkha-darśana-heya
samudaya-darśana-heya
```

**dvayoḥ kṣaye dve te** means that the destruction of the remaining two classes yields two further parijñās. The Bhāṣya identifies these as the afflictions abandoned through seeing:

```text
nirodha
mārga
```

**tathordhvaṃ tisra eva tāḥ** states that precisely the same threefold division applies above the desire-domain, collectively to the form and formless domains.

## Literal Translation

There are nine complete discriminations. The complete destruction, beginning with the desire-domain, of the first pair of classes is one. Upon the destruction of the other two, there are two. Likewise, above, there are precisely those three.

## Philosophical Translation

There are nine designated completions of abandonment. In the desire-domain, the joint exhaustion of the afflictions abandoned through seeing suffering and origin constitutes one complete discrimination. The separate exhaustion of those abandoned through seeing cessation and those abandoned through seeing the Path constitutes two further complete discriminations. The same threefold articulation applies to the higher domains.

## Technical Vocabulary

### parijñā

The Bhāṣya distinguishes two senses:

```text
jñāna-parijñā
    complete discrimination as knowing

prahāṇa-parijñā
    complete discrimination as abandonment
```

The present enumeration concerns **prahāṇa-parijñā**.

It is therefore not merely:

```text
I know that an affliction exists.
```

It is:

```text
the affliction has been abandoned,
and that completed abandonment
receives the designation parijñā.
```

The word retains the structure of discrimination, but the designation is applied to the accomplished result because the result is grounded in discriminative knowing.

### saṃkṣaya

**Saṃkṣaya** is complete exhaustion or destruction.

```text
kṣaya
    destruction

saṃkṣaya
    complete destruction as a determined total
```

The verse does not count every individually destroyed affliction as a separate parijñā. It groups destructions according to a doctrinally significant completion.

### prakāra

**Prakāra** is a mode, class, or determinate type.

Here the relevant modes are indexed by the Four Truths:

```text
duḥkha-darśana-heya
samudaya-darśana-heya
nirodha-darśana-heya
mārga-darśana-heya
```

The first two form one group. The latter two are counted separately.

### kāma and ūrdhva

The verse first gives the three parijñās belonging to the desire-domain and then repeats the same structure for the higher domains.

```text
kāma-dhātu
    three

rūpa + ārūpya
    three
```

The Bhāṣya treats form and formless here together because the relevant seeing-abandonments have the same threefold organization.

## Analysis

VAK 5.63 distinguished:

```text
destruction of affliction
    from
acquisition of disconnection
```

VAK 5.64 now asks:

```text
Under what completed configurations
is abandonment designated parijñā?
```

The answer is not atomistic. The Kośa does not assign one parijñā to every single affliction destroyed.

Instead, it constructs a topology of completed abandonment:

```text
desire-domain
    duḥkha + samudaya classes destroyed
        → parijñā 1

    nirodha class destroyed
        → parijñā 2

    mārga class destroyed
        → parijñā 3

higher domains
    duḥkha + samudaya classes destroyed
        → parijñā 4

    nirodha class destroyed
        → parijñā 5

    mārga class destroyed
        → parijñā 6
```

VAK 5.65 will add the final three, belonging to abandonment through cultivation.

The striking feature is the conjunction of suffering and origin:

```text
duḥkha
    what is conditioned and afflicted

samudaya
    the origin of its recurrence
```

Their abandonment is counted as one complete discrimination.

This suggests that the experienced field and its generating ground form one diagnostic complex:

```text
conditioned result
    +
its afflictive origin
        → one completed seeing
```

By contrast, cessation and Path are separately articulated:

```text
nirodha
    the truth of release

mārga
    the determinate means of release
```

The Path therefore does not merely negate the experienced result. It discriminates:

```text
what is suffered
why it originates
what its cessation is
how cessation is realized
```

## Logical Determination

The principal logical determination is **completion by grouped exhaustion**.

```text
individual destruction
    ≠ automatically
parijñā
```

A parijñā is established when a doctrinally complete class of abandonment has been accomplished.

The verse therefore defines a quotient structure:

```text
many afflictions
    grouped by truth and domain
        ↓
one completed abandonment
        ↓
one parijñā
```

The Four Truths do not yield four parijñās per domain. They yield three:

```text
{duḥkha, samudaya}
{nirodha}
{mārga}
```

This is not a mere numerical compression. It expresses a logical asymmetry:

```text
duḥkha and samudaya
    belong to the one conditioned complex
    of manifestation and origin

nirodha
    determines release

mārga
    determines the mediation of release
```

Thus the seeing-process has the form:

```text
conditioned field and origin
    ↓
cessation
    ↓
Path
```

The first is a twofold object but one completed negative determination. Cessation and Path each possess their own distinct positive determination.

## Interpretive Note

This verse gives a precise form to the thought that the Path must remain centered on the Truth of Origin.

The practitioner first sees the conditioned field together with its arising:

```text
This is duḥkha.
This is how duḥkha originates.
```

These are not two unrelated observations. They complete one movement of diagnosis:

```text
appearance
    traced back to
origin
```

Only then can cessation and Path be determined without abstraction.

```text
origin not seen
    → cessation becomes an imagined absence

origin not seen
    → Path becomes external discipline
```

But when suffering and origin are discriminated as one connected structure:

```text
nirodha
    becomes determinate cessation

mārga
    becomes determinate counter-movement
```

The nine parijñās are therefore not nine pieces of information. They are nine attained configurations in which the field of bondage has been decisively delimited and abandoned.

## Organon Note

The verse suggests a model in which a completed discrimination is not attached directly to an isolated defect but to a completed partition of the defect-field.

```text
AfflictionInstance
    belongsTo TruthIndexedClass
    occursIn Domain

AbandonmentCompletion
    exhausts TruthIndexedClass
    covers DomainRange
    receivesDesignation Parijna
```

The first six parijñās can be represented as:

```text
Parijna_1
    domain: kāma
    class: {duḥkha-heya, samudaya-heya}

Parijna_2
    domain: kāma
    class: {nirodha-heya}

Parijna_3
    domain: kāma
    class: {mārga-heya}

Parijna_4
    domain: ūrdhva
    class: {duḥkha-heya, samudaya-heya}

Parijna_5
    domain: ūrdhva
    class: {nirodha-heya}

Parijna_6
    domain: ūrdhva
    class: {mārga-heya}
```

This is a completion lattice rather than a flat list.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. Why are the afflictions abandoned through seeing suffering and origin grouped into one parijñā rather than two?
2. Why are cessation and Path counted separately?
3. Why are the form and formless domains grouped together under **ūrdhvam** for these three parijñās?
4. Does **parijñā** designate the abandonment itself, the acquisition of disconnection, or the abandonment insofar as qualified by that acquisition?
5. How exactly does the distinction between **jñāna-parijñā** and **prahāṇa-parijñā** govern the use of the term here?
6. Which specific path moments complete each of the first six parijñās?

## Core Determination

```text
There are nine completed discriminations.

The first six organize abandonment through seeing:

three in the desire-domain,
three in the higher domains.

In each domain-range:

suffering and origin form one completion,
cessation forms a second,
and the Path forms a third.
```
