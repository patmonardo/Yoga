# VAK 5.49

## Sanskrit

कौकृत्यं विचिकित्सातः क्रोधेर्ष्ये प्रतिघान्वये ।
अन्ये च षट्क्लेशमलाः माया शाठ्यं मदस्तथा ॥ ५.४९ ॥

## IAST

kaukṛtyaṃ vicikitsātaḥ krodherṣye pratighānvaye /
anye ca ṣaṭ kleśa-malāḥ māyā śāṭhyaṃ madas tathā // 5.49 //

## Padaccheda

kaukṛtyam | vicikitsātaḥ | krodha-īrṣye | pratigha-anvaye | anye | ca | ṣaṭ | kleśa-malāḥ | māyā | śāṭhyam | madaḥ | tathā

## Grammar

- **kaukṛtyam** — nominative singular neuter, “remorse,” “regret,” or “worry over what has or has not been done.”
- **vicikitsātaḥ** — ablative singular feminine, “from doubt”; the ablative marks the generating root.
- **krodha-īrṣye** — nominative dual, “anger and jealousy.”
- **pratigha-anvaye** — locative singular compound, “in the lineage of hostility,” “belonging to the derivation from aversion.”
- **anye ca** — “and others,” opening a further classificatory series.
- **ṣaṭ kleśa-malāḥ** — nominative plural, “six stains of affliction.”
- **māyā, śāṭhyam, madaḥ** — the first three members of the sixfold series; the remaining three occur in VAK 5.50.

## Literal Translation

Remorse arises from doubt; anger and jealousy belong to the lineage of hostility. There are also six other stains of affliction: deceit, crookedness, intoxication, and likewise [three more].

## Philosophical Translation

The derivation of the manifest afflictive states is completed: remorse proceeds from doubt, while anger and jealousy are determinate expressions of hostility. A further class is then introduced—the six stains through which affliction corrupts conduct and self-presentation: deceit, duplicity, intoxicated self-inflation, and the three still to be named.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.48 traced several envelopments back to attachment and ignorance. VAK 5.49 completes that causal map:

```text
vicikitsā
    → kaukṛtya

pratigha
    → krodha
    → īrṣyā
```

The movement then shifts from **paryavasthāna**, active occupation of the mental field, to **kleśa-mala**, the staining effect of affliction upon action, relation, and appearance.

```text
root affliction
    ↓
derivative occupation
    ↓
stained presentation and conduct
```

The six stains are distributed across VAK 5.49–5.50:

```text
māyā
śāṭhya
mada
pradāśa
upanāha
vihiṃsā
```

VAK 5.49 therefore both closes one derivational sequence and opens another.

## Technical Vocabulary

### Kaukṛtya

From **ku-kṛta**, “badly done,” extended to regret, remorse, or anxious review of what was done or omitted.

Its structure is retrospective:

```text
an action is past
    ↓
its determination is reopened
    ↓
judgment cannot settle
    ↓
remorse occupies the field
```

Because it arises from **vicikitsā**, remorse is not merely moral sensitivity. It is the persistence of undecided judgment after action.

### Vicikitsā

Doubt or divided judgment.

Here doubt does not remain theoretical. It produces a temporal after-effect in which action cannot be integrated into a settled practical determination.

```text
doubt before or within action
    → remorse after action
```

### Pratigha

Hostility, resistance, or repulsive opposition.

It is the root orientation from which both anger and jealousy can arise. The common form is negation of another determination:

```text
krodha
    direct reactive negation

īrṣyā
    negation of another's excellence or gain
```

### Anvaya

Lineage, connection, derivational belonging.

The term indicates more than occasional association. Anger and jealousy belong structurally to the family of hostility.

### Kleśa-mala

Literally, “stain of affliction.”

A **mala** is not simply another root affliction. It is the defilement or distortion through which an affliction marks conduct, expression, or relation.

Working determination:

```text
kleśa
    root contradiction

mala
    the stain left by that contradiction
    in presentation and action
```

### Māyā

Deceit or deceptive presentation: making oneself appear otherwise than one is in order to conceal fault or secure advantage.

### Śāṭhya

Crookedness, duplicity, or strategic insincerity. Unlike simple concealment, it bends communication and conduct around a hidden intention.

### Mada

Intoxication, conceited exhilaration, or self-inflation based on youth, health, status, power, learning, or other favorable conditions.

## Doctrinal Determination

The verse teaches that derivative afflictions must be diagnosed through their roots, but it also introduces a further distinction between an affliction and its stain.

```text
vicikitsā
    root divided judgment

kaukṛtya
    its manifest retrospective occupation
```

```text
pratigha
    root resistant negation

krodha / īrṣyā
    its determinate relational expressions
```

The six **kleśa-malas** concern the way affliction corrupts the person's relation to truth, others, and self-presentation.

The first three already disclose three distinct modes:

```text
māyā
    falsification of appearance

śāṭhya
    falsification of intention

mada
    falsification of self-measure
```

Thus the Path requires more than suppressing unpleasant states. It requires discrimination of how the root affliction reorganizes cognition, action, and presentation.

## Logical Determination

VAK 5.49 introduces a layered logic of manifestation:

```text
root
    → derivative state
    → stained relation
```

The root is not directly identical with its manifestation. Rather, it determines a family of effects.

```text
pratigha
    is not identical with
krodha or īrṣyā

but

krodha and īrṣyā
    are intelligible only through
pratigha
```

This is a logic of **anvaya**, grounded derivational belonging.

The transition to **mala** adds a further moment:

```text
a determination may cease to be merely internal
    and become
an alteration of the subject's whole mode of appearing
```

The stain is therefore the externalized or relational persistence of the root contradiction.

## Path Rationale

The verse gives a precise diagnostic rule:

```text
manifest disturbance
    ↓
identify its derivational lineage
    ↓
discriminate the root contradiction
    ↓
observe how it stains action and presentation
```

This reveals why moral correction alone is insufficient. A deceptive act may be restrained, but unless the underlying attachment, hostility, ignorance, or distorted view is understood, the same root can generate another stain.

The Path therefore operates at several levels:

```text
restrain the manifestation
understand the derivation
remove the root
purify the relational field
```

## Organon Note

VAK 5.49 suggests three distinct dependency relations for the Platform:

```text
arisesFrom
belongsToLineage
stainsField
```

These should not be collapsed.

```text
Kaukṛtya
    arisesFrom Vicikitsā

Krodha
    belongsToLineage Pratigha

Māyā
    stains SelfPresentation
```

The distinction is important because causal origination, taxonomic lineage, and functional corruption are different logical relations.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. Why is **kaukṛtya** assigned specifically to **vicikitsā** rather than to ignorance or hostility?
2. Does **pratighānvaya** indicate direct causal origination, shared substance, or doctrinal lineage?
3. How does the Bhāṣya distinguish **māyā** from **mrakṣa**, concealment of fault?
4. What precise distinction is drawn between **māyā** and **śāṭhya**?
5. Why are these six called **malas** rather than merely **upakleśas** or **paryavasthānas**?
6. Does **mada** arise only from attachment, or can it be generated through view and conceit as well?
7. How are the six stains distributed among their root afflictions in VAK 5.50–5.51?
