# VAK 5.66

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

अनागम्यफलं सर्वा ध्यानानां पञ्च वाथवा ।
अष्टौ सामन्तकस्यैका मौलारूप्यत्रयस्य च ॥ ५.६६ ॥

### IAST

anāgamya-phalaṃ sarvā dhyānānāṃ pañca vāthavā |
aṣṭau sāmantakasyaikā maulārūpya-trayasya ca || 5.66 ||

## Padaccheda

anāgamya-phalam | sarvāḥ | dhyānānām | pañca | vā | athavā | aṣṭau | sāmantakasya | ekā | maula-ārūpya-trayasya | ca

## Grammar

- **anāgamya-phalam** — nominative singular neuter used predicatively with the feminine plural **sarvāḥ** understood distributively: “all [nine] are fruits of the anāgamya.” The compound names the result obtainable on the basis of the preliminary concentration immediately below the first dhyāna.
- **sarvāḥ** — nominative plural feminine, “all,” referring to the nine **parijñās** established in VAK 5.64–5.65.
- **dhyānānām** — genitive plural neuter, “of the dhyānas,” here the fundamental form-domain absorptions.
- **pañca** — nominative plural, “five.”
- **vā** — disjunctive particle, “or.”
- **athavā** — “or else,” marking the alternative doctrinal count.
- **aṣṭau** — nominative plural, “eight.”
- **sāmantakasya** — genitive singular neuter, “of the neighboring or preliminary concentration.” In the Bhāṣya this refers specifically to the preliminary stage of the sphere of infinite space.
- **ekā** — nominative singular feminine, “one,” agreeing with **parijñā** understood.
- **maula-ārūpya-trayasya** — genitive singular compound, “of the three fundamental formless attainments.”
- **ca** — conjunctive particle, “and.”

## Literal Translation

All [nine complete discriminations] are fruits of the preliminary concentration. Of the dhyānas there are five—or, alternatively, eight. One is the fruit of the preliminary [formless attainment], and one is the fruit of the three fundamental formless attainments.

## Philosophical Translation

The nine completed abandonments are now classified according to the meditative basis from which they can be actualized. The preliminary concentration below the first dhyāna can support all nine. The fundamental dhyānas support either five, according to the Vaibhāṣika determination, or eight according to an alternative interpretation. The preliminary concentration of the first formless attainment supports the complete abandonment of attachment to form; the three fundamental formless attainments support only the final exhaustion of every remaining bond.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.64–5.65 answered:

```text
What are the nine parijñās?
```

VAK 5.66 asks:

```text
From which meditative bases
can those completed abandonments arise?
```

The verse therefore introduces a new relation:

```text
meditative basis
    → attainable parijñā-set
```

The distribution is:

```text
anāgamya
    → all nine parijñās

fundamental dhyānas
    → five parijñās
    or, on another view,
    → eight parijñās

first formless preliminary concentration
    → one parijñā
       rūparāga-kṣaya

three fundamental formless attainments
    → one parijñā
       sarva-saṃyojana-paryādāna
```

This is not a classification of the parijñās by content alone. It is a classification by the **operative ground of attainment**.

## Technical Vocabulary

### Anāgamya

**Anāgamya** is the preliminary concentration immediately preceding the first dhyāna.

Its doctrinal importance here is that it is sufficiently concentrated to function as a basis for the noble path while not yet being one of the fundamental dhyānas.

```text
ordinary desire-domain mind
    ↓
preliminary concentration
    ↓
first dhyāna
```

Because the abandonment of desire-domain afflictions is specifically connected with this preliminary basis, all nine parijñās can be reached from it.

### Dhyāna

The **dhyānas** are the fundamental absorptions of the form-domain.

The Vaibhāṣika count gives them five parijñās as fruits: those whose content is the abandonment of form- and formless-domain afflictions.

The desire-domain abandonments are excluded from that count because their operative basis is assigned to **anāgamya**.

### Five or Eight

The words **pañca vāthavā aṣṭau** preserve a doctrinal divergence.

The Vaibhāṣika position:

```text
fundamental dhyānas
    → five parijñās
```

An alternative attributed by the Bhāṣya to Bhadanta Ghoṣaka:

```text
fundamental dhyānas
    → eight parijñās
```

The alternative includes the three desire-domain abandonments belonging to seeing, on the ground that even one already free from desire-domain attachment may obtain a fresh undefiled acquisition of disconnection through the path of seeing.

The lower-fetter destruction parijñā remains exclusively an **anāgamya** fruit.

### Sāmantaka

A **sāmantaka** is a neighboring or preliminary concentration standing immediately below a fundamental attainment.

Here the Bhāṣya specifies the preliminary concentration of the sphere of infinite space.

Its single fruit is:

```text
rūparāga-kṣaya-parijñā
    complete discrimination through destruction of attachment to form
```

The logic is exact: transcendence of the form-domain requires a meditative basis located at its upper boundary.

### Maula-ārūpya

**Maula-ārūpya** means a fundamental formless attainment, as distinct from its preliminary stage.

The three relevant fundamental formless attainments each support only the final parijñā:

```text
sarva-saṃyojana-paryādāna-parijñā
    complete exhaustion of all fetters
```

The final abandonment is possible there because what remains belongs to the higher formless continuum itself.

## Doctrinal Determination

The verse establishes that an abandonment and the meditative basis from which it is achieved must be distinguished.

```text
parijñā
    = completed abandonment

samāpatti-basis
    = operative support of that completion
```

The same parijñā may be attainable from more than one basis, but not every basis can produce every parijñā.

This yields a constrained relation:

```text
MeditativeBasis × Parijñā
    → attainable / unattainable
```

### Why anāgamya supports all nine

The preliminary concentration is the decisive hinge between the desire-domain and the form-domain.

Because the noble path may operate there against afflictions from every domain, it can support all nine completed abandonments.

```text
not fully within desire
not yet fixed in fundamental dhyāna
    ↓
maximum range of counteraction
```

Its intermediate status is therefore not weakness. It gives it the widest practical range.

### Why the fundamental dhyānas have a narrower range

A fundamental dhyāna is already a determinate meditative domain.

On the Vaibhāṣika reading, it does not serve as the specific basis for abandoning desire-domain afflictions. It functions against the afflictions of its own and higher domains.

Thus:

```text
higher stability
    does not imply
wider counteractive range
```

The more determinate the basis, the more determinate its field of operation.

### Why the formless bases yield only one each

At the threshold of the formless domain, the remaining task is no longer the analysis of the full field of affliction.

The operative tasks have become highly restricted:

```text
first formless preliminary
    → overcome attachment to form

fundamental formless attainments
    → exhaust the remaining formless bonds
```

The range narrows because the lower domains have already been transcended.

## Logical Determination

VAK 5.66 adds a logic of **qualified capacity**.

A basis is defined not merely by what it is, but by what transformations it can support.

```text
Basis
    hasCapacityFor
    Parijñā
```

The relation is neither one-to-one nor universal:

```text
one basis may support many parijñās
one parijñā may be supported by several bases
some pairings are impossible
```

The full pattern is:

```text
broad transitional basis
    → broad transformative capacity

determinate fundamental basis
    → restricted transformative capacity

upper boundary basis
    → domain-transcending completion

highest-domain basis
    → final exhaustion only
```

### Transition and range

The philosophically striking result is that the transitional state has the widest range.

```text
anāgamya
    is not merely an incomplete dhyāna

it is the operative middle
    from which the whole field
    can be transformed
```

This confirms the practical importance of the Path appearing “in the middle.” The middle is not an indeterminate compromise. It is the standpoint from which both the lower origin and the higher completion can be grasped.

### Determinate disagreement

The five-versus-eight dispute is itself logically instructive.

Both positions accept the same nine parijñās. They differ over the attribution relation:

```text
Which meditative basis
counts as the fruit-ground
of a given abandonment?
```

Thus the disagreement concerns the mapping between achievement and support, not the existence of the achievement itself.

## Path Determination

The verse prevents a simple hierarchy of the form:

```text
higher meditation
    = more complete liberation
```

That inference is false.

A higher state may possess greater concentration while having a narrower counteractive function.

```text
meditative height
    ≠
range of abandonment
```

The Path must therefore ask:

```text
What affliction is being opposed?

From which meditative basis
can its abandonment be completed?
```

The practical map is:

```text
identify the remaining bond
    ↓
identify its domain
    ↓
identify the required counteragent
    ↓
identify the meditative basis
capable of supporting that counteragent
```

This is a stronger doctrine than merely “enter deeper meditation.” The question is not depth alone, but functional adequacy.

## Organon Note

VAK 5.66 requires a distinction between a state and its transformation-capacity.

```text
MeditativeState
    hasDomain
    hasLevel
    supportsCounteragent
    yieldsParijñā
```

A possible model:

```text
Anagamya
    yields all Parijñās

FundamentalDhyāna
    yields five Parijñās
    alternativeView yields eight

FirstFormlessPreliminary
    yields RūparāgaKṣayaParijñā

FundamentalFormlessThree
    yield SarvaSaṃyojanaParyādānaParijñā
```

The five/eight divergence should not be flattened. It should be represented as a provenance-qualified doctrinal assertion:

```text
VaibhāṣikaView
    asserts count = 5

GhoṣakaView
    asserts count = 8
```

This preserves disagreement without corrupting the shared ontology.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. Why can **anāgamya** serve as the basis for all nine parijñās while the fundamental dhyānas have a narrower range?
2. Which exact five parijñās are counted as fruits of the fundamental dhyānas in the Vaibhāṣika position?
3. How does Bhadanta Ghoṣaka justify the count of eight?
4. Why is the destruction of the lower fetters exclusively an **anāgamya** fruit even on the alternative interpretation?
5. Does **dhyānāntara** inherit exactly the same fruit-range as the fundamental dhyānas?
6. Why does the preliminary concentration of infinite space yield specifically the destruction of attachment to form?
7. Why do the three fundamental formless attainments each yield only the final parijñā rather than distinct parijñās?
8. Is **phala** here a causal result, an attainment supported by the meditative basis, or a technical relation of path-production?

## Core Determination

```text
The nine completed abandonments
are not available from every meditative basis alike.

The transitional preliminary concentration
has the widest range.

Fundamental states have more restricted functions,
and the highest formless bases
support only the final exhaustion
appropriate to their domain.
```
