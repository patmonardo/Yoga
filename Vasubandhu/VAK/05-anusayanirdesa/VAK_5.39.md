## Grammar

- **aṇavaḥ** — nominative masculine plural of *aṇu*; “subtle,” “minute,” “difficult to detect.”
- **anugatāḥ** — nominative masculine plural past passive participle of *anu-√gam*; “following,” “accompanying,” “persisting along with.”
- **ete** — nominative masculine plural pronoun; “these,” referring to the afflictive tendencies under discussion.
- **dvidhā** — indeclinable; “in two ways.” The exact twofold division requires Bhāṣya confirmation; the preceding analysis strongly suggests conjunction with cognition and object-conditioned latent lodging.
- **anuśerate** — third-person plural middle of *anu-√śī*; “they lie along,” “remain latent,” “repose within,” “continue as underlying tendencies.”
- **anubadhnanti** — third-person plural active of *anu-√bandh*; “they bind along,” “continue to attach,” “follow and constrain.”
- **yasmāt ... tasmāt** — correlative construction: “because ... therefore.”
- **anuśayāḥ smṛtāḥ** — “they are called/remembered as anuśayas.”

## Literal Translation

These are subtle and accompanying; they also lie latent in two ways. Because they continue to bind, therefore they are called *anuśayas*.

## Philosophical Translation

The latent afflictions are not substantial things hidden behind experience. They are subtle powers that accompany the stream, remain disposed in two modes, and continue to bind later events. Their name expresses this operative persistence.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.39 gathers the preceding discussion into an account of why these afflictions are called *anuśaya*.

The verse attributes four powers to them:

1. **Subtlety — aṇutva**
   
   They are not always manifest as explicit thoughts, emotions, or judgments. Their presence may be inferred only from the kinds of events they make possible.

2. **Accompaniment — anugamana**
   
   They follow the continuum. They are not identical with each manifest episode, but they condition later episodes across the stream.

3. **Latent lodging — anuśayana**
   
   They “lie along” the stream rather than continuously operating in manifest form. Their reality is dispositional.

4. **Continued binding — anubandhana**
   
   They constrain subsequent cognition and action until abandoned. Their power is shown by the recurrence and organization of later events.

The verse therefore rejects two simplistic models:

```text
anuśaya = continuously manifest affliction
```

and

```text
anuśaya = inert hidden object
```

Instead:

```text
anuśaya
    = subtle dispositional power
      accompanying the continuum
      capable of renewed activation
      and continued binding
```

## Technical Vocabulary

### aṇu

Here *aṇu* means subtle or minute, not necessarily an independently existing atom. The anuśaya is difficult to detect because its operation may be indirect and deferred.

### anugata

That which follows or accompanies. This term expresses continuity without requiring a numerically identical substance traveling unchanged through time.

### anuśayana

Literally, lying along or remaining latent. The term points toward a mode of nonmanifest presence rather than a second hidden self beneath cognition.

### anubandhana

Continued binding or consequential attachment. The anuśaya is known through its power to constrain later events.

## Doctrinal Determination

An anuśaya is defined by a pattern of efficacy across a continuum.

It is:

```text
not always manifest
but still causally available
```

This is precisely why the Kośa must separate:

```text
existence
manifestation
operation
abandonment
```

The latent tendency may exist without presently operating as an overt kleśa. Yet it remains capable of conditioning future events so long as it has not been abandoned.

The verse therefore strengthens the interpretation developed in 5.34:

```text
latent power
    + appropriate conditions
        → manifest event
```

VAK 5.39 now adds that the latent power itself is recognized through its subtle accompaniment and continuing bond.

## Logical Determination

The same determination may possess different temporal and operational statuses:

```text
isPresentAsDisposition
    ≠
isManifestAsEvent
```

and:

```text
isNotManifest
    ↛
isWithoutPower
```

The logic required is therefore dispositional and event-sensitive.

A tendency is not represented merely as a static property of an agent. It must be represented as a power whose manifestation depends on conditions and whose persistence is evidenced by later events.

Possible relations:

```text
accompaniesStream
liesLatentIn
canManifestUnder
continuesToBind
isAbandonedBy
```

## Organon Note

For the Dialectical Copula, this verse gives a decisive practical extension.

The Copula must not only connect actual terms. It must also carry unrealized but determinate possibilities forward through an event-stream.

```text
Copula
    = actual relation
      + retained disposition
      + condition of renewed generation
```

This is where the Copula's generative Spanda becomes technically precise.

A pulse does not create from nothing. It actualizes a subtle power already accompanying the stream under newly completed conditions.

Thus:

```text
Spanda
    does not move a thing through time

Spanda
    actualizes a determinate possibility
    within the living stream
```

The anuśaya is therefore not a hidden thing in the Heart. It is a structured possibility of recurrence—a way the Heart can pulse again unless the binding determination has been sublated.

This also sharpens the maxim:

> **Faith in Actuality: make the analysis actual.**

To make the analysis actual is to detect subtle powers not by reifying them, but by tracing the event-patterns through which they accompany, recur, and bind.
