# VAK 5.68

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

अनास्रववियोगाप्तेर्भवाग्रविकलीकृतेः ।
हेतुद्वयसमुद्घातात् परिज्ञा धात्वतिक्रमात् ॥ ५.६८ ॥

### IAST

anāsrava-viyogāpter bhavāgra-vikalīkṛteḥ |
hetu-dvaya-samudghātāt parijñā dhātv-atikramāt || 5.68 ||

## Padaccheda

anāsrava-viyoga-āpteḥ | bhava-agra-vikalīkṛteḥ | hetu-dvaya-samudghātāt | parijñā | dhātu-atikramāt

## Grammar

- **anāsrava-viyoga-āpteḥ** — ablative singular compound: “because of the acquisition of uncontaminated disconnection.” The compound joins **anāsrava** (uncontaminated), **viyoga** (separation, disconnection), and **āpti** (acquisition).
- **bhava-agra-vikalīkṛteḥ** — ablative singular compound: “because the summit of existence has been rendered powerless,” “because the peak of becoming has been disabled.” **Bhavāgra** denotes the highest state within conditioned existence.
- **hetu-dvaya-samudghātāt** — ablative singular compound: “because of the eradication of the two grounds.” **Samudghāta** indicates complete uprooting or removal.
- **parijñā** — nominative singular feminine: “complete discrimination,” here the technical designation for a completed abandonment.
- **dhātu-atikramāt** — ablative singular compound: “because of the transcendence of a domain.”

The verse consists of four ablative grounds explaining why the relevant abandonments receive the technical name **parijñā**.

## Literal Translation

A completed abandonment is called a parijñā because of the acquisition of uncontaminated disconnection, because the summit of existence is rendered powerless, because the two grounds are uprooted, and because a domain is transcended.

## Philosophical Translation

The designation “complete discrimination” applies when abandonment has become structurally complete: an uncontaminated separation has been acquired, the highest remaining support of conditioned existence has been disabled, the two sustaining grounds have been eradicated, and the practitioner has passed beyond the domain in which those afflictions operated.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.64–5.67 enumerated and classified the nine **parijñās** according to:

```text
object-domain
mode of abandonment
meditative basis
path-type
knowledge-family
```

VAK 5.68 now asks a different question:

```text
Why does an abandonment
qualify as a parijñā?
```

The answer is not simply:

```text
because an affliction has ceased
```

Rather, four conditions define completed abandonment:

```text
1. acquisition of uncontaminated disconnection
2. disabling of the summit of existence
3. eradication of two grounds
4. transcendence of a domain
```

The verse therefore moves from enumeration to definition.

## Technical Vocabulary

### anāsrava-viyogāpti

**Anāsrava-viyogāpti** is the acquisition of separation through an uncontaminated path.

It marks a distinction between:

```text
mere non-occurrence
    and
attained disconnection
```

An affliction may be temporarily absent without being abandoned. A parijñā requires an acquired relation of non-association grounded in the uncontaminated path.

### bhavāgra-vikalīkaraṇa

**Bhavāgra** is the summit of conditioned existence, conventionally the highest formless attainment.

**Vikalīkṛta** means weakened, disabled, rendered ineffective, or deprived of operative force.

Thus:

```text
bhavāgra remains within saṃsāra
but its governing force is broken
```

The point is not merely ascent to the highest conditioned state. The highest state itself must lose its capacity to sustain bondage.

### hetu-dvaya-samudghāta

The verse speaks of the uprooting of **two grounds**.

The exact identity of the pair must be fixed from the Bhāṣya, but the logical form is clear:

```text
a manifest affliction
    persists through more than one sustaining condition

complete abandonment
    removes both
```

The term **samudghāta** is stronger than temporary suppression. It indicates removal at the level of ground.

### dhātu-atikrama

**Dhātu-atikrama** is the passing beyond a domain.

This does not mean spatial movement. It means that the practitioner is no longer governed by the afflictive structure proper to that domain.

```text
domain
    = range of possible afflictive operation

transcendence
    = no longer subject to that range
```

## Doctrinal Determination

The four grounds describe different dimensions of completed abandonment.

### 1. Attainment-dimension

```text
anāsrava-viyogāpti
    acquisition of uncontaminated separation
```

This concerns what has been acquired.

### 2. Upper-limit dimension

```text
bhavāgra-vikalīkaraṇa
    disabling of the highest conditioned support
```

This concerns how far the counteractive force extends.

### 3. Causal dimension

```text
hetu-dvaya-samudghāta
    eradication of both sustaining grounds
```

This concerns whether the affliction can regenerate.

### 4. Domain dimension

```text
dhātu-atikrama
    transcendence of the domain
```

This concerns whether the practitioner remains within the range governed by the abandoned structure.

The technical name **parijñā** therefore implies more than cognitive recognition.

```text
knowing the defect
    is not yet
complete discrimination
```

Complete discrimination requires completed structural separation.

## Logical Determination

The verse defines **parijñā** through a conjunction of completion-conditions.

```text
Parijñā(x)
    only if
x includes attained disconnection,
upper-limit disabling,
ground-eradication,
and domain-transcendence
```

The four clauses should not be collapsed into synonyms.

They correspond to four distinct logical relations:

```text
acquisition
range
causal ground
domain membership
```

A defective determination is fully abandoned only when:

```text
its opposite has been attained
its highest support has been neutralized
its causes have been uprooted
its domain has been surpassed
```

This is a rigorous criterion of completion.

## Path Determination

The practical importance is that apparent progress can fail at four different points.

```text
an affliction may be absent
without disconnection being acquired

lower forms may be abandoned
while the highest support remains operative

one sustaining ground may be removed
while another persists

a state may be weakened
without the domain being transcended
```

The Path must therefore test abandonment along all four axes.

```text
Is the separation uncontaminated?
Has the highest support been disabled?
Have both grounds been uprooted?
Has the relevant domain been transcended?
```

Only then does abandonment qualify as complete discrimination.

## Organon Note

VAK 5.68 supplies a completion predicate for transformation.

```text
TransformationComplete
    requires
    acquiredContrary
    upperBoundNeutralized
    allGroundsRemoved
    sourceDomainExceeded
```

This prevents a system from treating local suppression as global resolution.

A useful distinction is:

```text
state inactive
    ≠
state disconnected

state disconnected
    ≠
all grounds removed

all grounds removed
    ≠
domain transcended
```

The verse therefore gives a four-part validation rule for completed negation.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. What exactly are the two grounds in **hetu-dvaya**?
2. Does each of the nine parijñās satisfy all four conditions, or are the four conditions distributed among different parijñās?
3. How precisely is **anāsrava-viyogāpti** distinguished from the repeated **visaṃyoga-lābha** of VAK 5.63?
4. What does it mean for **bhavāgra** to be **vikalīkṛta** rather than fully abandoned?
5. Is **dhātu-atikrama** defined by abandonment of all afflictions of a domain, by attainment of a higher domain, or by a specific fruit?
6. How does this fourfold definition determine the counting of the nine parijñās?

## Core Determination

```text
A parijñā is not mere recognition.
It is completed abandonment:
separation is acquired,
the highest support is disabled,
both grounds are uprooted,
and the relevant domain is transcended.
```
