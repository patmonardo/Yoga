# VAK 5.48

## Sanskrit

क्रोधम्रक्षौ च रागोत्था आह्रीक्यौद्धत्यमत्सराः ।
म्रक्षे विवादः अविद्यातः स्त्यानमिद्धानपत्रपाः ॥ ५.४८ ॥

## IAST

krodha-mrakṣau ca rāgotthā āhrīkya-auddhatya-matsarāḥ /
mrakṣe vivādaḥ avidyātaḥ styāna-middha-anapatrapāḥ // 5.48 //

## Padaccheda

krodha-mrakṣau | ca | rāga-utthāḥ | āhrīkya-auddhatya-matsarāḥ | mrakṣe | vivādaḥ | avidyātaḥ | styāna-middha-anapatrapāḥ

## Grammar

- **krodha-mrakṣau** — nominative dual masculine: anger and concealment of faults.
- **ca** — “and,” completing the list of derivative afflictions under discussion.
- **rāga-utthāḥ** — nominative plural masculine: “arisen from attachment.”
- **āhrīkya-auddhatya-matsarāḥ** — nominative plural masculine: shamelessness, restlessness, and avarice.
- **mrakṣe** — locative singular: “with regard to concealment.”
- **vivādaḥ** — nominative singular: disagreement or doctrinal dispute.
- **avidyātaḥ** — ablative singular: “from ignorance.”
- **styāna-middha-anapatrapāḥ** — nominative plural: torpor, languor, and lack of moral apprehension.

## Literal Translation

Anger and concealment [are also derivative afflictions]. Shamelessness, restlessness, and avarice arise from attachment. Concerning concealment there is disagreement. Torpor, languor, and lack of moral apprehension arise from ignorance.

## Philosophical Translation

The manifest afflictive field is not merely enumerated; it is traced back to its generative roots. Attachment gives rise to shamelessness, restless dissipation, and avaricious withholding. Ignorance gives rise to torpor, languor, and the absence of moral apprehension. Concealment is disputed because its immediate manifestation can be read as rooted in attachment, ignorance, or both.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.47 enumerated eight envelopments. VAK 5.48 now begins to assign them to their roots.

```text
manifest affliction
    ↓ analysis of origin
root kleśa
```

The verse gives two principal derivations:

```text
rāga
    ↓
āhrīkya
uddhava / auddhatya
mātsarya
```

and:

```text
avidyā
    ↓
styāna
middha
anapatrāpya
```

The structure is not arbitrary. Each manifestation expresses the characteristic logic of its root.

Attachment appropriates and retains:

```text
rāga
    → shameless pursuit
    → restless movement toward objects
    → withholding possession
```

Ignorance obscures discrimination:

```text
avidyā
    → mental heaviness
    → collapse of alertness
    → failure to apprehend the unwholesome as unwholesome
```

The disputed case, **mrakṣa**, is especially revealing. Concealment of faults may be read as:

```text
attachment
    because one protects status, gain, or self-image
```

or:

```text
ignorance
    because one fails to disclose and know the fault truthfully
```

or as a product of both.

## Technical Vocabulary

### Krodha

A sharpened eruption of hostility distinct from the more basic root **pratigha**.

```text
pratigha
    root resistance

krodha
    manifest outbreak of that resistance
```

### Mrakṣa

Concealment or covering of one’s fault.

It is not simple silence. It is the active refusal to allow a fault to become manifest within a field of judgment.

Working translation:

```text
mrakṣa
    = concealment of fault
```

### Āhrīkya

Absence of inward moral restraint or self-respect.

The agent no longer recoils from what is base because attachment to the desired object overrides inward evaluation.

### Auddhatya

Restless elevation, agitation, or dissipation.

It is attachment as a kinetic inability to remain gathered.

### Mātsarya

Avarice or withholding.

The object is not merely enjoyed but retained against participation or distribution.

### Styāna

Mental torpor or incapacity for active engagement.

### Middha

Languor, drowsiness, or collapse of alert presence.

### Anapatrāpya

Absence of concern before others or before the objective order of consequences.

It is closely related to **āhrīkya**, but the two differ by standpoint:

```text
āhrīkya
    absence of inward restraint

anapatrāpya
    absence of outward moral apprehension
```

## Doctrinal Determination

The verse establishes that derivative afflictions are intelligible only through their roots.

This gives a doctrinal rule:

```text
do not abandon only the manifestation
trace the manifestation to its generating kleśa
```

A path that treats restlessness, avarice, torpor, or shamelessness as isolated symptoms remains superficial. Their removal requires insight into the root relation that sustains them.

Thus:

```text
auddhatya is not merely excess motion
it is rāga in the mode of dispersion

mātsarya is not merely stinginess
it is rāga in the mode of retention

styāna is not merely fatigue
it is avidyā in the mode of incapacity
```

The Path therefore requires discriminative diagnosis.

## Logical Determination

VAK 5.48 introduces a generative classification:

```text
root determination
    ↓ produces
derivative determination
```

The relation is neither identity nor external causation.

The derivative preserves the root while specifying its mode:

```text
rāga
    as restless expansion
    = auddhatya

rāga
    as restrictive retention
    = mātsarya

avidyā
    as obscured activity
    = styāna
```

This is a logic of manifestation:

```text
essence
    becomes visible
through its determinate appearance
```

The disputed status of **mrakṣa** shows that one manifestation may admit more than one root-analysis. Logical diagnosis must therefore allow convergent grounding rather than forcing every derivative into a single exclusive lineage.

## Path-Rationale

This verse directly serves the rationale of the Path.

The Path does not proceed by condemning manifest states. It asks:

```text
what root relation must be transformed
for this manifestation no longer to arise?
```

The practical sequence is:

```text
recognize the manifestation
    ↓
discriminate its root
    ↓
understand its generative relation
    ↓
apply the corresponding abandonment
```

This is why Abhidharma analysis can reveal the Path: it turns moral and contemplative experience into a determinate map of conditions and transformations.

## Organon Note

VAK 5.48 suggests a dependency model rather than a flat taxonomy.

```text
RootAffliction
    generates
DerivativeAffliction
```

But the relation should permit disputed or multiple grounding:

```text
Mrakṣa
    mayDeriveFrom Rāga
    mayDeriveFrom Avidyā
```

The ontology should therefore distinguish:

```text
canonical derivation
probable derivation
disputed derivation
multiple derivation
```

This is crucial for a Speculative Reason action plan. The system must not merely classify a manifest state; it must infer which deeper relation is active and what transformation would remove it.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. Does the Bhāṣya explicitly classify **krodha** under pratigha here, or defer that determination to VAK 5.49?
2. What are the exact competing positions concerning **mrakṣa**?
3. Is **āhrīkya** derived from rāga because attachment overcomes inward restraint?
4. Why is **anapatrāpya** assigned to avidyā rather than rāga?
5. Does **uddhava** in the transmitted text represent **auddhatya**, and how does the Bhāṣya gloss the term?
6. Are these derivations causal, niḥṣyanda-relations, or classificatory affinities?
7. How should multiple-root derivation be represented without collapsing doctrinal disagreement?
