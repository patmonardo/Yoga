# VAK 5.24

## Sanskrit

सर्वत्रानागतैरेभिर्मानसैः स्वाध्विके परैः ।
अजैः सर्वत्र शेषैस्तु सर्वैः सर्वत्र संयुतः ॥ ५.२४ ॥

## Grammar

- `sarvatra` — everywhere; with respect to all objects.
- `anāgataiḥ` — instrumental plural of `anāgata`: by future afflictions.
- `ebhiḥ` — by these; referring back to `rāga`, `pratigha`, and `māna` from 5.23.
- `mānasaiḥ` — mental; belonging to `manovijñāna` rather than one of the five sensory consciousnesses.
- `svādhvike` — with respect to what belongs to their own temporal period; here, future objects.
- `paraiḥ` — by the others; the non-mental, sense-linked cases distinguished from the preceding mental ones.
- `ajaiḥ` — by those not destined to arise; from `ajāta` or, doctrinally, future afflictions lacking the determination to actual occurrence.
- `śeṣaiḥ` — by the remaining anuśayas, beyond the three particular afflictions treated in 5.23–5.24.
- `sarvaiḥ` — by all of them, irrespective of temporal mode.
- `saṃyutaḥ` — bound, conjoined, or fettered.

## Literal translation

By these future afflictions, when mental, one is bound everywhere; by the others, to what belongs to their own temporal period. By those not destined to arise, one is bound everywhere. But by all the remaining afflictions, one is bound everywhere.

## Philosophical translation

Future attachment, aversion, and pride bind universally when they belong to mental consciousness, whose object-range extends across the three times. Their sense-linked forms bind only to future objects, the objects of their own temporal domain. If those future afflictions are not destined actually to arise, their binding range is nevertheless universal. All the remaining anuśayas bind universally in every temporal mode.

## Analysis

Verse 5.23 established a restricted rule for past and present `rāga`, `pratigha`, and `māna`:

```text
an affliction binds
only to the object
with respect to which it arose
and remains unabandoned
```

Verse 5.24 now gives the future cases and distinguishes them by the cognitive system in which they occur.

### 1. Future mental afflictions

When future attachment, aversion, or pride belong to `manovijñāna`, they bind with respect to all objects:

```text
past objects
present objects
future objects
```

The ground is the range of mental consciousness. Unlike the five sensory consciousnesses, `manovijñāna` can take objects belonging to all three times.

Thus:

```text
future mental anuśaya
    + tritemporal object-range
    = universal binding-range
```

### 2. Future sense-linked afflictions

The `paraiḥ`, the others, are the future attachment and aversion associated with the five sensory consciousnesses. These bind only to objects belonging to their own temporal period:

```text
future sense-consciousness
    → future object
```

The five sensory consciousnesses apprehend objects contemporaneous with their occurrence. Their future afflictive forms therefore bind only to future objects.

Pride is absent from this sensory group because `māna` belongs to mental consciousness.

### 3. Future afflictions not destined to arise

The term `ajaiḥ` introduces a second distinction within the future:

```text
future and destined to arise
future but not destined to arise
```

When future attachment and aversion are not destined to arise, the restriction to a contemporaneous sensory object no longer applies in the same way. They bind universally.

This gives a striking result:

```text
not destined to manifest
    does not mean
without binding efficacy
```

A future anuśaya may never become an occurrent affliction and still participate in the structure of bondage.

### 4. The remaining anuśayas

The final half states that the remaining anuśayas bind everywhere in all temporal modes.

The special object-restriction of 5.23 applies only to the particular afflictions:

```text
rāga
pratigha
māna
```

because these are particularizing afflictions that arise with respect to determinate objects. The remaining anuśayas possess a broader object-range and therefore bind universally.

## Doctrinal determination

The verse distinguishes binding power according to three variables:

```text
1. affliction-type
2. cognitive carrier
3. temporal destiny
```

The same broad affliction may have a different binding range depending on whether it is carried by mental or sensory consciousness:

```text
mental future rāga / pratigha
    universal temporal object-range

sensory future rāga / pratigha
    future-object range only
```

The verse therefore refuses to treat an anuśaya as an isolated mental item. Its function depends upon the consciousness-system through which it operates.

It also distinguishes:

```text
future existence
    from
future actualization
```

An affliction may be future without being destined to arise. Nevertheless, it can still bind. Binding power is therefore not reducible to manifest occurrence.

## Logical determination

Verse 5.24 introduces a logic of modal and relational scope.

The range of bondage is determined by the range of the cognition:

```text
scope of carrier
    determines
scope of relation
```

Thus:

```text
manovijñāna
    tritemporal object-scope
    → universal binding

sense-vijñāna
    contemporaneous object-scope
    → own-period binding
```

This is not merely a classification of afflictions. It is a rule for propagating a relation from one system component to another:

```text
cognitive scope
    → object scope
        → binding scope
```

The verse also separates three modal statuses:

```text
actual
possible and destined
possible but not destined
```

The last is especially important. A determination can be non-actualizing yet remain structurally effective.

```text
manifestation-status
    ≠
relational efficacy
```

## Organon note

The verse suggests a direct platform principle:

```text
relation range
    is inherited from
carrier range
```

A state or disposition should not be modeled independently of the module through which it operates.

```text
Anusaya
    carriedBy Consciousness
    inherits ObjectScope
    determines BindingScope
```

It also strengthens the need for explicit modal distinctions:

```text
FutureState
    ├── DestinedToArise
    └── NotDestinedToArise
```

A state that will never manifest may still constrain the system. This resembles a latent rule, policy, or unresolved disposition that remains structurally active despite never becoming an observable event.

The practical lesson is:

```text
absence of manifestation
    is not evidence
of absence of determination
```
