# VAK 5.28

## Sanskrit

प्रहीणे दुःखदृग्घेये संयुक्तः शेषसर्वगैः ।
प्राक्प्रहीणे प्रकारे च शेषैस्तद्विषयैर्मलैः ॥ ५.२८ ॥

## Grammar

- `prahīṇe` — locative singular of the past passive participle from `pra-hā`: when abandoned, in that which has been abandoned.
- `duḥkhadṛggheye` — locative singular compound: in what is to be abandoned by seeing suffering (`duḥkhadarśanaheya`).
- `saṃyuktaḥ` — nominative singular: connected, bound, conjoined.
- `śeṣasarvagaiḥ` — instrumental plural: by the remaining universally ranging afflictions.
- `prākprahīṇe` — locative singular: in a type or grade previously abandoned.
- `prakāre` — locative singular: in a kind, mode, or grade.
- `śeṣaiḥ` — instrumental plural: by the remaining ones.
- `tadviṣayaiḥ` — instrumental plural: having that as their object.
- `malaiḥ` — instrumental plural: by stains or defilements.

## Literal translation

When what is abandoned by seeing suffering has been abandoned, one remains connected with it through the remaining universally ranging afflictions. And when a grade has previously been abandoned, one remains connected with it through the remaining defilements that take it as their object.

## Philosophical translation

Abandonment of an afflictive class does not by itself amount to complete release from every relation to that class. Even after the factors abandoned by seeing suffering have been eliminated, the remaining universal afflictions may still bind the practitioner to them as objects. Likewise, during cultivation, an earlier grade already abandoned can remain an object for the defilements belonging to the grades not yet eliminated.

## Analysis

Verses 5.23–5.24 analyzed bondage in relation to an object across temporal modes. Verses 5.25–5.27 then opened the theory of temporal existence and operative function required to explain that bondage. Verse 5.28 returns to the practical path and makes a decisive distinction:

```text
abandonment of a dharma
    ≠
complete disconnection from that dharma
```

The first case belongs to the path of seeing.

When knowledge of suffering has arisen but knowledge of origination has not yet arisen, the class abandoned by seeing suffering has already been abandoned. Yet universal afflictions belonging to what is abandoned by seeing origination can still take that abandoned class as their object.

Thus:

```text
factor abandoned
    but
object-relation not yet exhausted
```

The second case belongs to the path of cultivation.

The afflictions abandoned through cultivation are analyzed into grades. When an earlier grade has been eliminated, the remaining higher or lower grades not yet eliminated may still take that abandoned grade as their object.

Thus:

```text
one grade eliminated
    while
other grades still objectify it
```

The verse therefore distinguishes at least three states:

```text
1. present possession of an affliction
2. abandonment of that affliction
3. complete release from every remaining relation to it
```

The third cannot be inferred merely from the second.

## Technical vocabulary

```text
prahāṇa
    abandonment
    elimination of an afflictive factor

saṃyoga
    connection or bondage
    here retained through another affliction's object-relation

sarvaga
    universally ranging affliction
    an affliction capable of taking a broad class of contaminated dharmas as object

prakāra
    kind, mode, or grade
    here a graded form of an affliction abandoned through cultivation

viṣaya
    object or object-domain
    that toward which a cognition or affliction is directed

mala
    stain or defilement
```

## Doctrinal determination

The verse establishes that abandonment is relation-specific.

```text
an affliction may be abandoned as possessed
    while
another unabandoned affliction still relates to it as object
```

Accordingly, liberation cannot be modeled as a single Boolean transition:

```text
present → absent
```

It requires a relational account:

```text
is possessed by
is generated by
is taken as object by
is abandoned through
remains connected through
```

A dharma can cease to be an active possession while remaining implicated in the network of bondage through another dharma's object-directed activity.

## Logical determination

Verse 5.28 moves from simple negation to determinate negation.

```text
simple negation
    this affliction is no longer present

determinate negation
    this affliction is abandoned in one respect
    but remains related in another respect
```

This is why `prahīṇa` does not immediately entail complete `visaṃyoga`.

The first negation removes the dharma as an operative possession. The second task is to remove the remaining relations through which the field can still be organized around it.

The logical form is:

```text
A is negated as member
    while
A persists as object of B
```

Therefore:

```text
negation of membership
    ≠
negation of relation
```

The path must successively negate both.

## Organon note

This is a crucial stance for an operational system.

A state cannot be treated as fully cleared merely because its direct instance has been removed.

```text
DirectStateRemoved
    does not imply
AllReferencesResolved
```

The Platform therefore needs to distinguish:

```text
hasActiveAffliction
hasAbandonedAffliction
hasObjectRelationTo
remainsBoundThrough
isFullyReleasedFrom
```

A `DoesNotKnow` event, an error state, or an abandoned hypothesis may no longer be active while still structuring later cognition through unresolved references, cached assumptions, or dependent rules.

So complete release requires garbage collection of relations, not merely deletion of the node.

## Bhāṣya questions

1. How precisely does the Bhāṣya distinguish `prahāṇa` from complete `visaṃyoga`?
2. Which `sarvaga` afflictions abandoned by seeing origination continue to take the suffering-class as object?
3. In the cultivation case, how are the nine grades ordered, and which remaining grades can objectify a previously abandoned grade?
4. Does the persistence of object-relation imply any continued causal efficacy of the abandoned dharma itself, or only efficacy of the remaining affliction?
5. How does this distinction relate to `prāpti`, possession, and the later theory of abandonment?
