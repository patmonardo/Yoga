# VAK 5.67

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

आर्यमार्गस्य सर्वाः द्वे लौकिकस्य अन्वयस्य च ।
धर्मज्ञानस्य तिस्रस्तु षट् तत्पक्षस्य पञ्च च ॥ ५.६७ ॥

### IAST

āryamārgasya sarvāḥ dve laukikasya anvayasya ca /
dharmajñānasya tisras tu ṣaṭ tatpakṣasya pañca ca // 5.67 //

## Padaccheda

ārya-mārgasya | sarvāḥ | dve | laukikasya | anvayasya | ca | dharma-jñānasya | tisraḥ | tu | ṣaṭ | tat-pakṣasya | pañca | ca

## Grammar

- **ārya-mārgasya** — genitive singular masculine compound: “of the noble path.”
- **sarvāḥ** — nominative plural feminine, referring to all nine **parijñās**.
- **dve** — nominative dual feminine: two.
- **laukikasya** — genitive singular masculine: “of the mundane path.”
- **anvayasya** — genitive singular masculine, referring here to **anvayajñāna**: inferential or subsequent knowledge directed toward the higher domains.
- **ca** — and.
- **dharma-jñānasya** — genitive singular neuter compound: “of dharma-knowledge,” the knowledge directed toward the truths of the desire-domain.
- **tisraḥ** — nominative plural feminine: three.
- **tu** — but, however; marks a contrast with the preceding dual classification.
- **ṣaṭ** — six.
- **tat-pakṣasya** — genitive singular: “of its side,” “of its class,” or “of the group belonging to it.” In the Bhāṣya this means the full side comprising both the corresponding **kṣāntis** and **jñānas**.
- **pañca** — five.
- **ca** — and.

The kārikā is highly compressed. Its distribution, as unpacked by the Bhāṣya, is:

```text
noble path                 → all nine
mundane path               → two
anvayajñāna                → two
dharmajñāna                → three
dharmajñāna-side           → six
anvayajñāna-side           → five
```

## Literal Translation

All [nine complete discriminations] are fruits of the noble path; two are fruits of the mundane path and also of inferential knowledge. Three are fruits of dharma-knowledge; six are fruits of its side, and five of the other side.

## Philosophical Translation

Every completed abandonment belongs to the efficacy of the noble path. The mundane path reaches only two such completions. Inferential knowledge, which operates upon the higher domains, likewise grounds two. Dharma-knowledge, whose counteractive scope extends through all three domains in cultivation, grounds three. When each knowledge is taken together with its associated moments of patience and knowledge, the dharma-knowledge side yields six complete discriminations, while the inferential side yields five.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.66 classified the nine **parijñās** by meditative basis.

VAK 5.67 adds another coordinate:

```text
not:
where is the path supported?

but:
which cognitive-path operation
produces which completed abandonment?
```

The verse therefore distributes the same nine results across six operative classes.

### 1. The noble path

```text
āryamārga
    → all nine parijñās
```

This is the comprehensive case. Every complete discrimination, whether attained through seeing or cultivation, is properly a fruit of the noble path.

The category is universal because the noble path includes the uncontaminated operations by which the afflictions are definitively abandoned.

### 2. The mundane path

```text
laukikamārga
    → two parijñās
```

The mundane path can suppress and abandon certain cultivated afflictions, but it does not generate the full ninefold completion.

Its range is therefore real but restricted.

The two intended results are those accessible through mundane detachment from lower levels, especially the completed abandonment of the lower fetters and the exhaustion associated with form-attachment.

### 3. Anvayajñāna

```text
anvayajñāna
    → two parijñās
```

**Anvayajñāna** concerns the truths as they apply to the higher domains of form and formlessness.

Its direct counteractive range therefore reaches the final two cultivation-stage completions connected with those higher domains.

### 4. Dharmajñāna

```text
dharmajñāna
    → three parijñās
```

Although **dharmajñāna** is originally directed toward the truths of the desire-domain, in the cultivation path its power functions as a counteragent with respect to afflictions throughout all three domains.

Therefore it can produce the final three parijñās:

```text
lower-fetter destruction
form-attachment destruction
all-influx destruction
```

This is the crucial asymmetry between the two knowledges.

### 5. The dharmajñāna side

```text
dharmajñāna-pakṣa
    → six parijñās
```

The word **pakṣa** expands the reference beyond dharmajñāna alone.

It includes:

```text
dharmajñāna-kṣānti
+
dharmajñāna
```

across the relevant moments of the Path.

Taken as a full operative side, it yields six completed abandonments.

### 6. The anvayajñāna side

```text
anvayajñāna-pakṣa
    → five parijñās
```

Likewise, the inferential side includes:

```text
anvayajñāna-kṣānti
+
anvayajñāna
```

Taken as a full group, it yields five completed abandonments.

## Technical Vocabulary

### Āryamārga

**Āryamārga** is the uncontaminated noble path.

In this verse it is not one path-moment among others but the comprehensive class under which all nine complete discriminations are attained.

```text
āryamārga
    = universal liberating range
```

### Laukikamārga

**Laukikamārga** is the mundane path of concentration and detachment.

It can remove certain afflictions through worldly counteragents, but it does not disclose or exhaust the complete structure of the Four Truths in the manner of the noble path.

Thus:

```text
mundane efficacy
    is genuine
but
mundane efficacy
    is not universal
```

### Dharmajñāna

**Dharmajñāna** is knowledge of the truths in relation to the desire-domain.

Its significance in this verse is not merely its object but its operative reach in cultivation.

The Bhāṣya states that it can counteract the afflictions abandoned through cultivation in all three domains.

Therefore:

```text
object-domain
    ≠
counteractive range
```

A knowledge may arise with one truth-domain as its explicit object while functioning more broadly as a Path-counteragent.

### Anvayajñāna

**Anvayajñāna** is knowledge of the truths as subsequently or inferentially applicable to the higher domains.

Its range is more specifically directed upward:

```text
form-domain
+
formless-domain
```

Consequently its direct parijñā-fruits are fewer than those of dharmajñāna.

### Pakṣa

**Pakṣa** means side, class, or operative group.

Here it is doctrinally precise:

```text
knowledge-side
    includes
patience + knowledge
```

The verse therefore distinguishes the fruit-range of a single knowledge from the fruit-range of the complete sequence belonging to that knowledge-family.

## Doctrinal Determination

The verse establishes that a completed abandonment may be classified from several standpoints without contradiction.

The same parijñā can be:

```text
a fruit of the noble path
and
a fruit of a particular knowledge
and
a fruit of a larger knowledge-side
```

These are not rival causes. They are nested descriptions at different levels of abstraction.

### Universal and restricted causes

```text
noble path
    universal cause-class

mundane path
    restricted cause-class

dharmajñāna / anvayajñāna
    determinate cognitive operations

knowledge-side
    extended operative family
```

The doctrine is therefore not a flat one-to-one assignment.

It is a layered causal classification.

### The asymmetry of dharmajñāna and anvayajñāna

The numbers are not symmetrical:

```text
dharmajñāna           → three
anvayajñāna            → two

dharmajñāna-side      → six
anvayajñāna-side       → five
```

The difference follows from counteractive range.

Dharmajñāna, though anchored in the desire-domain truth-complex, functions in cultivation against afflictions of all three domains.

Anvayajñāna is restricted to the higher domains.

Thus the lower-domain knowledge is not logically inferior. It is more universal in practical counteraction.

### Mundane path and noble path

The mundane path can attain two parijñās, but all nine are fruits of the noble path.

This means:

```text
worldly detachment
    may accomplish real abandonment

but

complete discriminative structure
    belongs to the noble path
```

The mundane path can change the practitioner's relation to a domain. The noble path comprehends and completes abandonment under the truth-structure itself.

## Logical Determination

VAK 5.67 presents a many-to-many relation.

```text
PathOperation × Parijñā
    → fruit relation
```

A single parijñā may have several valid classifications because the predicates differ.

For example:

```text
a final parijñā
    may be fruit of
    āryamārga
    dharmajñāna
    dharmajñāna-pakṣa
```

No contradiction follows because:

```text
comprehensive class
    ≠
specific cognition
    ≠
full cognitive family
```

### Set structure

The verse can be represented as nested result-sets:

```text
ĀryamārgaFruitSet
    = 9

LaukikaFruitSet
    = 2

DharmajñānaFruitSet
    = 3

AnvayajñānaFruitSet
    = 2

DharmajñānaPakṣaFruitSet
    = 6

AnvayajñānaPakṣaFruitSet
    = 5
```

The numbers measure operative range, not intrinsic dignity.

### Direct operation and family operation

The distinction between **jñāna** and **tatpakṣa** yields:

```text
single determinate operation
    versus
complete sequence of related operations
```

Thus:

```text
dharmajñāna alone
    → 3

dharmajñāna-family
    → 6
```

and:

```text
anvayajñāna alone
    → 2

anvayajñāna-family
    → 5
```

The full family contains the preparatory patience-moments that produce additional abandonments.

## Path Determination

The Path is not one undifferentiated power.

It consists of operations with different ranges:

```text
mundane detachment
noble discrimination
dharma-knowledge
inferential knowledge
patience-knowledge sequences
```

The practical question is therefore not merely:

```text
Has an affliction been abandoned?
```

but:

```text
By which path-operation?
At what level?
With what range?
As the fruit of which cognitive sequence?
```

This matters because an apparent result may be:

```text
temporary worldly detachment
or
definitive noble abandonment
```

The external appearance may be similar while the ground of the result differs.

The verse therefore sharpens the markers of seeing:

```text
result
    ↓
causal path-class
    ↓
specific cognitive operation
    ↓
full operative family
```

## Organon Note

VAK 5.67 requires a distinction among three causal levels.

```text
PathClass
    āryamārga
    laukikamārga

KnowledgeOperation
    dharmajñāna
    anvayajñāna

KnowledgeFamily
    dharmajñāna-pakṣa
    anvayajñāna-pakṣa
```

A parijñā should not have a single `hasCause` field.

It requires typed relations:

```text
isFruitOfPathClass
isFruitOfKnowledge
isFruitOfKnowledgeFamily
```

Suggested structure:

```text
Parijñā
    isFruitOfPathClass → Āryamārga
    isFruitOfKnowledge → Dharmajñāna
    isFruitOfKnowledgeFamily → DharmajñānaPakṣa
```

This prevents the model from collapsing nested causal descriptions into competing alternatives.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. Which two parijñās are explicitly assigned to the mundane path?
2. Why are the same two assigned to anvayajñāna?
3. How does dharmajñāna, whose object is the desire-domain truth-complex, function as counteragent for cultivation-stage afflictions in all three domains?
4. Does **tatpakṣa** in the second half refer first to the dharmajñāna side and then, by ellipsis, to the anvayajñāna side?
5. Which six parijñās are fruits of the dharmajñāna side?
6. Which five are fruits of the anvayajñāna side?
7. How exactly are the corresponding **kṣāntis** and **jñānas** counted within each side?
8. Are these fruit-relations simultaneous classifications or temporally distinct acquisitions?
9. How does this classification coordinate with the meditative-basis classification in VAK 5.66?
10. Why does the Kośa preserve both a universal noble-path classification and narrower knowledge-specific classifications?

## Core Determination

```text
All nine complete discriminations
are fruits of the noble path.

Restricted path and knowledge operations
produce restricted subsets.

The complete knowledge-sides,
including patience and knowledge,
have a wider fruit-range
than either knowledge taken alone.
```
