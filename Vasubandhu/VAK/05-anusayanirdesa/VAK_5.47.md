# VAK 5.47

## Sanskrit

आह्रीक्यमनपत्राप्यमीर्ष्यामात्सर्यमुद्धवः ।
कौकृत्यं स्त्यानमिद्धं च पर्यवस्थानमष्टधा ॥ ५.४७ ॥

## IAST

āhrīkyam anapatrāpyam īrṣyā mātsaryam uddhavaḥ /
kaukṛtyaṃ styānamiddhaṃ ca paryavasthānam aṣṭadhā // 5.47 //

## Padaccheda

āhrīkyam | anapatrāpyam | īrṣyā | mātsaryam | uddhavaḥ | kaukṛtyam | styānam | middham | ca | paryavasthānam | aṣṭadhā

## Grammar

- **āhrīkyam** — nominative singular neuter, “shamelessness,” absence of inward moral restraint.
- **anapatrāpyam** — nominative singular neuter, “lack of moral concern before others,” absence of outward ethical restraint.
- **īrṣyā** — nominative singular feminine, “envy,” distress at another’s excellence or gain.
- **mātsaryam** — nominative singular neuter, “avarice,” possessive withholding of one’s own advantage or resources.
- **uddhavaḥ** — nominative singular masculine in the transmitted kārikā text. The precise reading and relation to the standard term **auddhatya** should be checked against the critical text and Bhāṣya.
- **kaukṛtyam** — nominative singular neuter, “remorse,” the troubled reworking of an action as badly done or omitted.
- **styānam** — nominative singular neuter, “mental torpor,” incapacity or heaviness of mind.
- **middham** — nominative singular neuter, “sleepiness,” bodily-mental sinking or dullness.
- **ca** — “and.”
- **paryavasthānam** — nominative singular neuter, “envelopment,” an affliction insofar as it occupies and constrains the mental continuum.
- **aṣṭadhā** — indeclinable, “eightfold.”

## Literal Translation

Shamelessness, lack of moral concern, envy, avarice, uddhava, remorse, torpor, and sleepiness: envelopment is eightfold.

## Philosophical Translation

The derivative afflictive field becomes manifest as eight enveloping formations: the collapse of inward and outward restraint, antagonism toward another’s good, withholding of one’s own good, agitated elevation, remorseful recoil, mental incapacity, and sinking dullness.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.46 established the general relation:

```text
kleśa
    ↓ generates
upakleśa
```

VAK 5.47 now selects eight derivative afflictions under the name **paryavasthāna**:

```text
āhrīkya
anapatrāpya
īrṣyā
mātsarya
uddhava / auddhatya
kaukṛtya
styāna
middha
```

The movement is therefore:

```text
root affliction
    ↓
derivative determination
    ↓
active occupation of the continuum
```

**Paryavasthāna** is not merely another synonym for affliction. It names the derivative determination when it has become sufficiently manifest to surround, occupy, or constrain the field of mind.

## Technical Vocabulary

### Paryavasthāna

From **pari-ava-√sthā**, with the sense of standing around, settling over, or enclosing.

Working translation:

```text
paryavasthāna
    =
envelopment
```

The term expresses manifest domination:

```text
an afflictive determination
    becomes
an occupied mental field
```

### Āhrīkya and Anapatrāpya

These form a pair of failed restraint:

```text
āhrīkya
    absence of inward self-restraint

anapatrāpya
    absence of restraint before others or the normative field
```

Their distinction is not simply private versus public emotion. Together they mark the collapse of the two directions through which conduct is checked:

```text
relation to oneself
relation to the shared ethical order
```

### Īrṣyā and Mātsarya

These form a relational pair:

```text
īrṣyā
    negation of another’s good

mātsarya
    withholding of one’s own good
```

The first reacts against possession outside oneself. The second contracts around possession within oneself.

### Kaukṛtya

Remorse is not yet liberating recognition. It is the repetitive affective return to an action as done badly or left undone.

```text
action
    ↓ retrospective fixation
kaukṛtya
```

### Styāna and Middha

These form a pair of incapacity:

```text
styāna
    mental non-pliancy

middha
    sinking dullness or sleepiness
```

One inhibits the capacity to initiate cognition; the other withdraws the continuum from alert engagement.

## Doctrinal Determination

The eight envelopments are not eight new root afflictions. They are manifest derivative formations.

The distinction is:

```text
kleśa
    causal-root determination

upakleśa
    dependent derivative determination

paryavasthāna
    derivative determination in active occupation
```

Thus a single dharma can be considered under more than one doctrinal standpoint:

```text
as derivative
as manifest
as obstructive
```

VAK 5.47 gives the manifest standpoint.

The list also reveals four paired structures:

```text
failed restraint
    āhrīkya / anapatrāpya

failed reciprocity
    īrṣyā / mātsarya

agitation and recoil
    uddhava / kaukṛtya

incapacity and sinking
    styāna / middha
```

The exact internal grouping should remain provisional until checked against the Bhāṣya, especially the transmitted **uddhavaḥ**.

## Logical Determination

The verse moves from derivation to manifestation.

```text
essence
    root affliction

reflection
    derivative determination

immediacy of reflection
    envelopment
```

An envelopment is therefore an immediate determination of a reflected ground. It does not posit its own basis; it expresses a kleśa already operating beneath it.

This gives the logical sequence:

```text
ground
    ↓
derived form
    ↓
occupied field
```

The eightfold list is not arbitrary enumeration. It distributes envelopment across basic modes in which agency becomes unavailable to itself:

```text
normative failure
relational contraction
agitated disturbance
incapacitation
```

The decisive point is that reflection becomes immediate as a determinate state.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. Does the Bhāṣya read **uddhavaḥ**, **auddhatyam**, or another form in this verse?
2. Why are precisely these eight called **paryavasthāna**?
3. Does **paryavasthāna** name active manifestation, intensified presence, or enclosure of the mental continuum?
4. Are the eight arranged as four doctrinal pairs, or is that only a useful logical reconstruction?
5. How are **styāna** and **middha** distinguished in terms of mental and bodily incapacity?
6. Is **kaukṛtya** always afflicted, or does the Bhāṣya distinguish wholesome regret from afflictive remorse?
7. Which root kleśas generate each of these eight? VAK 5.48–5.49 should provide the immediate answer.
