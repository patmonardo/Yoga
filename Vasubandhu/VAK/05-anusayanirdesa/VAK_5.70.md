# VAK 5.70

## Sanskrit

### Devanāgarī

तासां संकलनं धातुवैराग्यफललाभतः ।
एकां द्वे पञ्च षट् कश्चिज्जहात्याप्नोति पञ्च न ॥ ५.७० ॥

### IAST

tāsāṃ saṃkalanaṃ dhātu-vairāgya-phala-lābhataḥ |
ekāṃ dve pañca ṣaṭ kaścij jahāty āpnoti pañca na || 5.70 ||

## Padaccheda

tāsām | saṃkalanam | dhātu-vairāgya-phala-lābhataḥ | ekām | dve | pañca | ṣaṭ | kaścit | jahāti | āpnoti | pañca | na

## Grammar

- **tāsām** — genitive plural feminine: “of those,” referring to the nine **parijñās** enumerated in VAK 5.64–5.65.
- **saṃkalanam** — nominative/accusative singular neuter from **sam-√kal**, “collecting together,” “consolidation,” “reduction to a combined unit.” Here it denotes the reorganization of several separately counted parijñās into a more comprehensive attainment.
- **dhātu-vairāgya-phala-lābhataḥ** — ablative singular compound expressing the grounds or occasions of consolidation: “from dispassion toward a domain and from the attainment of a fruit.”
- **dhātu** — domain.
- **vairāgya** — dispassion, freedom from attachment, or detachment from a domain.
- **phala-lābha** — attainment of a Path-fruit.
- **ekām** — accusative singular feminine: one parijñā.
- **dve** — accusative dual feminine: two parijñās.
- **pañca** — accusative plural: five.
- **ṣaṭ** — accusative plural: six.
- **kaścit** — nominative singular masculine indefinite pronoun: “a certain practitioner,” “someone.”
- **jahāti** — third-person singular present from **√hā**: abandons, relinquishes, loses.
- **āpnoti** — third-person singular present from **ā-√āp**: obtains, attains, acquires.
- **pañca na** — “not five.” The prohibition qualifies acquisition: five may be relinquished, but five are not acquired as a set.

The second half is elliptical. Its most economical reconstruction is:

```text
one relinquishes
    one, two, five, or six;

one acquires
    one, two, or six,
    but not five.
```

## Literal Translation

Their consolidation occurs through dispassion toward a domain and through attainment of a fruit. A certain practitioner relinquishes one, two, five, or six; one acquires [one, two, or six], but not five.

## Philosophical Translation

The separately counted complete discriminations are reorganized when an entire domain is transcended or when a new Path-fruit is attained. At such transitions, a practitioner may cease to possess one, two, five, or six parijñās under their former classification, and may acquire one, two, or six under a new and more comprehensive classification—but never five as a newly acquired total.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.69 counted how many parijñās are possessed at different Path-stages.

VAK 5.70 explains how those counts change.

```text
stage-accounting
    → transition-accounting
```

The verse identifies two transition conditions:

```text
dhātu-vairāgya
    transcendence of a domain

phala-lābha
    attainment of a Path-fruit
```

When either occurs, the practitioner’s parijñā-set is recalculated.

```text
former partial completions
    ↓
consolidation
    ↓
new comprehensive completion
```

The key term is **saṃkalana**.

It does not mean that previously destroyed afflictions return. Nor does it mean that the practitioner becomes ignorant of an already realized truth.

It means that several earlier attainments cease to be counted separately because a later attainment contains their operative range in a more comprehensive form.

```text
loss of separate count
    ≠
loss of liberation
```

## Technical Vocabulary

### Saṃkalana

**Saṃkalana** is consolidation or collection into a larger unit.

The nine parijñās are not nine independent possessions arranged side by side without transformation. Their numerical form changes as the Path develops.

```text
plural determinate attainments
    become
one more comprehensive attainment
```

Thus the Path has a logic of supersession:

```text
earlier attainment
    preserved in effect
    but no longer counted separately
```

### Dhātu-vairāgya

**Dhātu-vairāgya** is dispassion toward an entire domain.

A domain is not transcended merely because a particular affliction belonging to it is temporarily absent. The relevant attachment to that domain must be overcome as a whole.

```text
local abandonment
    concerns a determinate affliction

domain-dispassion
    concerns the entire operative field
```

When a whole domain is crossed, earlier parijñās attached to partial abandonments within that domain are gathered into a more comprehensive determination.

### Phala-lābha

**Phala-lābha** is attainment of a fruit.

A fruit is not merely another event added to the sequence. It changes the practitioner’s status and therefore changes how prior abandonments are formally possessed and counted.

```text
new fruit
    → new Path-status
    → new parijñā accounting
```

### Jahāti and Āpnoti

The verse pairs:

```text
jahāti
    relinquishes

āpnoti
    acquires
```

These verbs must be read within the doctrine of attainment.

They do not imply:

```text
affliction destroyed
    then restored
```

They indicate:

```text
one attainment-configuration ends
    another attainment-configuration begins
```

### Pañca na

The final prohibition is exact:

```text
five
    may be relinquished

five
    are never acquired
```

The fivefold set is therefore transitional rather than terminal.

It can exist as the accumulated result of the Path of Seeing, but no later transition generates it as a newly acquired consolidated state.

## Doctrinal Determination

The nine parijñās are nominally distinct because they mark distinct completed abandonments. Yet their possession is not static.

VAK 5.70 establishes that their number depends upon the practitioner’s current Path-status.

The doctrinal structure is:

```text
abandonment itself
    irreversible

formal possession and count
    reorganizable
```

This agrees with VAK 5.63:

```text
kṣaya
    occurs once

visaṃyoga-lābha
    may occur repeatedly
```

VAK 5.70 applies the same distinction to the nine parijñās.

The destruction of affliction is not repeated. What changes is the attained configuration through which that destruction is possessed and named.

### Consolidation through domain-dispassion

When the practitioner becomes dispassionate toward an entire domain, earlier abandonments within that domain cease to remain isolated achievements.

```text
partial abandonments in one domain
    ↓
domain transcended
    ↓
one comprehensive completion
```

The whole domain has now lost its binding force.

### Consolidation through fruit-attainment

When a fruit is attained, the practitioner receives a new status.

That new status reorganizes the prior abandonments into the set appropriate to the fruit.

```text
prior path moments
    ↓
fruit attainment
    ↓
new possession structure
```

### Why five are not acquired

The fivefold possession belongs to a progressive accumulation before the completion of the Path of Seeing.

```text
one
    → two
    → three
    → four
    → five
```

But later transitions do not produce five as a consolidated result.

Once the relevant transition occurs, the Path moves to:

```text
six
or
one
or
two
```

as stated in VAK 5.69.

Therefore:

```text
five
    is an intermediate maximum
    not a fruit-generated state
```

## Logical Determination

The final verse presents the Path as a state-transition system.

Let:

```text
P
    = currently possessed parijñā-set

T
    = domain-dispassion or fruit-attainment
```

Then:

```text
T(P)
    = newly consolidated parijñā-set
```

The transformation is not simple addition.

```text
new attainment
    may reduce the number counted
```

For example:

```text
five separately counted completions
    + decisive transition
    → one or another smaller comprehensive set
```

Thus numerical decrease may express logical advance.

```text
smaller cardinality
    may contain
larger realized scope
```

This is the central formal lesson of the closing sequence.

### Permitted changes

The verse gives the permitted relinquishment counts:

```text
{1, 2, 5, 6}
```

and the permitted acquisition counts:

```text
{1, 2, 6}
```

with the explicit constraint:

```text
Acquire(5) = false
```

The system is therefore asymmetric.

```text
what may disappear as a formal set
    need not be producible as a later formal set
```

### Preservation through supersession

The logical relation is not cancellation but containment.

```text
earlier parijñās
    are not falsified

later parijñā
    contains their completed function
```

The Path therefore advances through determinate preservation:

```text
separation
    → accumulation
    → consolidation
```

## Path Determination

The final practical warning is against measuring progress by the number of explicitly recognized attainments.

```text
more named achievements
    ≠
more complete freedom
```

A practitioner may move from five parijñās to one, yet the one is more comprehensive because an entire domain has been crossed or a fruit attained.

The correct diagnostic question is not:

```text
How many attainments can I enumerate?
```

but:

```text
What field of bondage has actually lost its force?
```

The Path tests completion by scope.

```text
local insight
    may multiply distinctions

completed dispassion
    consolidates them
```

This makes the closing sequence reflexive. The practitioner must not appropriate the Path itself as a collection of possessions.

```text
attachment to attainments
    mistakes enumeration
    for liberation
```

The final verse instead presents attainment as a changing formal relation to completed abandonment.

## Organon Note

VAK 5.70 provides a direct model for state supersession.

```text
State A
    contains several completed determinations

Transition
    domain-dispassion or fruit-attainment

State B
    contains their unified result
```

The transition is neither deletion nor simple accumulation.

It is **consolidating sublation**:

```text
preserve realized effect
remove obsolete separate counting
raise the determinations into a wider state
```

For the Platform, the model should distinguish:

```text
HistoricalAttainment
    what was achieved at an earlier stage

CurrentPossession
    what is presently counted as possessed

ConsolidatedScope
    what operative range is included now
```

Suggested transition rule:

```text
PathTransition
    triggeredBy DomainDispassion | FruitAttainment
    relinquishes ParijnaPossessionSet
    acquires ParijnaPossessionSet
    preserves AbandonmentEffect
```

The invariant is:

```text
completed abandonment
    is never reversed
```

The variable is:

```text
formal mode of possession
```

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. Which exact Path-stages relinquish one, two, five, and six parijñās respectively?
2. Which exact transitions produce acquisitions of one, two, and six?
3. How does the Bhāṣya explain the impossibility of acquiring five?
4. Does **saṃkalana** mean that several distinct **prāptis** are replaced by one new **prāpti**, or that the designation **parijñā** is reassigned while the earlier acquisitions remain?
5. How are **dhātu-vairāgya** and **phala-lābha** coordinated when both occur at the same Path transition?
6. Does the fivefold set belong exclusively to the Path of Seeing before the knowledge of the Path, or are there alternative Vaibhāṣika enumerations?
7. How does the closing state-accounting anticipate the more detailed classifications of persons and fruits in Chapter 6?

## Core Determination

```text
The Path does not merely accumulate completed abandonments.

When a domain is transcended
or a fruit is attained,
those abandonments are consolidated
into a new possession-structure.

One may relinquish
one, two, five, or six;
one may acquire
one, two, or six,
but never five.

The count may decrease
while the realized scope increases.
```

```text

    the discussion of complete discrimination is concluded

abhidharmakośe 'nuśayanirdeśo nāma
pañcamaṃ kośasthānaṃ samāptam
    the fifth Treasury-section,
    called the Exposition of the Latent Afflictions,
    is completed
```

The chapter’s final movement is:

```text
anuśaya
    latent ground of bondage

kleśa
    manifest afflictive operation

prahāṇa
    determinate abandonment

visaṃyoga
    acquired separation

parijñā
    completed discrimination

saṃkalana
    consolidation at domain-transcendence and fruit
```

Thus the **Anuśayanirdeśa** ends not with a further description of affliction, but with the formal accounting of accomplished freedom.
