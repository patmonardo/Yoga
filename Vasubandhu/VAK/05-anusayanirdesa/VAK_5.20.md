## Source caution

The kārikā-only source repeats `akuśalamūlāni` in the third pāda. This cannot be the intended reading, because the second half then names `tṛṣṇā`, `avidyā`, and `mati` as the three roots under discussion after verse 5.19 has introduced the category of the ethically indeterminate.

The Bhāṣya explicitly asks:

```text
katy avyākṛtamūlāni?
```

and answers:

```text
trīṇy avyākṛtamūlāni
```

It then identifies them as:

```text
tṛṣṇā
avidyā
mati / prajñā
```

Accordingly, `avyākṛtamūlāni` is adopted as the doctrinally and grammatically required reading while the received kārikā text is preserved above.

## Grammar

- `kāme` — locative singular: in the desire-domain.
- `akuśalamūlāni` — nominative neuter plural: roots of the unwholesome.
- `rāga` — attachment or passion.
- `pratigha` — resistance, hostility, or aversion.
- `mūḍhayaḥ` — states or forms of delusion; the expression designates deluded ignorance apart from the ignorance conjoined with the two neutral views identified in 5.19.
- `trīṇi` — nominative neuter plural: three.
- `avyākṛtamūlāni` — nominative neuter plural: roots of the ethically indeterminate.
- `tṛṣṇā` — craving or thirst.
- `avidyā` — ignorance.
- `matiḥ` — cognition, understanding, or discriminative intelligence; the Bhāṣya glosses it as `prajñā`.
- `ca` — and.
- `sā` — that; it indicates that the three just named are to be taken in their ethically indeterminate form.

## Literal translation

In the desire-domain, attachment, hostility, and deluded states are roots of the unwholesome. The roots of the indeterminate are three: craving, ignorance, and that cognition.

## Philosophical translation

Within the desire-domain, the unwholesome is grounded in attachment, aversion, and deluded ignorance. The ethically indeterminate likewise has three roots: neutral craving, neutral ignorance, and neutral discriminative cognition.

## Analysis

Verse 5.19 classified the anuśayas according to ethical status:

```text
higher domains
    all anuśayas avyākṛta

desire-domain
    two views and their conjoined ignorance avyākṛta
    the remaining anuśayas aśubha
```

Verse 5.20 now asks which of these afflictive determinations function as roots.

The first triad is:

```text
rāga
pratigha
mūḍhi / deluded ignorance
```

These are not merely unwholesome states. They are `akuśalamūla`: generative grounds from which other unwholesome dharmas arise.

The qualification inherited from 5.19 is decisive. Not every ignorance in the desire-domain is an unwholesome root. The ignorance conjoined with satkāyadṛṣṭi and antagrāhadṛṣṭi is ethically indeterminate. Thus the third unwholesome root is the remaining unwholesome ignorance.

The second triad is:

```text
tṛṣṇā
avidyā
mati / prajñā
```

These are the roots of the ethically indeterminate. The final `sā` points back to the neutral determination:

```text
that tṛṣṇā which is avyākṛta
that avidyā which is avyākṛta
that mati which is avyākṛta
```

The Bhāṣya extends this category even to resultant cognition. Thus `mati` cannot be reduced here to liberating principle. It is cognitive determination as such, capable of functioning within an ethically neutral complex.

## Doctrinal determination

The verse distinguishes a dharma from a root:

```text
ethical state
    what a dharma is

ethical root
    what generates dharmas of that type
```

Accordingly:

```text
akuśala
    is broader than
akuśalamūla
```

Only those unwholesome afflictions that are themselves unwholesome and also generate further unwholesome dharmas count as roots.

The verse establishes two root-triads:

```text
unwholesome roots
    attachment
    hostility
    deluded ignorance

indeterminate roots
    craving
    ignorance
    cognition
```

The same broad determination may appear differently according to ethical mode. Attachment becomes `rāga` as an unwholesome root and `tṛṣṇā` as a neutral root. Ignorance likewise divides according to the ethical complex in which it functions.

## Logical determination

Verse 5.20 shifts from classification to generation.

```text
5.19
    ethical predication
    what status does the anuśaya possess?

5.20
    grounding relation
    which anuśayas generate dharmas of that status?
```

A root is not merely one member of a class. It is the immanent ground of reproduction of that class:

```text
mūla
    determination that generates
    and sustains a determinate field
```

The verse therefore introduces a distinction between:

```text
quality
    akuśala / avyākṛta

productive ground
    akuśalamūla / avyākṛtamūla
```

This is a movement from immediacy into ground:

```text
ethical determination
    → root of ethical determination
    → reproduction of the corresponding field
```

The triads are not symmetrical enumerations. Their members disclose different structures of generation:

```text
akuśala
    attraction
    repulsion
    obscuration

avyākṛta
    craving continuity
    ignorance
    cognitive determination
```

The neutral field therefore has cognition among its roots. Ethical neutrality is not absence of determination; it is a positively generated cognitive-affective structure.

## Organon note

The strongest architectural result is that classification and causation must remain distinct.

```text
hasEthicalStatus
    ≠
functionsAsRootOf
```

A dharma may possess an ethical status without serving as the root of further dharmas of that status.

The verse also prevents the identification of `prajñā` with an intrinsically pure power. Here `mati`, glossed as `prajñā`, functions as an indeterminate root. Discriminative cognition is therefore formally powerful but not intrinsically liberating:

```text
cognitive determination
    can organize bondage
    as well as liberation
```

This agrees with the larger project principle that principle must be determined by its path and complex, not sanctified by vocabulary alone.
