# VAK 5.42

## Sanskrit

एकान्ताकुशलं यस्मात् स्वतन्त्रं चोभयं यतः ।
ईर्ष्यामात्सर्यमेषूक्तं पृथक् संयोजनद्वयम् ॥ ५.४२ ॥

## IAST

ekāntākuśalaṃ yasmāt svatantraṃ cobhayaṃ yataḥ /
īrṣyā-mātsaryam eṣūktaṃ pṛthak saṃyojana-dvayam // 5.42 //

## Padaccheda

ekānta-akuśalam | yasmāt | svatantram | ca | ubhayam | yataḥ | īrṣyā-mātsaryam | eṣu | uktam | pṛthak | saṃyojana-dvayam

## Grammar

- **ekānta-akuśalam** — nominative/accusative singular neuter used predicatively, “exclusively unwholesome,” “unqualifiedly non-wholesome.”
- **yasmāt** — ablative singular of the relative pronoun, “because,” “inasmuch as.”
- **svatantram** — nominative/accusative singular neuter used predicatively, “independent,” “functioning on its own account.”
- **ca** — “and.”
- **ubhayam** — nominative/accusative singular neuter, “both,” referring collectively to jealousy and avarice.
- **yataḥ** — relative adverb, “because,” reinforcing the causal construction.
- **īrṣyā-mātsaryam** — coordinative compound, “jealousy and avarice.”
- **eṣu** — locative plural, “among these,” referring to the fetter-classifications under discussion.
- **uktam** — nominative/accusative singular neuter, past passive participle from √vac, “stated,” “taught.”
- **pṛthak** — indeclinable, “separately,” “as distinct items.”
- **saṃyojana-dvayam** — nominative/accusative singular neuter, “a pair of fetters,” “two fetters.”

## Literal Translation

Because both are exclusively unwholesome and because each is independent, jealousy and avarice are stated separately among these as a pair of fetters.

## Philosophical Translation

Jealousy and avarice are counted as two distinct bonds because neither is merely a neutral distortion or a subordinate moment of another affliction. Each is wholly unwholesome and possesses its own autonomous operation within the afflicted continuum.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.41 explained why several views can be gathered under common fetter-headings:

```text
shared dravya
    +
shared āmarśana
    ↓
collective fetter-class
```

VAK 5.42 presents the inverse principle.

Jealousy and avarice are not collapsed into one genus because each has:

```text
exclusive unwholesomeness
    +
independent operation
    ↓
separate fetter-status
```

The transition is therefore from **common determination** to **autonomous determination**.

The classification does not simply count mental factors. It asks whether a factor can be represented through another or whether it must be retained as an irreducible source of bondage.

| Affliction | Distinct operative direction |
|---|---|
| **īrṣyā** | distress at another's excellence, gain, or success |
| **mātsarya** | contraction around one's own possessions, advantages, or dharmas |

Their intentional directions differ:

```text
īrṣyā
    turns against the good of another

mātsarya
    withholds one's own good from another
```

Thus both concern comparative possession, but they negate relation from opposite sides.

## Technical Vocabulary

### Ekāntākuśala

**Ekānta** gives the sense of exclusivity or absence of qualification. These dharmas are not merely capable of becoming unwholesome under certain conditions; their afflicted operation is unwholesome as such.

This distinguishes them from determinations that may be ethically indeterminate in some domains or that vary according to association.

### Svatantra

Here **svatantra** should not be read as metaphysical self-subsistence. It means operational independence within the taxonomy.

A svatantra affliction:

```text
has its own arising
has its own intentional direction
has its own obstructive effect
cannot be reduced to a mere mode of another listed affliction
```

### Īrṣyā

Jealousy or envy: disturbance at the prosperity, excellence, recognition, or advantage of another.

Its form is comparative negation:

```text
another possesses good
    ↓
my cognition suffers that good as a diminishment of myself
```

### Mātsarya

Avarice, stinginess, or possessive withholding. It contracts around what is taken as one's own and resists its communication or participation.

Its form is appropriative closure:

```text
I possess good
    ↓
relation to another is blocked
    ↓
the good is enclosed as mine
```

### Pṛthak Saṃyojana

A separately counted bond. The emphasis falls on irreducible function rather than numerical convenience.

## Doctrinal Determination

Jealousy and avarice are distinct fetters because they bind the continuum through opposed but complementary operations.

```text
īrṣyā
    negates another's possession

mātsarya
    absolutizes one's own possession
```

Both arise in the field of comparison and appropriation, yet they cannot be identified.

Jealousy says:

```text
what is yours should not be yours
```

Avarice says:

```text
what is mine must not become yours
```

The first attacks the other's good; the second prevents the communication of one's own good.

Hence the bond is social and cognitive at once. The continuum does not merely attach to an object. It organizes a divided field of self and other through possession, rivalry, and withholding.

The verse also supplies a taxonomic rule:

```text
common substance alone
    does not require
common classification

where independent operation is doctrinally decisive
```

## Logical Determination

VAK 5.41 and 5.42 together establish two opposed rules of division.

### Rule of collection

```text
shared bearer
    +
shared mode of grasping
    →
one higher classificatory unity
```

### Rule of separation

```text
distinct intentional direction
    +
independent causal operation
    →
separate determination
```

Thus logical division must preserve both:

```text
identity sufficient for collection
and
difference sufficient for separation
```

The transition can be represented as:

```text
Universal
    common afflicted field

Particular
    comparative appropriation

Singular
    jealousy / avarice as distinct acts
```

But the singular determinations are not merely examples of a genus. Each actualizes a different negative relation.

```text
īrṣyā(x, y, good)
    = x suffers y's possession of good

mātsarya(x, good, y)
    = x blocks y's participation in x's good
```

The argument therefore turns on **directionality**. The same relational field produces distinct bonds according to the direction of negation.

## Transition from VAK 5.41

The two verses should be read as a single meditation on classification.

```text
VAK 5.41
    plurality can be gathered
    when substance and grasping are common

VAK 5.42
    apparent similarity must be divided
    when each operation is autonomous
```

This is the emergence of determinate conflict within the afflicted field.

The affliction is no longer treated merely as a latent root or a general stream-power. It now differentiates itself into concrete relational oppositions.

```text
common afflictive ground
    ↓
comparative self/other field
    ↓
opposed autonomous bonds
```

## Organon Note

For the Platform, VAK 5.42 requires that classification support both grouping and non-reducibility.

A broad class such as:

```text
PossessiveConflict
```

may contain:

```text
Jealousy
Avarice
```

but no rule should infer that the two are functionally interchangeable.

Their relation signatures differ:

```text
Jealousy
    evaluates another's good negatively

Avarice
    restricts transfer of one's own good
```

This suggests two distinct conflict operators:

```text
negatesOtherAdvantage
withholdsOwnedAdvantage
```

The deeper architectural point is that conflict is not primitive chaos. It emerges through determinate directional relations within one shared field.

This bears upon the distinction between Pure Prajñā and the dharmas it discriminates. The discriminatory principle need not itself enter into conflict with either determination. It distinguishes the opposed operations precisely by not being reducible to either operation.

That observation belongs to our later synthetic comment, but VAK 5.42 supplies its kārikā-level ground:

```text
conflict belongs to the discriminated determinations
not necessarily to the act of pure discrimination
```

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. Does Vasubandhu explain **ekāntākuśala** primarily through their ethical status in the desire domain?
2. Does **svatantra** mean that jealousy and avarice arise independently, or that they are not merely included under another fetter?
3. Which earlier list of fetters is presupposed by **eṣu**?
4. Does the Bhāṣya explicitly contrast these two with the grouped views of VAK 5.41?
5. Is the distinction between another's gain and one's own withholding made explicit in the commentary?
6. Are jealousy and avarice separate because of intentional object, causal function, associated mental factors, or all three?
7. How does this classification relate to their later treatment among the paryavasthānas and upakleśas?
