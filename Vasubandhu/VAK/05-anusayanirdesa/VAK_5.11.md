# VAK_5.11 — What Does Not Arise in the Noble One

## 1. Sanskrit — Devanāgarī

```sanskrit
विभवेच्छा न चार्यस्य सम्भवन्ति विधादयः ।
नास्मिता दृष्टिपुष्टत्वात् कौकृत्यं नापि चाशुभम् ॥ ५.११ ॥
```

## 2. Sanskrit — IAST

```text
vibhavecchā na cāryasya saṃbhavanti vidhādayaḥ |
nāsmitā dṛṣṭipuṣṭatvāt kaukṛtyaṃ nāpi cāśubham || 5.11 ||
```

The local source gives:

```text
vibhavecchā na cāryasya saṃbhavanti vidhādayaḥ /
nāsmitā dṛṣṭipuṣṭatvāt kaukṛtyaṃ nāpi cāśubham // VAkK_5.11 //
```

The verse continues the discussion of conceit from 5.10, but now asks which apparently related determinations can or cannot occur in an `ārya`.

A cautious syntactical reconstruction is:

```text

    vibhava-icchā na [saṃbhavati],
    vidhā-ādayaḥ ca na saṃbhavanti,

```

This reconstruction is provisional. The exact scope of the negatives and the final clause must be checked against the Bhāṣya.

---

## 4. Grammar

| Term | Form | Working meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `vibhava-icchā` | nominative feminine singular compound | desire for non-existence, desire for cessation of one's existence |
| `āryasya` | genitive masculine singular | of or in the noble one |
| `saṃbhavanti` | 3rd person plural present | arise, occur, are possible |
| `vidhā-ādayaḥ` | nominative masculine plural | the modes beginning with `vidhā`; likely the comparative forms of conceit just enumerated |
| `asmitā` | nominative feminine singular | the determination “I am,” I-am-ness |
| `dṛṣṭi-puṣṭatvāt` | ablative singular abstract compound | because of being nourished or strengthened by view |
| `kaukṛtyam` | nominative/accusative neuter singular | remorse, regret, troubled reflection upon what was done |
| `aśubham` | nominative/accusative neuter singular | unwholesome |

The kārikā is highly compressed. In particular:

```text
vidhādayaḥ
```

must be read in relation to the ninefold conceit-analysis of 5.10, while:

```text
kaukṛtyaṃ nāpi cāśubham
```

admits more than one possible syntactical attachment without the Bhāṣya.

---

## 5. Literal translation

> In the noble one there is no desire for non-existence, and the modes beginning with comparative conceit do not arise. Nor does I-am-ness, because it is nourished by view. Remorse, moreover, is not unwholesome.

This translation follows the most direct segmentation but remains provisional at the final clause.

---

## 6. Philosophical translation

> In one who has entered the noble path, the wish for sheer non-being does not arise as a valid form of release. Nor do the determinate comparative constructions of conceit continue in the same way, and the view-supported assertion of “I am” is no longer sustained once its cognitive basis has been broken. Even regret must be distinguished according to its function: not every reflective disturbance concerning action is simply unwholesome.

The verse therefore introduces an important distinction:

```text
removal of false view
    does not mean
removal of every reflexive or affective determination
```

---

# 7. `Vibhava-icchā`

```text
vibhava
    non-existence
    cessation of becoming
    annihilation

icchā
    wish
    desire
```

`Vibhava-icchā` is not liberation merely because it rejects continued existence.

```text
attachment to existence
    bhava-rāga

recoil into non-existence
    vibhava-icchā
```

These are opposed in content but may share the same underlying error:

```text
appropriated existence
    is taken as something to preserve
or
appropriated existence
    is taken as something to destroy
```

The `ārya` does not substitute annihilation for bondage.

```text
mokṣa
    ≠
self-annihilation
```

This is a strongly dialectical determination. The negation of conditioned existence must itself be purified of the standpoint that still presupposes an entity whose non-existence is desired.

---

# 8. `Vidhādayaḥ`

The expression likely points back to the ninefold forms of conceit in 5.10.

```text
superior
 equal
 inferior
```

combined with judgments such as:

```text
I am superior
I am equal
I am inferior
```

These are not neutral comparisons. They are reflexive determinations organized around appropriation of a personal standpoint.

```text
self-position
    measured against another
        → conceit
```

The noble cognition breaks the false ground upon which these comparative identities are absolutized.

But the verse should not yet be made to claim that every practical comparison disappears. What is removed is the afflicted mode of self-measurement.

```text
functional discrimination
    may remain

afflicted self-ranking
    does not
```

The Bhāṣya must identify the exact range of `vidhādayaḥ`.

---

# 9. `Asmitā`

```text
asmi
    I am

-tā
    abstract state

asmitā
    I-am-ness
```

Here `asmitā` is not bare grammatical self-reference.

It is the reflexive determination:

```text
there is an independently established “I”
```

The verse connects it with:

```text
dṛṣṭi-puṣṭatva
    being nourished by view
```

Thus `asmitā` is not isolated. It is sustained by a prior cognitive determination.

```text
view
    supplies the ground

I-am-ness
    receives persistence from that ground
```

When the false view is destroyed through seeing, the derived self-assertion loses its support.

This continues the positive dialectic of the preceding verses:

```text
false view removed
    → supporting ground removed
    → dependent conceit can no longer arise in the same form
```

---

# 10. `Dṛṣṭi-puṣṭatva`

```text
dṛṣṭi
    view

puṣṭa
    nourished
    strengthened
    sustained

-tva
    state or condition
```

The compound gives a genetic relation:

```text
asmitā
    is not self-grounding

it is nourished by
    dṛṣṭi
```

This is philosophically important.

The afflicted self is not presented as a primitive substance. It is an effect continually sustained by false seeing.

```text
false seeing
    → self-positing
    → comparative conceit
    → desire concerning being or non-being
```

The chapter is therefore reconstructing a system, not listing disconnected faults.

---

# 11. `Kaukṛtya`

```text
kaukṛtya
    remorse
    regret
    troubled reflection on what has been done
```

The closing statement appears to distinguish remorse from what is simply unwholesome:

```text
kaukṛtyaṃ nāpi cāśubham
```

A possible first-pass determination is:

```text
regret is not univocally unwholesome
```

Why?

Because reflective dissatisfaction with a prior action can have different functions:

```text
afflicted brooding
    reproduces disturbance

ethical recognition
    interrupts repetition
```

The verse may therefore be preventing a crude classification in which every painful reflexive state is automatically treated as morally corrupt.

However, this is precisely where the Bhāṣya is required. It must establish:

```text
whether the clause concerns the ārya specifically
whether all kaukṛtya or only a determinate form is intended
how its ethical class is defined
```

---

# 12. The noble one as transformed possibility

The verse uses the language of occurrence:

```text
saṃbhavanti
    arise
    occur
    are possible
```

This means that becoming an `ārya` is not merely acquiring a new opinion.

It alters the field of possible afflictive determinations.

```text
before noble seeing
    false view can nourish self-assertion
    self-assertion can generate comparison
    appropriation can generate desire for being or non-being

after noble seeing
    the ground of those determinations has been broken
```

The transformation is structural:

```text
change of seeing
    → change of what can arise
```

This confirms the priority of `darśana` encountered in the preceding verses.

---

# 13. Positive dialectic

The verse does not say merely:

```text
remove desire
remove conceit
remove self-reference
remove regret
```

Instead it differentiates:

```text
what cannot arise after noble seeing
what depended upon false view
what may remain under another ethical determination
```

This is positive dialectic because negation is determinate.

```text
false ground negated
    → dependent forms lose possibility

but

reflection as such
    is not indiscriminately destroyed
```

The resulting freedom is not blankness. It is a reorganization of the field of possible cognition and affect.

---

# 14. Kārikā–Bhāṣya tension

The kārikā independently establishes four points:

```text
1. vibhava-icchā is denied of the ārya
2. the modes beginning with comparative conceit do not arise
3. asmitā depends upon nourishment by view
4. kaukṛtya cannot be classified crudely as simply unwholesome
```

But it leaves major questions compressed:

```text
What exactly are the vidhādayaḥ?

Does dṛṣṭipuṣṭatvāt govern only asmitā?

What kind of kaukṛtya is not unwholesome?

Does the final clause still have āryasya as its governing context?
```

These are not defects in the first-pass study. They identify the exact doctrinal work demanded from the Bhāṣya.

---

# 15. Logical reconstruction

```text
false view
    nourishes
        asmitā

asmitā
    organizes
        comparative conceit

appropriated self
    desires
        continued being
        or non-being

noble seeing
    destroys the false ground
        therefore
    these derivative formations no longer arise
```

But:

```text
remorse or reflective concern
    must be classified by function
    not by surface affect alone
```

---

# 16. Restrained Organon interpretation

The verse suggests a dependency graph rather than a flat list of errors:

```text
View
    → supports Self-Determination
        → supports Comparative Evaluation
            → supports Existential Preference
```

A scientific correction therefore proceeds at the root:

```text
correct the governing view
    rather than
suppress each downstream state independently
```

This remains a restrained architectural analogy. The primary result belongs to the Kośa's analysis of the transformed continuum of the `ārya`.

# 18. Final determination

```text
noble seeing
    does not merely add knowledge

it removes the ground
    from which afflicted self-determination arises
```

The verse's most important movement is:

```text
dṛṣṭi
    → nourishes asmitā
    → supports comparative conceit
    → supports desire concerning being and non-being

ārya-darśana
    → breaks that dependency chain
```

Yet the final mention of `kaukṛtya` warns us against treating liberation as the indiscriminate elimination of every negative or reflective state.

The Kośa is determining which formations are structurally impossible after seeing, which are dependent upon false view, and which require a more exact ethical classification.
