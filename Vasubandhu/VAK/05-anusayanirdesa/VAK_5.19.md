# VAK 5.19

## Sanskrit

ऊर्ध्वमव्याकृताः सर्वे कामे सत्कायदर्शनम् ।
अन्तग्राहः सहाभ्यां च मोहः शेषास्त्विहाशुभाः ॥ ५.१९ ॥

## IAST

## Grammar

- `ūrdhvam` — adverbial accusative: above, in the higher domains; here, above the desire-domain.
- `avyākṛtāḥ` — nominative masculine plural: indeterminate, ethically neutral.
- `sarve` — nominative masculine plural: all; referring to the anuśayas present in the higher domains.
- `kāme` — locative singular: in the desire-domain.
- `satkāyadarśanam` — nominative neuter singular: the view of a real collection or existing body.
- `antagrāhaḥ` — nominative masculine singular: grasping an extreme; the extreme-view.
- `sahābhyām` — instrumental dual: together with those two.
- `mohaḥ` — nominative masculine singular: delusion; here the ignorance conjoined with the two views.
- `śeṣāḥ` — nominative masculine plural: the remaining anuśayas.
- `tu` — adversative particle: but.
- `iha` — here; in this domain, namely the desire-domain.
- `aśubhāḥ` — nominative masculine plural: unwholesome.

## Literal translation

Above, all are indeterminate. In the desire-domain, the view of a real collection, grasping an extreme, and the delusion together with those two are indeterminate; but the remaining ones here are unwholesome.

## Philosophical translation

All anuśayas belonging to the higher domains are ethically indeterminate. Within the desire-domain, however, only satkāyadṛṣṭi, antagrāhadṛṣṭi, and the ignorance conjoined with these two are indeterminate; the remaining anuśayas of the desire-domain are unwholesome.

## Analysis

Verses 5.17–5.18 determined the two modes by which an anuśaya adheres:

```text
through its object
through conjunction
```

Verse 5.19 now changes the axis of classification. The question is no longer how an anuśaya adheres, but what ethical determination it possesses in each domain.

The primary division is:

```text
desire-domain
    mixed ethical determination

higher domains
    all anuśayas indeterminate
```

`ūrdhvam` refers to the form-domain and formless-domain. All anuśayas there are `avyākṛta`: they are afflicted, but not unwholesome in the strict karmic sense.

This distinction is important:

```text
kliṣṭa
    does not necessarily mean
akuśala
```

An anuśaya may obscure, bind, and sustain saṃsāra without producing the specifically unwholesome determination attributed to desire-domain affliction.

Within the desire-domain, three cases are separated from the rest:

```text
satkāyadarśana
antagrāhadṛṣṭi
moha conjoined with those two
```

These are indeterminate rather than unwholesome. The remaining desire-domain anuśayas are `aśubha`, that is, unwholesome.

The phrase `sahābhyāṃ ca mohaḥ` is restrictive. It does not classify all ignorance in the desire-domain as indeterminate. It identifies only the ignorance conjoined with the two views:

```text
satkāyadarśana + associated moha
antagrāhadṛṣṭi + associated moha
```

The verse therefore distinguishes ignorance according to conjunction. Its ethical determination depends upon the complex in which it occurs.

## Technical vocabulary

```text

    the higher domains:
    rūpadhātu and ārūpyadhātu

    ethically indeterminate
    neither kuśala nor akuśala

    the desire-domain

satkāyadarśana
    view of a real collection
    taking the conditioned aggregate-complex as self or belonging to self

antagrāha
    grasping an extreme
    especially eternalist or annihilationist determination

moha
    delusion or ignorance
    here specifically ignorance conjoined with the two views

    unwholesome
    equivalent here to akuśala
```

## Doctrinal determination

The verse establishes that the ethical quality of an affliction is domain-dependent.

```text
higher-domain anuśaya
    = kliṣṭa
    = avyākṛta
    ≠ akuśala

desire-domain anuśaya
    = partly avyākṛta
    = otherwise aśubha
```

The higher-domain afflictions remain causes of bondage, but their refined and meditative context prevents them from being classified as unwholesome in the same way as desire-domain afflictions.

The two views named here are treated as indeterminate because they function primarily as cognitive distortions concerning selfhood and persistence, rather than as immediately injurious volitional states. The ignorance conjoined with them receives the same determination through association.

## Logical determination

The verse introduces a distinction between two orders of determination:

```text
formal contamination
    kliṣṭa / akliṣṭa

ethical efficacy
    kuśala / akuśala / avyākṛta
```

These orders cannot be collapsed.

An anuśaya may be formally contaminated while remaining ethically indeterminate:

```text
kliṣṭa
    without
akuśala
```

The determining relation is twofold:

```text
domain
    determines the general ethical range

conjunction
    determines the status of a particular mental factor
```

Thus the verse applies the conjunction-relation established in 5.18 to ethical classification. `Moha` is not assigned a fixed value in abstraction from its complex; the associated whole determines its status.

The movement is:

```text
anuśaya as adhering determination
    → anuśaya as domain-conditioned determination
    → anuśaya as ethically specified determination
```

## Organon note

Verse 5.19 prevents the identification of defect with evil.

```text
defect of knowing
    is broader than
unwholesome willing
```

Satkāyadṛṣṭi and antagrāhadṛṣṭi are false determinations of the object. They constitute cognitive bondage, but are not therefore immediately identical with an unwholesome practical act.

The higher-domain anuśayas make the same point at a broader level. Refinement of the field does not yet amount to liberation:

```text
ethical refinement
    ≠ freedom from contamination
```

A meditative domain may exclude coarse unwholesomeness while retaining the deeper structure of appropriation, ignorance, and becoming.

For the Organon architecture, this gives two independent properties of a determination:

```text
contamination-status
ethical-status
```

These should not be modeled as a single binary classification.

## Bhāṣya questions

1. Why precisely are all higher-domain anuśayas classified as `avyākṛta` rather than `akuśala`?
2. What reason does the Bhāṣya give for treating satkāyadṛṣṭi and antagrāhadṛṣṭi as indeterminate in the desire-domain?
3. Does `satkāyadarśanam` here function strictly as a synonym of `satkāyadṛṣṭi`?
4. Is `sahābhyāṃ mohaḥ` limited to ignorance directly conjoined with the two views, or does it include a broader associated complex?
5. Does the Bhāṣya explicitly distinguish `aśubha` from `akuśala`, or use them equivalently here?
