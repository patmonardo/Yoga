# VAK 5.22

## Sanskrit

## Grammar

- `ekāṃśataḥ` — indeclinable adverb: from one side, in one respect, categorically, without qualification.
- `vyākaraṇam` — nominative or accusative neuter singular from `vyākaraṇa`: an answering, explanation, determination, or exposition.
- `vibhajya` — absolutive from `vi-bhaj`: having divided or distinguished; after making the required qualification.
- `paripṛcchya` — absolutive from `pari-prach`: having questioned in return; after a counter-question.
- `sthāpyam` — gerundive from `sthā`: to be set aside, left undetermined, or refused as wrongly framed.
- `maraṇa` — death; example of a question admitting a categorical answer.
- `utpatti` — arising or rebirth; example of a question requiring division and qualification.
- `viśiṣṭa` — superior, distinguished, or qualified; example of a question requiring a counter-question concerning the intended comparison.
- `ātma-anyatā` — the otherness or difference of a self; shorthand for a speculative question concerning whether the self is identical with or different from the aggregates or body.
- `ādi-vat` — as in these and similar cases.

## Literal translation

An answer is [given] categorically, after division, and after questioning in return; and [a question] is to be set aside—as in questions concerning death, arising, superiority, and the otherness of the self, respectively.

## Philosophical translation

Questions do not all possess the same logical form. One must sometimes answer unconditionally, sometimes answer only after introducing a distinction, sometimes expose an indeterminate comparison through a counter-question, and sometimes suspend the question because its own presupposition is defective. Death, rebirth, comparative superiority, and the alleged identity or difference of a self exemplify these four modes.

## Analysis

Verse 5.22 abruptly changes register. Verses 5.19–5.21 classified anuśayas and their roots. Verse 5.22 now classifies the forms of responsible answer.

The four modes are:

```text
1. ekāṃśato vyākaraṇa
   categorical answer

2. vibhajya vyākaraṇa
   discriminative or qualified answer

3. paripṛcchya vyākaraṇa
   answer through counter-question

4. sthāpanīya
   question to be set aside
```

This is not merely a list of conversational techniques. It is a discipline for matching the form of cognition to the form of the object and question.

### 1. Categorical answer

A question receives a categorical answer when the determination holds without a relevant qualification.

The example is death:

```text
Are all conditioned living beings subject to death?
    Yes.
```

The answer does not need the question to be divided into cases.

### 2. Answer after division

A question requires division when its apparent unity conceals distinct conditions.

The example is arising or rebirth:

```text
Does one who dies arise again?
```

The responsible answer distinguishes cases:

```text
with the relevant conditions of renewed existence
    arising follows

with those conditions exhausted
    arising does not follow
```

The truth is not captured by an unqualified yes or no. It emerges only after the field has been discriminated.

### 3. Counter-question

A question requires a counter-question when a comparative or relational predicate lacks its determining reference.

The example is `viśiṣṭa`, superiority or distinction:

```text
Is this person superior?
```

The proper response is:

```text
Superior to whom?
In which respect?
```

The counter-question does not evade the original question. It supplies the missing relation without which the predicate is indeterminate.

### 4. Setting aside

A question is set aside when answering it directly would validate a false presupposition.

The example concerns the self:

```text
Is the self identical with the aggregates,
or different from them?
```

The Buddhist analysis does not accept a substantially existing self that could enter either relation. Therefore neither alternative is properly affirmed.

```text
not yes
not no
not a division into cases

but suspension of the defective question
```

This is a logical act, not a refusal to think.

## Doctrinal determination

The verse establishes that correct speech depends upon correct discrimination.

```text
one question
    does not imply
one universal answer-form
```

The mode of answer is governed by the structure of the matter:

```text
unconditional determination
    → categorical answer

conditional determination
    → divided answer

relational indeterminacy
    → counter-question

false presupposition
    → setting aside
```

Thus right teaching is not the mechanical delivery of doctrine. It is the capacity to determine what kind of cognition the question itself permits.

## Logical determination

Verse 5.22 presents a compact logic of interrogative judgment.

```text
question
    must itself be judged
before its content can be answered
```

The discriminating mind first determines the logical status of the question:

```text
Is its predicate unconditional?
Are relevant cases conflated?
Is a comparison missing its correlate?
Does the question posit an inadmissible subject?
```

Only then does an answer become possible.

This yields four reflective operations:

```text
assert
    where determination is complete

distinguish
    where conditions divide the field

return the question
    where a relation remains unspecified

suspend
    where the form of the question is false
```

The fourth is especially important. `sthāpya` is not ignorance and not indecision. It is cognition recognizing that the offered alternatives fail to correspond to the real structure.

```text
indeterminacy in the knower
    ≠
invalid determination in the question
```

## Organon note

This verse belongs directly to the architecture of a knowing system.

A system should not be forced to map every prompt immediately onto `true` or `false`. Before answering, it should classify the question-form.

```text
AnswerMode
    Categorical
    Conditional
    CounterQuestion
    SetAside
```

The last mode is crucial for harvesting `DoesNotKnow` events accurately. Some failures arise from missing evidence; others arise because the request itself is malformed.

```text
MissingEvidence
    more perception is required

MissingDistinction
    the field must be divided

MissingRelation
    a counter-question is required

FalsePresupposition
    the question must be set aside
```

Thus the knowing Perceptron does not merely answer. It judges the admissible form of answering.
