# VAK 5.55

## Sanskrit

सुखाभ्यां संप्रयुक्तो हि रागो द्वेषो विपर्ययात् ।
मोहः सर्वैरसद्दृष्टिर्मनोदुःखसुखेन तु ॥ ५.५५ ॥

## IAST

sukhābhyāṃ saṃprayukto hi rāgaḥ dveṣo viparyayāt /
mohaḥ sarvair asaddṛṣṭir mano-duḥkha-sukhena tu // 5.55 //

## Padaccheda

sukha-ābhyām | saṃprayuktaḥ | hi | rāgaḥ | dveṣaḥ | viparyayāt | mohaḥ | sarvaiḥ | asat-dṛṣṭiḥ | manaḥ-duḥkha-sukhena | tu

## Grammar

- **sukhābhyām** — instrumental dual neuter, “with the two pleasant feelings.” The Bhāṣya identifies these as **sukha** and **saumanasya**: bodily pleasure and mental gladness.
- **saṃprayuktaḥ** — nominative singular masculine, past passive participle from **sam-pra-√yuj**, “associated,” “conjoined.” It indicates simultaneous conjunction within one cognitive event.
- **hi** — explanatory particle, “indeed,” “for.”
- **rāgaḥ** — nominative singular masculine, “attachment,” “appropriative attraction.”
- **dveṣaḥ** — nominative singular masculine, “hostility,” “aversive rejection.”
- **viparyayāt** — ablative singular, “from the reverse,” “by opposition.” Hostility is associated with the two painful feelings corresponding inversely to the two pleasant feelings.
- **mohaḥ** — nominative singular masculine, “delusion,” “obscuration.”
- **sarvaiḥ** — instrumental plural, “with all,” referring to all five feeling-faculties.
- **asaddṛṣṭiḥ** — nominative singular feminine, “false view,” especially **mithyādṛṣṭi**, a view that negates what is real or normatively effective.
- **mano-duḥkha-sukhena** — instrumental compound, collectively indicating mental pain and mental pleasure: **daurmanasya** and **saumanasya**.
- **tu** — contrastive particle, “but,” marking the more restricted association of false view.

## Literal Translation

Attachment is indeed associated with the two pleasant feelings; hostility, by reversal, with the two painful feelings. Delusion is associated with all; false view, however, with mental pain and mental pleasure.

## Philosophical Translation

Appropriative attraction arises together with bodily pleasure and mental gladness, while hostility arises with their painful opposites. Obscuration can pervade every affective mode. False view, however, belongs specifically to the reflective mental field and therefore combines with either distress or gladness according to the position it affirms or negates.

## Structural Analysis

VAK 5.54 classified afflictions according to their cognitive support:

```text
manovijñāna only
    versus
all six knowledges
```

VAK 5.55 adds a new coordinate:

```text
cognitive support
    +
affective conjunction
```

The question is no longer only:

```text
Where can an affliction arise?
```

It is now:

```text
With what mode of feeling
can that affliction arise?
```

The five feeling-faculties are:

```text
sukha          bodily pleasure
saumanasya     mental gladness
duḥkha         bodily pain
daurmanasya    mental distress
upekṣā         equanimous or neutral feeling
```

VAK 5.55 begins their distribution:

```text
rāga
    → sukha + saumanasya

dveṣa
    → duḥkha + daurmanasya

moha
    → all five

asaddṛṣṭi
    → saumanasya + daurmanasya
```

VAK 5.56 will complete the distribution by adding doubt, the remaining afflictions, equanimity, and the higher domains.

## Technical Vocabulary

### Saṃprayoga

**Saṃprayoga** is conjunction within one cognitive event.

It does not mean that one state externally causes another after a temporal interval. The affliction and the feeling arise together as mutually compatible determinations of one event.

```text
one cognitive occurrence
    contains
object-support
knowledge
feeling
afflictive orientation
```

Thus the relation mapped in this verse is synchronic:

```text
affliction
    co-arises with
feeling
```

### Sukha and Saumanasya

The dual **sukhābhyām** includes two agreeable feeling-faculties:

```text
sukha
    bodily pleasure

saumanasya
    mental gladness
```

Attachment can accompany both because it operates through affirmative appropriation:

```text
this is agreeable
    ↓
let it remain
    ↓
let it be mine
```

### Duḥkha and Daurmanasya

By **viparyaya**, hostility accompanies:

```text
duḥkha
    bodily pain

daurmanasya
    mental distress
```

Its movement is aversive:

```text
this is disagreeable
    ↓
let it cease
    ↓
let it be excluded
```

### Moha

**Moha** is not one affective tone among others. It is obscuration capable of accompanying every affective tone.

```text
pleasure may be misunderstood
pain may be misunderstood
gladness may be misunderstood
distress may be misunderstood
neutrality may be misunderstood
```

Therefore:

```text
moha
    is affectively universal
```

### Asaddṛṣṭi

**Asaddṛṣṭi** is a false or normatively defective view. Unlike rāga and dveṣa, it belongs to reflective mental knowledge rather than bodily sensory knowledge.

It can be accompanied by:

```text
saumanasya
    glad affirmation of a false position

daurmanasya
    distressed negation or nihilistic rejection
```

The view may therefore be affectively affirmative or negative while remaining cognitively false.

## Doctrinal Determination

The verse does not reduce affliction to feeling.

```text
rāga
    is not pleasure

dveṣa
    is not pain

moha
    is not neutrality
```

Feeling gives the affective tone of the event. Affliction gives its distorted orientation.

Thus:

```text
vedanā
    registers agreeability,
disagreeability,
or neutrality

kleśa
    appropriates,
rejects,
or obscures
```

The two must therefore be modeled as distinct but conjoined determinations.

### Attachment and affirmative feeling

Attachment is compatible with both bodily and mental pleasure because its defining operation is affirmative retention.

```text
pleasant feeling
    provides the agreeable tone

rāga
    converts agreeability into appropriation
```

### Hostility and negative feeling

Hostility is compatible with bodily and mental pain because its defining operation is rejection.

```text
painful feeling
    provides the disagreeable tone

dveṣa
    converts disagreeability into exclusion
```

### Delusion and all feeling

Delusion is universal because any affective event can be misconstrued.

This is doctrinally important for the Path. One cannot infer freedom from delusion merely from the presence of pleasant, painful, or neutral experience.

```text
affective elevation
    ≠
knowledge
```

Even equanimity can remain conjoined with obscuration.

### False view and mental feeling

False view requires conceptual or judgmental articulation. It therefore accompanies mental gladness or distress, not bodily pleasure or pain as such.

The false view may be enjoyed:

```text
I am pleased by this doctrine
```

or suffered:

```text
I am distressed by the reality I deny
```

In both cases, its falsity belongs to the view, not to the feeling.

## Logical Determination

VAK 5.55 introduces a compatibility relation.

```text
not every affliction
can combine with
every feeling
```

The structure is:

```text
AfflictionType × FeelingType
    → permitted conjunctions
```

The verse therefore gives a constraint matrix rather than a causal sequence.

```text
rāga         {sukha, saumanasya}
dveṣa        {duḥkha, daurmanasya}
moha         {all five}
asaddṛṣṭi    {saumanasya, daurmanasya}
```

This is a logic of determinate compatibility.

### Polarity

Rāga and dveṣa form an opposed pair:

```text
affirmative appropriation
    ↔
aversive exclusion
```

Their feelings are correspondingly opposed:

```text
pleasant
    ↔
painful
```

But moha is not a third feeling-polarity. It is the obscuration that can inhabit either pole and their neutral middle.

```text
positive tone
negative tone
neutral tone
    ↓
all may be obscured
```

### Reflective restriction

False view is restricted to mental feeling because judgment requires reflective synthesis.

```text
bodily feeling
    gives immediacy

mental feeling
    can accompany a position
```

Thus the verse connects the ontology of feeling with the logic of judgment.

## Path Determination

This classification reveals why practical observation must be more exact than the maxim “avoid pain and seek pleasure.”

Pleasure may support attachment. Pain may support hostility. Both may support delusion.

```text
pleasant
    does not entail wholesome

painful
    does not entail unwholesome

neutral
    does not entail clear
```

The Path must therefore discriminate two layers in every experience:

```text
What is the feeling-tone?

What orientation has arisen with it?
```

This gives a practical marker system:

```text
pleasant + retaining
    → inspect rāga

painful + rejecting
    → inspect dveṣa

any feeling + non-discrimination
    → inspect moha

mental pleasure or distress + fixed assertion
    → inspect dṛṣṭi
```

The Path “appears in the middle” because discrimination operates within already conditioned experience. Yet its purpose is to trace the manifest conjunction back to its origin:

```text
felt event
    ↓
afflictive orientation
    ↓
root condition
    ↓
mode of abandonment
```

## Organon Note

VAK 5.55 requires the Platform to distinguish state content from state valuation.

```text
Feeling
    = affective tone

Affliction
    = relational orientation
```

A single feeling instance may support different orientations:

```text
saumanasya
    + non-attachment

saumanasya
    + rāga

saumanasya
    + false view

saumanasya
    + moha
```

Therefore no inference should run directly from feeling to ethical or cognitive status.

Suggested model:

```text
CognitiveEvent
    hasFeeling
    hasAfflictiveOrientation
    hasKnowledgeSupport
    hasObjectSupport
    occursInDomain
```

Compatibility should be expressed as a constraint rather than identity:

```text
Rāga
    mayCoOccurWith
Sukha
Saumanasya
```

The system must also preserve multiple conjunctions:

```text
one event
    may contain
saumanasya + rāga + moha
```

because delusion can accompany another affliction and its feeling.

## Bhāṣya Questions

1. Does **sukhābhyām** here refer exclusively to **sukha** and **saumanasya** in the desire-domain?
2. Does **viparyayāt** establish a strict polarity between rāga and dveṣa, or merely the reverse pair of associated feelings?
3. Why can moha accompany all five feelings? Is the explanation solely its conjunction with every root affliction?
4. Does **asaddṛṣṭi** refer specifically to **mithyādṛṣṭi**, or more broadly to defective views?
5. Why is false view associated with both mental gladness and mental distress?
6. Can false view occur with equanimity, or is that possibility supplied only by the universal rule in VAK 5.56?
7. How does the Bhāṣya distinguish feeling as **saṃprayukta** from feeling as a causal condition for affliction?
8. Are the associations in this verse restricted to desire-domain afflictions, with higher-domain cases deferred entirely to VAK 5.56?
