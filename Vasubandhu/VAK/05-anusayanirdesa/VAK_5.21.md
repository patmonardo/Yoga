# VAK 5.21

## Sanskrit

द्वैधोर्ध्ववृत्तेर्नातोऽन्यौ चत्वार्येवेति बाह्यकाः ।
तृष्णादृङ्मानमोहास्ते ध्यायितृत्वादविद्यया ॥ ५.२१ ॥

## IAST

dvaidhordhvavṛtter nāto 'nyau catvāry eveti bāhyakāḥ |
tṛṣṇā-dṛṅ-māna-mohās te dhyāyitritvād avidyayā || 5.21 ||

## Source caution

The kārikā-only source gives `dvidhordhvavṛtter`, while the Bhāṣya transmits and explains `dvaidhordhvavṛtteḥ`:

```text
dvaidha-vṛtti
    twofold movement
    said of vicikitsā

ūrdhva-vṛtti
    upward movement
    said of māna
```

The Bhāṣya's wording directly governs the explanation of the first pāda, so `dvaidhordhvavṛtteḥ` is adopted here as the coherent reading. The kārikā-only variant is retained as a source note for the later textual pass.

## Grammar

- `dvaidha-vṛtteḥ` — ablative singular: because of twofold or divided movement; applied to `vicikitsā`, whose wavering prevents it from being a stable root.
- `ūrdhva-vṛtteḥ` — ablative singular: because of upward movement; applied to `māna`, whose defining movement is elevation.
- `na ataḥ anyau` — therefore not the other two; namely doubt and pride are excluded from the Kāśmīra list of three neutral roots.
- `catvāri eva` — four only.
- `iti bāhyakāḥ` — thus the outsiders or non-Kāśmīra authorities maintain.
- `tṛṣṇā-dṛṅ-māna-mohāḥ` — craving, view, pride, and delusion; nominative plural compound enumerating the four neutral roots accepted by the alternative school.
- `te` — those; referring to the four just named.
- `dhyāyitṛ-tvāt` — because of the existence or threefold character of meditators.
- `avidyayā` — through or under the power of ignorance.

## Literal translation

Because of divided movement and upward movement, the other two are therefore not roots. The outsiders say that there are only four: craving, view, pride, and delusion. They are so because meditators are threefold through ignorance.

## Philosophical translation

On the Kāśmīra account, doubt and pride are excluded from the roots of the indeterminate: doubt is unstable through its divided movement, and pride turns upward rather than downward into grounding. Other authorities, however, recognize four neutral roots—craving, view, pride, and ignorance—because meditators become threefold according to craving, view, and pride, all under the governing power of ignorance.

## Analysis

Verse 5.20 introduced three roots of the ethically indeterminate:

```text
tṛṣṇā
avidyā
mati / prajñā
```

Verse 5.21 first explains why two other anuśayas are not added to that list:

```text
vicikitsā
    dvaidha-vṛtti
    moves in two directions
    unstable and wavering

māna
    ūrdhva-vṛtti
    moves upward through elevation
```

The image governing the argument is that a root must be:

```text
stable
and
downward-turning
```

A root fixes and supports a field. Doubt cannot do this because it alternates between alternatives. Pride cannot do this because its movement is elevation rather than grounding.

The second half then records an alternative classification attributed to the `bāhyakāḥ`:

```text
four indeterminate roots
    tṛṣṇā
    dṛṣṭi
    māna
    moha / avidyā
```

Their evidence is practical rather than merely taxonomic. There are three types of meditator whose higher meditation is driven respectively by:

```text
craving
view
pride
```

All three, however, operate through ignorance:

```text
tṛṣṇā-driven meditator
    under avidyā

dṛṣṭi-driven meditator
    under avidyā

māna-driven meditator
    under avidyā
```

Thus ignorance is not simply a fourth item beside the other three. It is also the common condition through which the three modes of distorted meditation arise.

## Doctrinal determination

The verse presents two rival criteria for roothood.

The Kāśmīra criterion is structural:

```text
root
    must be stable
    must turn downward
    must support reproduction
```

Accordingly:

```text
vicikitsā
    excluded because unstable

māna
    excluded because upward-turning
```

The alternative criterion is genetic and experiential:

```text
what generates a recurring type of meditator
    counts as a root
```

Hence craving, view, and pride are counted because each can organize a form of higher meditation, while ignorance conditions all three.

The disagreement is therefore not simply about counting:

```text
three neutral roots
    ontological or structural criterion

four neutral roots
    genetic or practitioner-based criterion
```

## Logical determination

Verse 5.21 makes explicit that a root is not merely an antecedent cause. It has a determinate form of movement.

```text
root-form
    stability
    descent
    support
```

This produces a polarity:

```text
doubt
    lateral division

pride
    upward elevation

root
    downward fixation
```

The Kāśmīra argument therefore defines ground spatially and dynamically:

```text
what grounds
    does not oscillate
    does not elevate itself
    but settles beneath and sustains
```

The alternative position shifts from the form of ground to its manifested products:

```text
structural root
    inferred from mode of movement

productive root
    inferred from generated practitioner-type
```

This is an important methodological difference. One school determines roothood from the inner form of the anuśaya; the other determines it from the organized pattern that appears from it.

## Organon note

The strongest result is a distinction between three directional forms:

```text
division
    vicikitsā

ascension
    māna

grounding
    mūla
```

This can be modeled as more than metaphor. A determination's direction of movement constrains its logical function:

```text
oscillating determination
    cannot stabilize a ground

elevating determination
    cannot serve as substrate

descending determination
    can settle into ground
```

The alternative school adds a second test:

```text
roothood by manifested type
```

A determination can be recognized as a root because it repeatedly generates a recognizable form of agent or practitioner. This gives two possible Organon predicates:

```text
hasGroundingForm

generatesAgentType
```

They should not be assumed equivalent.

## Bhāṣya questions

1. How exact is the image that roots are `sthira` and `adhovṛtti`, and is it presented as ordinary-world analogy or formal definition?
2. Does `dvaidha-vṛtti` mean simple indecision, alternating cognition, or a deeper two-sided structure of doubt?
3. Why does the alternative school omit `mati / prajñā`, which the Kāśmīras counted in 5.20?
4. Are the three meditator-types established scripturally, scholastically, or by observed practice?
5. Does `avidyayā` make ignorance both one root among four and the common ground of the other three?
