### **Transliteration**:

### **Translation (with Sankara's Commentary)**:

In this verse, Krishna asks how the eternal, imperishable, and unchanging Self (Atman) can ever be subject to destruction or killing, especially when it is **not born (ajam)** and **not subject to decay (avyayam)**. The verse emphasizes that the Self, which is beyond the transitory, is unaffected by the destruction of the body.

- **"vedāvināśinaṁ nityaṁ"**: This refers to the **Self (Atman)** being imperishable, eternal, and unaffected by time.

- **"ya enama-jam avyayam"**: The Self is described as **ajam** (not born) and **avyayam** (imperishable), further underscoring its timeless and immutable nature.

- **"kathaṁ sa puruṣaḥ pārtha kaṁ ghātayati hanti kam"**: Krishna questions how the **wise person** (puruṣaḥ) can cause harm to this **immortal Self**. The question emphasizes the absurdity of killing or harming the **Atman**.

The Self (Atman) is beyond all transactional actions like **birth**, **death**, and **destruction**. This verse expands on this point by reiterating that **the imperishable Self** cannot be harmed by any actions because it is not involved in **karma** (action).

**विदुषः कं कर्मासम्भवहेतुविशेषं पश्यन् कर्माण्याक्षिपति भगवान् ‘कथं स पुरुषः’ इति। ननु उक्त एवात्मनः अविक्रियत्वं सर्वकर्मासम्भवकारणविशेषः। सत्यमुक्तः। न तु सः कारणविशेषः, अन्यत्वात् विदुषः अविक्रियादात्मनः। न हि अविक्रियं स्थाणुं विदितवतः कर्म न सम्भवति इति चेत्, न; विदुषः आत्मत्वात्। न देहादिसङ्घातस्य विद्वत्ता। अतः पारिशेष्यात् असंहतः आत्मा विद्वान् अविक्रियः इति तस्य विदुषः कर्मासम्भवात् आक्षेपो युक्तः ‘कथं स पुरुषः’ इति।**

### **Transliteration**:

### **Translation**:

In this portion, **Sankara** explains that the **wise person** (viduṣaḥ) is not capable of performing actions (karma) because their understanding of the Self is clear: the Self is **immovable** (avikriya). Sankara responds to the question raised by **Krishna**: **“How can the wise person harm or kill the eternal Self?”** by stating that if a person truly understands the **immutable nature of the Self**, they cannot possibly perform any actions like harming or killing it.

1. **Vidusha's perspective on karma**:
   Sankara refers to the argument that if the Self were not immutable (avikriya), then actions could indeed affect it. But since the Self is **immovable** and **eternal**, no action can actually impact it. The **wise person** (viduṣaḥ) does not engage in any actions that would harm the Self because their understanding is clear.

2. **The Self and its imperishable nature**:
   Sankara clarifies that the Self is **immutable**, unlike **the body** (deha) or its components. Even if one were to have knowledge about the **body's transience** and the transient nature of actions, the **wise person** understands that this knowledge does not pertain to the **Self**, which is **not subject to action** or decay.

3. **The role of **ignorance (avidya)** in perceiving the Self**:
   Sankara makes the point that those who lack proper understanding (avidya) might mistake the imperishable Self for something subject to action, due to the influence of **ignorance** and **illusion**. The **wise person**, however, recognizes the Self as beyond action and impervious to any destruction.

In summary, Sankara reinforces that **the Atman is imperishable**, and no matter what actions take place in the material world, they cannot touch or harm the true Self. He asserts that the **wise person**, having transcended ignorance, will not engage in actions that are harmful to the **Atman**, as their understanding of its true nature leaves no room for such mistakes.

### **Translation**:

In this section, **Sankara** explains that **actions** are prescribed for those in **ignorance (avidya)**, but for those in **knowledge (vidya)**, these actions are irrelevant because they do not involve the **true self (Atman)**.

1. **The role of ignorance (avidya)** in performing karma**:
   Sankara starts by pointing out that for the **ignorant person**, the prescribed actions (like performing rituals or duties) are valid, while for the **wise person**, these actions are meaningless after realizing the **true nature of the self**. He gives the example of **Agnihotra** (a ritual) and other prescribed duties, which the **ignorant person** performs by thinking "I am the doer" (kartā aham) and "this is my duty" (mama kartavyam). However, this view is **incorrect** for the **wise person**, who understands that there is no real doer or enjoyer of actions, and therefore, these actions do not apply to their **true self**.

2. **The transformation of understanding for the wise person**:
   The wise person who recognizes the **imperishable nature of the self** (Atman) and understands **unity with the ultimate truth (Brahman)** realizes that **there is no real action**. For them, the mind may perform activities, but their understanding is that the **Self** is **beyond any action**. This understanding leads to renunciation of all actions, both external and internal. Sankara emphasizes that for the **wise person**, there is no sense of **doership** (karma) or **enjoyership** (bhokta).

3. **Division of paths and renunciation of action**:
   Sankara here aligns with the **Bhagavad Gita** in stating that the **wise** and the **ignorant** are on different paths. For those on the path of **jnana yoga** (knowledge), actions are relinquished by the recognition that the **self** does not engage in them. For those on the path of **karma yoga** (action), actions are performed without attachment to the outcomes. Sankara also makes reference to the famous verse from the Bhagavad Gita: "All actions are to be renounced in the mind" (**sarvakarmāṇi manasā saṃnyasyāste**).

4. **The goal of renunciation (sannyasa)**:
   Sankara concludes by reiterating that **the wise person** (vidvān) is the one who truly renounces all actions because they understand the **true nature of the self**. This is the **real sannyasa** (renunciation), which does not require the abandonment of physical actions but rather involves abandoning attachment to those actions and realizing the unity of the **self** with the ultimate reality.

The true path of renunciation (sannyasa) involves the understanding that the **self** is not the doer, and thus, all actions are to be performed **without attachment**. ### **Translation**:

In this passage, **Sankara** explains the nature of **renunciation** (saṃnyāsa) and its relation to **mental** (manas), **speech** (vāc), and **physical** (kāyika) actions. The central issue is to clarify how **renunciation** of actions works in the context of different types of actions.

1. **Clarifying the Scope of Renunciation**:
   Some might argue that the phrase "**manasā**" (by the mind) suggests **renunciation** of **all actions**, including **verbal** and **physical** actions. Sankara rejects this interpretation, asserting that the phrase specifically refers to **mental actions**. Mental actions include **intention**, **thought**, and other mental operations, and are the root of **speech** and **physical action**. However, without **mental activity**, verbal and physical actions cannot occur. Therefore, the renunciation of **all actions** must include **mental activities** and not just physical actions or speech.

2. **Renunciation and the Gita’s Clarification**:
   Sankara further argues against the view that **renunciation** of action (karma) is a complete withdrawal from all forms of **action**. He refers to the **Bhagavad Gita** verse "naiva kurvann kārayan" (**Bhagavad Gita 5.13**) to clarify that true renunciation involves **renouncing attachment to the results of actions**, not renouncing actions themselves. Renunciation is about not being attached to the fruits of actions, even while continuing to act.

3. **The Relation of Renunciation to the Body and Actions**:
   Sankara also addresses the concern that renouncing all actions may imply that the person ceases to exist in the body. He refers to another verse from the Gita, "navadvāre pure dehī āste" (**Bhagavad Gita 5.13**), which suggests that the **self** (Ātman) remains detached from the body even while acting within it. The **self** is beyond all **actions**, and its nature is **unchanging** (avikriya). The renunciation of actions does not lead to the cessation of life or the **body’s functions**.

4. **Clarification of Renunciation’s True Meaning**:
   Sankara explains that the term "renunciation" (nyāsa) refers to **abandonment** or **giving up** attachment to actions, rather than to **complete withdrawal** from physical or verbal activities. **Renunciation** is not about stopping actions themselves but about eliminating the **attachment** and **egoistic identification** with them.

5. **The Role of Knowledge in Renunciation**:
   The final clarification is that true **renunciation** is based on **knowledge** (jnana). In the **Bhagavad Gita**, it is taught that only those with **knowledge of the self** (ātmajñāna) are capable of true **renunciation**. Therefore, the **right** to renounce actions is given to those who have attained **self-knowledge**, and **not to those who perform actions out of ignorance**. Sankara concludes that renunciation is intimately linked to **knowledge**, as it is through **self-realization** that one can relinquish all attachment to actions and their outcomes.
